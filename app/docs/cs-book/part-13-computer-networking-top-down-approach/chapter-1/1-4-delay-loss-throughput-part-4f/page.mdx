# Section 1.4 Delay, Loss, and Throughput in Packet-Switched Networks (Part 4F)

# Throughput in Computer Networks

So far in this section, we have learned about:

- Processing Delay
- Queueing Delay
- Transmission Delay
- Propagation Delay
- End-to-End Delay
- Packet Loss
- Congestion

Now another important question arises.

> <span style={{ color: "#38bdf8", fontWeight: 700 }}>Even if packets successfully reach their destination, how much data can actually be delivered every second?</span>

This introduces one of the most important <span style={{ color: "#fbbf24", fontWeight: 700 }}>performance metrics</span> in networking:

> **Throughput**

---

# What Is Throughput?

Definition:

> **Throughput is the rate at which data is successfully delivered from the sender to the receiver over a network.**

In simple words,

Throughput answers the question:

> <span style={{ color: "#f43f5e", fontWeight: 700 }}>How much useful data reaches the destination every second?</span>

---

# Real-Life Analogy

Imagine a water pipeline.

The pipe has a maximum capacity.

```text
Water Tank

↓

Pipe

↓

House
```

Question:

How many liters of water reach your house every second?

That is similar to:

> Throughput

---

# Another Analogy

Imagine a highway.

Suppose:

```text
500 Cars
```

successfully pass through every hour.

The highway throughput is:

```text
500 Cars/Hour
```

Similarly,

network throughput measures:

```text
Bits/Second
```

or

```text
Bytes/Second
```

---

# Units of Throughput

<span style={{ color: "#14b8a6", fontWeight: 700 }}>Throughput is commonly measured in:</span>

```text
bits/second (bps)
```

Examples:

```text
1 Kbps

1 Mbps

100 Mbps

1 Gbps

10 Gbps
```

Sometimes,

applications display:

```text
MB/s

(Megabytes per second)
```

Remember:

```text
1 Byte = 8 Bits
```

---

# Throughput vs Transmission Rate

Many beginners think these two terms mean the same thing.

They do not.

---

## Transmission Rate

Transmission Rate is:

> **The maximum capacity of a communication link.**

Example:

```text
100 Mbps Fiber Link
```

---

## Throughput

Throughput is:

> <span style={{ color: "#eab308", fontWeight: 700 }}>The actual amount of useful data successfully delivered.</span>

Example:

```text
Actual Download Speed

↓

65 Mbps
```

Even though the link supports:

```text
100 Mbps
```

---

# Why Are They Different?

Because <span style={{ color: "#d946ef", fontWeight: 700 }}>many factors reduce throughput.</span>

Examples:

- Network congestion
- Packet loss
- <span style={{ color: "#0ea5e9", fontWeight: 700 }}>Protocol overhead</span>
- <span style={{ color: "#84cc16", fontWeight: 700 }}>Shared bandwidth</span>
- Slow routers
- Bottleneck links

---

# Example

Suppose your ISP advertises:

```text
100 Mbps Internet
```

During a busy evening,

your download speed becomes:

```text
40 Mbps
```

Here:

Transmission Rate:

```text
100 Mbps
```

Throughput:

```text
40 Mbps
```

---

# Instantaneous Throughput

Definition:

> **The rate at which data is delivered at a specific instant in time.**

Imagine downloading a file.

Download speed may change continuously.

Example:

```text
10 Mbps

↓

25 Mbps

↓

80 Mbps

↓

60 Mbps
```

<span style={{ color: "#fb7185", fontWeight: 700 }}>These changing values represent:</span>

<span style={{ color: "#c084fc", fontWeight: 700 }}>Instantaneous Throughput.</span>

---

# Real-Life Analogy

Imagine driving a car.

<span style={{ color: "#60a5fa", fontWeight: 700 }}>Your speedometer shows:</span>

```text
40 km/h

↓

60 km/h

↓

80 km/h

↓

50 km/h
```

The speed changes continuously.

Similarly,

Instantaneous Throughput changes continuously.

---

# Average Throughput

Definition:

> **Average Throughput is the total amount of successfully delivered data divided by the total transfer time.**

Formula:

```text
Average Throughput

=

Total Data Delivered

/

Total Transfer Time
```

---

# Example

Suppose:

You download:

```text
500 MB
```

Time taken:

```text
50 seconds
```

Average Throughput:

```text
500 MB

/

50 s

=

10 MB/s
```

Convert to bits:

```text
10 MB/s

=

80 Mbps
```

---

# Example 2

Suppose:

File Size:

```text
2 GB
```

Download Time:

```text
100 seconds
```

Average Throughput:

```text
2 GB

/

100

=

20 MB/s
```

---

# Throughput Depends on the Entire Path

One important observation:

A packet travels through many communication links.

Example:

```text
Laptop

↓

WiFi

↓

Home Router

↓

ISP

↓

Backbone

↓

Google
```

Every communication link has its own bandwidth.

The overall throughput depends on **all of them**.

---

# Bottleneck Link

Suppose your communication path contains:

```text
100 Mbps

↓

50 Mbps

↓

10 Mbps

↓

200 Mbps
```

Question:

What is the maximum possible throughput?

Many beginners answer:

```text
200 Mbps
```

This is incorrect.

The correct answer is:

```text
10 Mbps
```

---

# Why?

Imagine a factory.

Stage 1:

```text
100 Boxes/Hour
```

Stage 2:

```text
80 Boxes/Hour
```

Stage 3:

```text
20 Boxes/Hour
```

Question:

How many finished boxes leave the factory every hour?

Answer:

```text
20
```

Because Stage 3 is the slowest.

Networking works exactly the same way.

---

# Definition

<span style={{ color: "#e879f9", fontWeight: 700 }}>The slowest communication link along a path is called the:</span>

> <span style={{ color: "#fde047", fontWeight: 700 }}>Bottleneck Link</span>

Definition:

> **A Bottleneck Link is the communication link with the lowest transmission capacity along an end-to-end path.**

---

# Visual Example

```text
Computer

↓

100 Mbps

↓

Router

↓

50 Mbps

↓

Router

↓

20 Mbps

↓

Server
```

Maximum Throughput:

```text
20 Mbps
```

---

# Why Is It Called a Bottleneck?

Imagine pouring water through bottles.

```text
Large Bottle

↓

Small Neck

↓

Large Bottle
```

The narrow neck limits the water flow.

Similarly,

the slowest communication link limits data flow.

---

# End-to-End Throughput

Suppose a packet travels through:

```text
100 Mbps

↓

80 Mbps

↓

60 Mbps

↓

20 Mbps
```

<span style={{ color: "#fca5a5", fontWeight: 700 }}>Overall Throughput:</span>

```text
20 Mbps
```

Even though other links are faster.

---

# Shared Links Reduce Throughput

Suppose one communication link supports:

```text
100 Mbps
```

Only one user:

```text
100 Mbps
```

available.

---

Now suppose:

Ten users share the same link.

Maximum average bandwidth per user becomes approximately:

```text
10 Mbps
```

assuming fair sharing.

---

# Congestion Reduces Throughput

During congestion,

packets are:

- Delayed
- Dropped
- Retransmitted

This reduces the amount of useful data reaching the destination.

Therefore,

Throughput decreases.

---

# Packet Loss and Throughput

Suppose:

100 packets are transmitted.

Only:

```text
90 packets
```

reach the destination.

The remaining:

```text
10 packets
```

must be retransmitted.

Network resources are wasted.

Effective Throughput decreases.

---

# Throughput vs Delay

Many students confuse these concepts.

---

## Delay

Measures:

```text
Time
```

Question:

How long does a packet take to arrive?

---

## Throughput

Measures:

```text
Rate
```

Question:

How much data arrives every second?

---

# Example

Imagine a truck.

Delay asks:

```text
How long until the truck arrives?
```

Throughput asks:

```text
How many boxes does the truck deliver every hour?
```

These are different questions.

---

```text
100 Mbps
```

↓

```text
Link 2

40 Mbps
```

↓

```text
Link 3

60 Mbps
```

↓

Server

Maximum Throughput:

```text
40 Mbps
```

Because Link 2 is the bottleneck.

---

# Another Example

Suppose:

A file size:

```text
800 MB
```

Download completes in:

```text
80 seconds
```

Average Throughput:

```text
800 MB

/

80

=

10 MB/s
```

Convert:

```text
10 MB/s

=

80 Mbps
```

# Relationship Between Bandwidth and Throughput

<span style={{ color: "#86efac", fontWeight: 700 }}>Higher bandwidth often allows higher throughput,</span>

<span style={{ color: "#a5b4fc", fontWeight: 700 }}>but it does not guarantee it.</span>

Because throughput also depends on:

- Congestion
- Packet loss
- <span style={{ color: "#38bdf8", fontWeight: 700 }}>Bottleneck links</span>
- Router performance
- <span style={{ color: "#fbbf24", fontWeight: 700 }}>Protocol overhead</span>

# Throughput in Different Applications

## Web Browsing

Moderate throughput is usually sufficient.

---

## Video Streaming

<span style={{ color: "#f43f5e", fontWeight: 700 }}>High throughput is required.</span>

Higher video quality requires higher throughput.

---

## Cloud Backup

<span style={{ color: "#14b8a6", fontWeight: 700 }}>Very high throughput reduces upload time.</span>

---

## Online Gaming

Games often require:

- Low delay
- Moderate throughput

<span style={{ color: "#eab308", fontWeight: 700 }}>Delay is usually more important than extremely high throughput.</span>

---

## Video Conferencing

Requires:

- <span style={{ color: "#d946ef", fontWeight: 700 }}>Stable throughput</span>
- Low delay
- Low packet loss

---

# Common Misconceptions

## Misconception 1

> Bandwidth and Throughput are the same.

Incorrect.

<span style={{ color: "#e879f9", fontWeight: 700 }}>Bandwidth is capacity.</span>

<span style={{ color: "#fde047", fontWeight: 700 }}>Throughput is actual achieved performance.</span>

---

## Misconception 2

> Fast Internet means zero delay.

Incorrect.

Delay always exists.

---

## Misconception 3

> <span style={{ color: "#fca5a5", fontWeight: 700 }}>The fastest link determines throughput.</span>

<span style={{ color: "#93c5fd", fontWeight: 700 }}>Incorrect.</span>

<span style={{ color: "#fdba74", fontWeight: 700 }}>The slowest link (Bottleneck Link) determines throughput.</span>

---

## Misconception 4

> Higher bandwidth always increases throughput.

Incorrect.

Congestion and packet loss may still reduce throughput.

# Section 1.4 Summary

Throughout this section, we studied the major factors affecting network performance.

## Delay

The time required for a packet to travel through the network.

It consists of:

- Processing Delay
- Queueing Delay
- Transmission Delay
- Propagation Delay

---

## Packet Loss

Occurs when:

- Router buffers become full.
- Packets are discarded.

Usually caused by congestion.

---

## Throughput

Measures:

The rate at which useful data is successfully delivered.

Affected by:

- Bandwidth
- Congestion
- Bottleneck Links
- Packet Loss

---

# Key Takeaways

- Throughput measures the rate of successful data delivery.
- Transmission Rate and Throughput are different concepts.
- Instantaneous Throughput changes over time.
- Average Throughput measures long-term performance.
- The Bottleneck Link determines the maximum End-to-End Throughput.
- Congestion and Packet Loss reduce Throughput.
- Good network performance requires:
  - Low Delay
  - Low Packet Loss
  - High Throughput

---

# Technical Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Throughput | Rate at which useful data is successfully delivered from source to destination. |
| Instantaneous Throughput | Data transfer rate measured at a specific moment in time. |
| Average Throughput | Total successfully delivered data divided by the total transfer time. |
| Transmission Rate | Maximum capacity of a communication link measured in bits per second. |
| Bottleneck Link | Communication link with the smallest transmission capacity along an end-to-end path. |
| End-to-End Throughput | Maximum achievable throughput across the complete communication path, limited by the bottleneck link. |
| Network Capacity | Maximum amount of data a network can carry per unit time. |
| Congestion | Condition where network traffic exceeds available resources, causing delays and packet loss. |
| Packet Retransmission | Sending a packet again after it is lost or corrupted. |
| Bandwidth | Maximum theoretical data transfer capacity of a communication link. |
