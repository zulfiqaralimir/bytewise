# Section 1.4 Delay, Loss, and Throughput in Packet-Switched Networks (Part 4D)

# Queueing Delay, Traffic Intensity, Packet Loss, and Congestion

In the previous parts, <span style={{ color: "#38bdf8", fontWeight: 700 }}>we studied all four components of Nodal Delay:</span>

- Processing Delay (`d_proc`)
- Queueing Delay (`d_queue`)
- Transmission Delay (`d_trans`)
- Propagation Delay (`d_prop`)

Among these four delays, one behaves very differently from the others.

That delay is:

> <span style={{ color: "#fbbf24", fontWeight: 700 }}>Queueing Delay</span>

<span style={{ color: "#f43f5e", fontWeight: 700 }}>Unlike the other three delays, Queueing Delay is highly unpredictable.</span>

Sometimes it is almost zero.

Sometimes it becomes several seconds.

<span style={{ color: "#14b8a6", fontWeight: 700 }}>Sometimes it becomes so large that packets are dropped</span> before they can even be transmitted.

This section explains why this happens.

---

# Revisiting Queueing Delay

Recall the definition.

> **Queueing Delay is the time a packet <span style={{ color: "#eab308", fontWeight: 700 }}>spends waiting in a router's buffer</span> before it can be transmitted onto the outgoing communication link.**

The key word here is:

> **Waiting**

A packet is not being processed.

It is not being transmitted.

It is simply waiting because another packet is currently using the communication link.

---

# Why Does Queueing Occur?

Imagine a single cashier in a supermarket.

Customers arrive continuously.

If customers arrive slowly,

everyone is served immediately.

```text
Customer

↓

Cashier

↓

Leaves
```

No queue forms.

---

Now imagine customers arrive much faster.

```text
Customer 1

Customer 2

Customer 3

Customer 4

Customer 5
```

Only one cashier exists.

A waiting line forms.

Networking works exactly the same way.

---

# Router Queue

Suppose a router receives packets like this.

```text
Incoming Packets

Packet A

Packet B

Packet C

Packet D

↓

Router
```

<span style={{ color: "#d946ef", fontWeight: 700 }}>The outgoing communication link can transmit only one packet at a time.</span>

<span style={{ color: "#0ea5e9", fontWeight: 700 }}>The remaining packets wait.</span>

```text
Outgoing Link

↓

Packet A  (Being Sent)

Packet B  (Waiting)

Packet C  (Waiting)

Packet D  (Waiting)
```

This waiting line is called a:

> **Queue**

---

# Router Buffer

Where are these waiting packets stored?

They are stored inside the router's:

> **Buffer**

The buffer is simply temporary memory.

```text
+---------------------------+

Packet A

Packet B

Packet C

Packet D

+---------------------------+

Router Buffer
```

Think of it as the waiting room inside the router.

---

# Important Observation

Every router has:

- Limited processing power
- Limited bandwidth
- Limited memory

Therefore,

buffers are **not infinite**.

They have a fixed capacity.

This fact becomes extremely important later.

---

# What Determines Queue Length?

<span style={{ color: "#84cc16", fontWeight: 700 }}>The length of the queue depends upon two things.</span>

## 1. Packet Arrival Rate

How quickly packets arrive.

---

## 2. Packet Departure Rate

How quickly packets leave.

---

Imagine a bathtub.

Water enters through the tap.

Water leaves through the drain.

If:

```text
Water In

=

Water Out
```

The water level stays constant.

---

If:

```text
Water In

>

Water Out
```

The water level rises.

Eventually,

the bathtub overflows.

Router buffers behave in exactly the same way.

---

# Traffic Intensity

Kurose introduces one of the most important concepts in networking.

It is called:

> **Traffic Intensity**

Traffic Intensity tells us:

> **How busy a communication link is.**

It compares:

- How much traffic arrives.
- How much traffic the link can handle.

---

# Mathematical Formula

Traffic Intensity is represented as:

```text
La / R
```

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|----------|----------|
| `L` | Packet Length (bits) |
| `a` | Average Packet Arrival Rate (packets/second) |
| `R` | Transmission Rate (bits/second) |

Notice something important.

The numerator:

```text
La
```

represents:

```text
Bits Arriving Per Second
```

The denominator:

```text
R
```

represents:

```text
Bits That Can Be Transmitted Per Second
```

Therefore,

Traffic Intensity compares:

```text
Incoming Traffic

/

Available Capacity
```

---

# Understanding the Formula

Suppose:

Packet Length:

```text
L = 1,000 bits
```

Packet Arrival Rate:

```text
a = 50 packets/sec
```

Transmission Rate:

```text
R = 100,000 bits/sec
```

Then:

```text
La

=

1000 × 50

=

50,000 bits/sec
```

Traffic Intensity becomes:

```text
La/R

=

50,000

/

100,000

=

0.5
```

Meaning:

The communication link is using about **50% of its capacity**.

---

# Interpretation of Traffic Intensity

The value of:

```text
La/R
```

is extremely important.

---

## Case 1

```text
La/R << 1
```

Example:

```text
0.2
```

Meaning:

The link is lightly loaded.

Packets leave faster than they arrive.

Queueing Delay is almost zero.

---

## Case 2

```text
La/R ≈ 1
```

Example:

```text
0.95
```

Meaning:

The link is operating close to its maximum capacity.

Small fluctuations in traffic can create noticeable queues.

Queueing Delay begins to increase.

---

## Case 3

```text
La/R > 1
```

Example:

```text
1.2
```

Meaning:

Traffic arrives faster than the router can transmit it.

The queue grows continuously.

If this condition continues,

the buffer eventually becomes full.

---

# Visualizing Traffic Intensity

### Low Traffic

```text
Packets

↓

Router

↓

Immediately Sent
```

Queue:

```text
Empty
```

---

### Medium Traffic

```text
Packets

↓

Router

↓

Small Queue

↓

Outgoing Link
```

Queueing Delay:

Small.

---

### Heavy Traffic

```text
Packets

Packets

Packets

Packets

Packets

↓

Router
```

Large queue.

Packets wait longer.

---

# What Happens When Traffic Intensity Is Greater Than 1?

Imagine a printer.

The printer can print:

```text
100 Pages/Minute
```

But students submit:

```text
150 Pages/Minute
```

Every minute:

```text
50 Pages
```

remain waiting.

After several minutes,

a very long queue forms.

Routers behave in exactly the same way.

---

# Can Queueing Delay Become Infinite?

In theory,

yes.

If:

```text
Arrival Rate

>

Departure Rate
```

for a long period,

the queue continues growing.

Packets wait longer and longer.

Eventually,

<span style={{ color: "#fb7185", fontWeight: 700 }}>the queue would become infinitely long.</span>

---

<span style={{ color: "#c084fc", fontWeight: 700 }}>However,</span>

<span style={{ color: "#60a5fa", fontWeight: 700 }}>real routers prevent this from happening because:</span>

<span style={{ color: "#e879f9", fontWeight: 700 }}>Buffers have limited size.</span>

---

# Buffer Overflow

Suppose a router buffer can store:

```text
100 Packets
```

Current state:

```text
Packet 1

Packet 2

...

Packet 100
```

The buffer is full.

Now another packet arrives.

Question:

Where can it be stored?

Answer:

Nowhere.

---

# Packet Loss

<span style={{ color: "#fde047", fontWeight: 700 }}>When the buffer becomes full,</span>

<span style={{ color: "#fca5a5", fontWeight: 700 }}>the router has only one option.</span>

<span style={{ color: "#93c5fd", fontWeight: 700 }}>It discards the arriving packet.</span>

<span style={{ color: "#fdba74", fontWeight: 700 }}>This is called:</span>

> <span style={{ color: "#86efac", fontWeight: 700 }}>Packet Loss</span>

---

# Visual Example

```text
Incoming Packet

↓

Router Buffer

████████████████████

FULL

↓

Packet Dropped
```

The packet never reaches its destination.

---

# Why Does Packet Loss Occur?

Packet Loss usually occurs because:

- Heavy traffic
- Congestion
- Full router buffers

Not because the packet is damaged.

---

# <span style={{ color: "#38bdf8", fontWeight: 700 }}>What Happens After Packet Loss?</span>

<span style={{ color: "#a5b4fc", fontWeight: 700 }}>Different applications react differently.</span>

---

## Web Browsing

Lost packets are retransmitted.

The page loads slightly slower.

---

## Email

Lost packets are retransmitted.

The email still arrives correctly.

---

## Video Streaming

Small losses may cause:

- <span style={{ color: "#fbbf24", fontWeight: 700 }}>Reduced video quality</span>
- Buffering

---

## Voice Calls

Packet loss may produce:

- Broken audio
- Missing words

---

## Online Gaming

Packet loss causes:

- Lag
- Teleporting players
- Delayed actions

---

# Congestion

Now we arrive at another important concept.

> **Congestion**

Congestion occurs when:

> <span style={{ color: "#f43f5e", fontWeight: 700 }}>The network receives more traffic than it can efficiently handle.</span>

Think of a highway during rush hour.

---

# Highway Analogy

Normal traffic.

```text
🚗 🚗 🚗
```

Cars move freely.

---

Rush hour.

```text
🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗🚗
```

Traffic slows.

Vehicles stop.

Congestion occurs.

The Internet experiences the same phenomenon.

---

# Causes of Congestion

Congestion usually results from:

- Too many users
- Limited bandwidth
- Large downloads
- Video streaming
- Software updates
- <span style={{ color: "#14b8a6", fontWeight: 700 }}>Cloud backups</span>

---

# Effects of Congestion

Congestion produces several problems.

## Longer Queueing Delay

Packets wait longer.

---

## Buffer Overflow

Queues become larger than available memory.

---

## Packet Loss

Packets are dropped.

---

## Lower Throughput

Users receive less useful data.

---

## Higher Latency

Applications become slower.

---

# <span style={{ color: "#d946ef", fontWeight: 700 }}>Router Buffer Management</span>

<span style={{ color: "#eab308", fontWeight: 700 }}>Modern routers use different techniques</span> to manage congestion.

Examples include:

- <span style={{ color: "#0ea5e9", fontWeight: 700 }}>First-In, First-Out (FIFO)</span>
- <span style={{ color: "#84cc16", fontWeight: 700 }}>Priority Queueing</span>
- <span style={{ color: "#fb7185", fontWeight: 700 }}>Weighted Fair Queueing (WFQ)</span>
- <span style={{ color: "#c084fc", fontWeight: 700 }}>Random Early Detection (RED)</span>

<span style={{ color: "#60a5fa", fontWeight: 700 }}>These techniques determine:</span>

- <span style={{ color: "#e879f9", fontWeight: 700 }}>Which packet should be transmitted next.</span>
- <span style={{ color: "#fde047", fontWeight: 700 }}>Which packet should be dropped when buffers become full.</span>

We will study these techniques in later chapters.

---

# Relationship Between Traffic Intensity and Queueing Delay

One of the most important graphs in networking shows:

```text
Queueing Delay
       ▲
       │
       │
       │
       │
       │
       │
       │
       └────────────────────────►

          Traffic Intensity (La/R)

          0      0.5    0.8   0.9   1
```

Interpretation:

- At low traffic intensity, Queueing Delay is almost zero.
- As traffic intensity approaches 1, Queueing Delay increases rapidly.
- Near full utilization, even small increases in traffic can produce very large delays.

This is why networks often become slow **before** they become completely saturated.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Queueing Delay occurs because packets wait in router buffers.
- Buffers have limited capacity.
- Traffic Intensity is measured by:

```text
La/R
```

- Low Traffic Intensity results in short queues.
- High Traffic Intensity produces long queues.
- When Traffic Intensity exceeds the transmission capacity for an extended period, buffers fill.
- Full buffers cause Packet Loss.
- Packet Loss and large Queueing Delays are symptoms of Congestion.
- Congestion significantly degrades network performance.

---

# Technical Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Queue | Ordered list of packets waiting for transmission. |
| Buffer | Temporary memory used by routers to store packets awaiting transmission. |
| Queueing Delay (`d_queue`) | Time a packet waits in the buffer before transmission. |
| Traffic Intensity (`La/R`) | Ratio comparing incoming traffic to the transmission capacity of a communication link. |
| Packet Arrival Rate (`a`) | Average number of packets arriving per second. |
| Packet Length (`L`) | Size of each packet measured in bits. |
| Transmission Rate (`R`) | Maximum number of bits that can be transmitted per second. |
| Buffer Overflow | Situation in which a router's buffer becomes completely full. |
| Packet Loss | Discarding of packets because no buffer space is available. |
| Congestion | Condition where network traffic exceeds available resources, leading to delays and packet loss. |
| FIFO (First-In, First-Out) | Queue management strategy that transmits packets in the order they arrive. |
| Weighted Fair Queueing (WFQ) | Queue scheduling algorithm that allocates bandwidth fairly among different traffic flows. |
| Random Early Detection (RED) | Congestion avoidance algorithm that proactively drops or marks packets before the buffer becomes full. |
