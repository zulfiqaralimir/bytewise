# Chapter 1: Computer Networks and the Internet

# Section 1.4 Delay, Loss, and Throughput in Packet-Switched Networks (Part 4C)

# Transmission Delay and Propagation Delay

In the previous part, we studied:

- Processing Delay (`d_proc`)
- Queueing Delay (`d_queue`)

Both of these delays occur **inside a router**.

Now we will study the remaining two delays:

- Transmission Delay (`d_trans`)
- Propagation Delay (`d_prop`)

These two delays occur **after the router has decided to forward the packet**.

Although they sound similar, they represent **completely different concepts**. Many beginners confuse them, so understanding the difference is extremely important.

---

# Where Are We in the Packet Journey?

Let's revisit the complete journey of a packet through a router.

```text
Packet Arrives
        │
        ▼
Processing Delay
        │
        ▼
Queueing Delay
        │
        ▼
Transmission Delay
        │
        ▼
Propagation Delay
        │
        ▼
Next Router
```

The first two delays occur **inside the router**.

The last two delays occur **while the packet leaves the router and travels across the communication link**.

---

# Understanding the Difference First

Before studying each delay individually, let's understand the big picture.

Imagine you are sending a train through a tunnel.

There are two different questions:

### Question 1

> How long does it take for the **entire train to enter the tunnel?**

This corresponds to:

> **Transmission Delay**

---

### Question 2

> After the train has entered the tunnel, how long does it take for the front of the train to reach the other side?

This corresponds to:

> **Propagation Delay**

Notice:

These are two different times.

Networking works exactly the same way.

---

# Transmission Delay (`d_trans`)

## What Is Transmission Delay?

Definition:

> **Transmission Delay is the time required to place all bits of a packet onto the communication link.**

Notice the wording carefully.

The router is **not waiting for the packet to reach the destination**.

Instead,

the router is busy **sending the bits onto the wire (or fiber).**

---

# Real-Life Analogy

Imagine filling a water pipe.

The pipe is initially empty.

You begin pouring water into it.

Question:

> **How long does it take to pour all the water into the pipe?**

This corresponds to:

> **Transmission Delay**

---

# Another Analogy

Suppose you have:

```text
100 Letters
```

You place them into a mailbox.

You insert them:

```text
Letter 1

Letter 2

Letter 3

...

Letter 100
```

The time required to insert all letters is:

Transmission Delay.

Whether the letters have reached their destination is a different matter.

---

# Why Does Transmission Delay Exist?

Communication links transmit data:

**Bit by bit**

They do **not** send an entire packet instantaneously.

Suppose a packet contains:

```text
12,000 bits
```

The router must transmit:

```text
Bit 1

Bit 2

Bit 3

...

Bit 12,000
```

Naturally,

this takes time.

---

# Mathematical Formula

Kurose defines Transmission Delay as:

```text
d_trans = L / R
```

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|----------|----------|
| `L` | Packet Length (bits) |
| `R` | Transmission Rate (bits/second) |

---

# Understanding the Formula

Suppose:

Packet Size:

```text
8,000 bits
```

Transmission Rate:

```text
2,000 bits/sec
```

Then:

```text
d_trans

=

8000

/

2000

=

4 seconds
```

The router needs:

```text
4 seconds
```

to place all bits onto the communication link.

---

# Example 1

Suppose:

Packet Length:

```text
1,500 Bytes
```

Convert to bits:

```text
1500 × 8

=

12,000 bits
```

Link Speed:

```text
100 Mbps

=

100,000,000 bits/sec
```

Transmission Delay:

```text
12000

/

100000000

=

0.00012 seconds
```

or

```text
120 microseconds
```

---

# What Affects Transmission Delay?

Only two factors affect it.

## Packet Length

Larger packets take longer.

Example:

```text
Small Packet

↓

Short Delay
```

```text
Large Packet

↓

Long Delay
```

---

## Link Speed

Higher bandwidth reduces Transmission Delay.

Example:

```text
10 Mbps

↓

Longer Delay
```

```text
1 Gbps

↓

Much Shorter Delay
```

---

# Important Observation

Transmission Delay has **nothing to do with distance**.

Whether the destination is:

```text
10 meters away
```

or

```text
10,000 kilometers away
```

Transmission Delay depends only on:

- Packet size
- Link speed

---

# Propagation Delay (`d_prop`)

Now the packet has completely entered the communication link.

Question:

> **How long does the signal need to travel through the communication medium?**

This is called:

> **Propagation Delay**

---

# Definition

Propagation Delay is:

> **The time required for a signal to travel from the sender to the receiver through the physical communication medium.**

---

# Real-Life Analogy

Imagine shouting across a valley.

Your voice travels through the air.

Even though sound is fast,

it still requires time to reach another person.

Similarly,

network signals require time to travel.

---

# Another Analogy

Imagine a train.

After the train has completely left the station,

it still must travel to the next city.

Travel time corresponds to:

Propagation Delay.

---

# Why Does Propagation Delay Exist?

Signals cannot travel infinitely fast.

Even light has a finite speed.

In communication networks,

signals travel through:

- Copper cables
- Fiber-optic cables
- Wireless radio waves

Each medium has a finite propagation speed.

---

# Mathematical Formula

Kurose defines:

```text
d_prop

=

Distance

/

Propagation Speed
```

or

```text
d_prop

=

d

/

s
```

where:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|----------|----------|
| `d` | Distance |
| `s` | Propagation Speed |

---

# Propagation Speed

Typical propagation speeds:

| Medium | Approximate Speed |
|----------|-------------------|
| Copper Cable | ~2 × 10⁸ m/s |
| Fiber Optic Cable | ~2 × 10⁸ m/s |
| Vacuum | 3 × 10⁸ m/s (Speed of Light) |

Notice:

Signals in fiber travel at approximately:

```text
Two-thirds

of

the speed of light.
```

---

# Example 2

Suppose:

Distance:

```text
1000 km
```

Convert:

```text
1000 km

=

1,000,000 meters
```

Propagation Speed:

```text
2 × 10⁸ m/s
```

Propagation Delay:

```text
1,000,000

/

200,000,000

=

0.005 seconds
```

or

```text
5 milliseconds
```

---

# What Affects Propagation Delay?

Two factors:

## Distance

Longer communication links increase Propagation Delay.

---

## Propagation Speed

Different communication media have different propagation speeds.

---

# Important Observation

Propagation Delay does **not** depend on packet size.

Whether the packet contains:

```text
100 bits
```

or

```text
10 million bits
```

The **first bit** still travels at the same propagation speed.

---

# Transmission Delay vs Propagation Delay

This is one of the most important comparisons in networking.

| Feature | Transmission Delay | Propagation Delay |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Definition | Time to push all bits onto the link | Time for signal to travel through the link |
| Depends On | Packet Length and Link Speed | Distance and Propagation Speed |
| Formula | `L / R` | `d / s` |
| Affected by Packet Size | Yes | No |
| Affected by Distance | No | Yes |
| Occurs | At the Sender | Along the Link |

---

# Visual Comparison

Imagine filling a pipe with water.

```text
Bucket

↓

Pipe

↓

Tank
```

---

### Transmission Delay

How long does it take to pour all the water into the pipe?

---

### Propagation Delay

How long does the water travel inside the pipe before reaching the tank?

---

# Numerical Example

Suppose:

Packet Length:

```text
16,000 bits
```

Transmission Rate:

```text
8 Mbps
```

Distance:

```text
200 km
```

Propagation Speed:

```text
2 × 10⁸ m/s
```

### Step 1

Transmission Delay:

```text
16000

/

8000000

=

0.002 seconds

=

2 milliseconds
```

---

### Step 2

Convert distance:

```text
200 km

=

200000 meters
```

Propagation Delay:

```text
200000

/

200000000

=

0.001 seconds

=

1 millisecond
```

Notice:

Even though the packet finishes entering the link after **2 ms**,

the signal still needs **1 ms** to travel.

---

# Which Delay Is Larger?

It depends.

### Large Packet + Fast Link

Transmission Delay may dominate.

---

### Long Fiber Link

Propagation Delay may dominate.

---

### Modern Data Centers

Propagation Delay is often very small because devices are physically close.

---

### Satellite Communication

Propagation Delay becomes significant because signals travel enormous distances.

---

# Common Misconceptions

## Misconception 1

> Transmission Delay and Propagation Delay are the same.

Incorrect.

They measure completely different events.

---

## Misconception 2

> Faster Internet eliminates Propagation Delay.

Incorrect.

Increasing bandwidth reduces Transmission Delay.

Distance remains unchanged.

---

## Misconception 3

> Larger packets increase Propagation Delay.

Incorrect.

Larger packets only increase Transmission Delay.

---

# Big Picture

Every packet now experiences:

```text
Packet Arrives

↓

Processing Delay

↓

Queueing Delay

↓

Transmission Delay

↓

Propagation Delay

↓

Next Router
```

This sequence repeats at every router.

---

# Key Takeaways

- Transmission Delay is the time required to place all bits onto the communication link.
- Transmission Delay depends on:
  - Packet Length (`L`)
  - Transmission Rate (`R`)
- Propagation Delay is the time required for the signal to travel across the communication medium.
- Propagation Delay depends on:
  - Distance (`d`)
  - Propagation Speed (`s`)
- Transmission Delay and Propagation Delay are different concepts and should not be confused.

---

# Technical Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Transmission Delay (`d_trans`) | Time required to transmit all bits of a packet onto the communication link. |
| Propagation Delay (`d_prop`) | Time required for a signal to travel from one end of the communication link to the other. |
| Packet Length (`L`) | Size of a packet measured in bits. |
| Transmission Rate (`R`) | Speed at which bits are transmitted onto the communication link (bits/sec). |
| Distance (`d`) | Physical length of the communication medium. |
| Propagation Speed (`s`) | Speed at which the signal travels through the communication medium. |
| Link Bandwidth | Maximum rate at which data can be transmitted over a communication link. |
| Fiber Optic Cable | Communication medium that transmits light signals at approximately two-thirds the speed of light. |
