## What This Course Is Actually About

Most people meet graph theory and additive combinatorics as separate subjects.

- **Graph theory** — dots and lines. How many edges can a graph have before it's forced to contain a triangle? A pentagon? Any fixed shape?
- **Additive combinatorics** — numbers and sums. If you pick a large chunk of the integers, does it have to contain three numbers in arithmetic progression (like 3, 5, 7)?

These sound like different fields. Zhao's course is built around a striking fact: **they are the same question, asked in two different languages.**

- Forbidding a triangle in a graph and forbidding a 3-term arithmetic progression in a set of integers are solved with the *same machinery* — regularity, pseudorandomness, and counting arguments that were originally developed for one field and then transplanted into the other.
- The course teaches that machinery once and then shows it firing in both directions, over and over.

> **Think of it like this:** a graph is a way of encoding "which pairs are related," and a sumset is a way of encoding "which triples add up." Once you build a lens for finding hidden structure in one, the same lens works on the other.

## The Ten-Lecture Arc

The 27 lectures are organized into ten movements. Here's the shape of the course:

| Movement | Lectures | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| Appetizer: Triangles and Equations | 1 | The opening hook — one problem from each field, side by side, to show they rhyme |
| Forbidding a Subgraph | 2–5 | Classical extremal graph theory: how many edges force a given subgraph to appear |
| Graph Regularity Method | 6–9 | Szemerédi's regularity lemma — the central tool of the whole course |
| Pseudorandom Graphs | 10–12 | What it means for a graph to "look random" and why that's useful |
| Graph Limits | 13–16 | Taking a sequence of larger and larger graphs and asking what they converge to |
| Graph Homomorphism Inequalities | 17–18 | Counting structure-preserving maps between graphs |
| Forbidding 3-Term Arithmetic Progressions | 19–21 | Roth's theorem — the additive-combinatorics twin of Lectures 2–5 |
| Structure of Set Addition | 22–25 | Freiman's theorem — when a sumset is small, the underlying set is almost a structured object (like an arithmetic progression) |
| Sum-Product Problem | 26 | Why a set can't be simultaneously additively *and* multiplicatively structured |
| Progressions in Sparse Pseudorandom Sets | 27 | The Green-Tao theorem territory — arithmetic progressions inside the primes |

Notice the pairing: Lectures 2–5 (forbidding a subgraph) and Lectures 19–21 (forbidding an arithmetic progression) are structurally the same proof, run on two different objects. That's the whole point of the course, made explicit.

## Why This Course Matters

- **It's a masterclass in transferable proof technique.** Regularity and pseudorandomness aren't just graph theory tricks — they're a general strategy for finding order inside anything that looks chaotic (a set of numbers, a network, a matrix).
- **It's where a famous result actually gets proven, not just quoted.** The Green-Tao theorem — "the primes contain arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions" — is one of the most cited results in modern number theory. This course builds up the exact tools (regularity, pseudorandomness, relative Szemerédi-type arguments) needed to understand *why* it's true, not just that it's true.
- **It rewards anyone who has hit the ceiling of standard graph theory or number theory courses.** If extremal graph theory problems ("how many edges force a triangle") or additive number theory problems ("does this set contain an AP") felt like isolated party tricks before, this course is the unifying frame that makes them feel inevitable.

---

## Who Should Take This

- Graduate students or advanced undergrads with real analysis and abstract algebra already under their belt
- Anyone doing research that touches extremal combinatorics, spectral graph theory, or analytic number theory
- Engineers curious about the theoretical backbone of pseudorandomness — the same regularity/pseudorandomness ideas show up in derandomization, expander graphs, and property testing in theoretical CS

> **Simple takeaway:** if you've ever wondered why "a graph with enough edges must contain a triangle" and "a large enough set of integers must contain an arithmetic progression" feel like they should be the same theorem — this course is where you find out they are.
