## A

### 51% Attack

An attack on a <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> blockchain where a single entity gains control of more than half the network's total <TermLink href="#hashrate">hashrate</TermLink>. With majority hashrate, the attacker can rewrite recent transaction history and double-spend coins. Economically impractical on large networks like Bitcoin — a one-hour attack would cost over $1 billion in electricity alone.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Attestation

In <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink> (Ethereum), the act of a <TermLink href="#validator">validator</TermLink> voting to confirm that a proposed block is valid. A supermajority of attestations is required before a block is added to the chain.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Avalanche Effect

A property of <TermLink href="#cryptographic-hash-function">cryptographic hash functions</TermLink>: changing even a single bit of the input causes the output hash to change completely and unpredictably. There is no gradual change — it is a cliff edge. This makes hash functions ideal for tamper detection.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

## B

### Block

The fundamental unit of a blockchain. Each block contains: (1) transaction data, (2) its own hash — a fingerprint of its contents, and (3) the hash of the previous block. The previous-hash field is what creates <TermLink href="#chaining">chaining</TermLink> and makes history tamper-evident.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Block Reward

Newly minted cryptocurrency awarded to the <TermLink href="#miner">miner</TermLink> or <TermLink href="#validator">validator</TermLink> who successfully adds a block to the chain. In Bitcoin, the block reward halves approximately every four years (the "halving"). Block rewards are the primary economic incentive for honest participation.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink> · <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Blockchain

A distributed ledger in which records (<TermLink href="#block">blocks</TermLink>) are linked by cryptographic hashes and held simultaneously by thousands of independent computers. The combination of <TermLink href="#cryptographic-hash-function">hashing</TermLink>, <TermLink href="#chaining">chaining</TermLink>, and distributed <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus</TermLink> makes the ledger tamper-evident without requiring any central authority.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

<UserTerm id="broadcast-to-the-network">Broadcast to the Network</UserTerm>

The act of sending a transaction or completed block to all connected <TermLink href="#node">nodes</TermLink> in the network simultaneously. When you initiate a transaction, your wallet broadcasts it to neighbouring nodes, who relay it further until it propagates across the network and lands in the <TermLink href="#mempool">mempool</TermLink>. When a <TermLink href="#miner">miner</TermLink> or <TermLink href="#validator">validator</TermLink> completes a valid block, they broadcast it the same way so all nodes can verify and add it to their copy of the chain.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

<UserTerm id="brute-force-computation">Brute Force Computation</UserTerm>

A problem-solving approach that systematically tries every possible option until the correct answer is found — with no mathematical shortcut available. In <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> <TermLink href="#mining">mining</TermLink>, finding a valid <TermLink href="#nonce">nonce</TermLink> is brute force: the miner tries values 1, 2, 3, ... sequentially, hashing each time, until the result meets the <TermLink href="#difficulty-adjustment">difficulty</TermLink> target. This cannot be faked or simulated — the only way to produce a valid result is to have genuinely performed the work.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Byzantine Generals Problem

A foundational problem in distributed systems, first described by Lamport, Shostak, and Pease (1982). It asks: how can distributed participants reach agreement on a shared plan when some participants may be dishonest — and there is no way to know who? Blockchain <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus mechanisms</TermLink> are practical solutions to this problem.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## C

### Chaining

The design principle of storing the previous block's hash inside every new <TermLink href="#block">block</TermLink>. Altering any block changes its hash, which breaks the "previous hash" reference in the next block — cascading a chain break through every subsequent block. This makes rewriting history computationally prohibitive even if someone has the power to recalculate one block.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Consensus Mechanism

The rules by which a decentralized network of computers agrees on the true state of the ledger — specifically, which block is the legitimate next addition to the chain. Must satisfy three requirements simultaneously: <TermLink href="#sybil-resistance">Sybil Resistance</TermLink>, <TermLink href="#fork-resolution">Fork Resolution</TermLink>, and <TermLink href="#incentive-alignment">Incentive Alignment</TermLink>. Primary examples: <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> and <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Cryptographic Hash Function

A mathematical function that converts any input — a word, a file, an entire database — into a fixed-length output string. Key properties: <TermLink href="#deterministic">deterministic</TermLink>, <TermLink href="#one-way-function">one-way</TermLink> (irreversible), and <TermLink href="#avalanche-effect">avalanche effect</TermLink>. SHA-256 is the hash function used by Bitcoin.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

## D

### Delegated Proof of Stake (DPoS)

A <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink> variant in which token holders vote to elect a fixed number of delegates (e.g., 21 on EOS) who take turns producing blocks. Faster and more efficient than standard PoS, but more centralized. Used by EOS, TRON, and BitShares.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Deterministic

A property of <TermLink href="#cryptographic-hash-function">cryptographic hash functions</TermLink>: the same input always produces the same output, every time, on every computer. "Hello" will always hash to exactly the same string.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Difficulty Adjustment

<TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work's</TermLink> self-regulating mechanism. Bitcoin automatically recalibrates the <TermLink href="#mining">mining</TermLink> target every 2,016 blocks (~2 weeks) to maintain a 10-minute average block time. If more <TermLink href="#miner">miners</TermLink> join, difficulty increases. If miners leave, difficulty decreases. The network behaves like a thermostat.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Distributed Ledger

A ledger (record of transactions) held simultaneously by thousands of independent computers rather than by a single institution. Every copy is identical. A change accepted by the network is reflected in all copies; a change made to one copy without network <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus</TermLink> is immediately detectable.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

## E

### Economic Finality

The point at which reversing a transaction would require an attacker to spend more than the value gained — making it economically irrational. In Ethereum <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>, economic finality is reached after ~12–15 minutes; reversal would require destroying at least one-third of all staked ETH, worth tens of billions of dollars.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Equivocation

A slashable offense in <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>: when a <TermLink href="#validator">validator</TermLink> simultaneously signs two different blocks at the same block height. This is a clear sign of malicious behaviour and triggers automatic <TermLink href="#slashing">slashing</TermLink> of the validator's <TermLink href="#stake">stake</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## F

### Finality

The point at which a transaction is considered irreversible. In Bitcoin <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink>, finality is reached after 6 block confirmations (~60 minutes). In Ethereum <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>, after ~12–15 minutes (<TermLink href="#economic-finality">economic finality</TermLink>). In <TermLink href="#pbft">PBFT</TermLink>-based systems like <TermLink href="#hyperledger-fabric">Hyperledger Fabric</TermLink>, finality is immediate — milliseconds.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Fork

When two <TermLink href="#miner">miners</TermLink> find valid blocks at almost the same instant, the network temporarily has two competing chains. Resolved by <TermLink href="#nakamoto-consensus">Nakamoto Consensus</TermLink> — the longer chain wins. The shorter fork is abandoned; its transactions return to the <TermLink href="#mempool">mempool</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Fork Resolution

One of the three requirements a <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus mechanism</TermLink> must satisfy. The network must have an unambiguous rule for choosing one chain when two valid competing chains exist. In <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink>: the longest chain wins (<TermLink href="#nakamoto-consensus">Nakamoto Consensus</TermLink>). In <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>: determined by <TermLink href="#validator">validator</TermLink> attestations and finality checkpoints.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## G

### Genesis Block

The very first block in a blockchain, with no parent — its "Previous Hash" field contains all zeros. Every subsequent block traces its lineage back to the genesis block through <TermLink href="#chaining">chaining</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

## H

### Hash Function

See <TermLink href="#cryptographic-hash-function">Cryptographic Hash Function</TermLink>.

---

### Hashrate

The total computational power being applied to a <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> blockchain at a given moment, measured in hashes per second. Higher hashrate means higher security — an attacker must match or exceed the entire network's hashrate to execute a <TermLink href="#51-attack">51% Attack</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Hyperledger Fabric

An enterprise-grade, permissioned blockchain platform maintained by the Linux Foundation. Uses <TermLink href="#pbft">PBFT</TermLink> consensus. Has no native cryptocurrency. Designed for known participants (e.g., supply chain consortiums, regulatory networks). The recommended platform for HalalLedger.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink> · <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## I

### Inactivity Penalty

In Ethereum's <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>, a small penalty applied to <TermLink href="#validator">validators</TermLink> that go offline or fail to attest. Designed to incentivise uptime alongside honesty. If the chain itself fails to finalise due to many validators going offline simultaneously, inactivity penalties escalate significantly.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Incentive Alignment

One of the three requirements a <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus mechanism</TermLink> must satisfy. The economic structure must make honest behaviour more profitable than dishonest behaviour. <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> achieves this through <TermLink href="#block-reward">block rewards</TermLink> vs. wasted electricity. <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink> achieves this through <TermLink href="#staking-reward">staking rewards</TermLink> vs. <TermLink href="#slashing">slashing</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## L

### Liquid Staking

A service (e.g., Lido, Rocket Pool) that allows users to participate in <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink> <TermLink href="#validator">validation</TermLink> without the 32 ETH minimum <TermLink href="#stake">stake</TermLink> requirement. The protocol pools smaller amounts, runs validators, and distributes proportional <TermLink href="#staking-reward">staking rewards</TermLink> to participants.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## M

### Mempool

*(Memory Pool)* — The waiting area for transactions that have been <TermLink href="#broadcast-to-the-network">broadcast to the network</TermLink> but not yet included in a block. <TermLink href="#miner">Miners</TermLink> and <TermLink href="#validator">validators</TermLink> pick transactions from the mempool to include in the next block, typically prioritising higher-fee transactions. Transactions from abandoned <TermLink href="#fork">forks</TermLink> return here.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Miner

A computer (and its operator) that competes to add the next block to a <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> blockchain by finding a valid <TermLink href="#nonce">nonce</TermLink>. Miners invest in hardware and electricity; they are rewarded with <TermLink href="#block-reward">block rewards</TermLink> and <TermLink href="#transaction-fee">transaction fees</TermLink> when they win.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Mining

The process of finding a valid <TermLink href="#nonce">nonce</TermLink> for a block in <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> — repeated hashing with different nonce values until the output meets the <TermLink href="#difficulty-adjustment">difficulty</TermLink> target. Deliberately slow and computationally expensive by design.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

## N

### Nakamoto Consensus

The <TermLink href="#fork">fork</TermLink>-resolution rule introduced by Bitcoin: always build on the longest chain. When two valid competing chains exist, <TermLink href="#miner">miners</TermLink> extend one of them; whichever grows longer faster becomes the accepted truth. Named after Bitcoin's pseudonymous creator Satoshi Nakamoto. Requires no central authority — the math decides.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Node

Any computer participating in a blockchain network. Nodes receive, validate, and relay transactions and blocks. Full nodes store the complete blockchain history; light nodes store only block headers and rely on full nodes for transaction verification. Transactions enter the network when they are <TermLink href="#broadcast-to-the-network">broadcast</TermLink> to a node.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Nonce

*(Number Used Once)* — A number included in a block's data that <TermLink href="#miner">miners</TermLink> vary — trying 1, 2, 3, ... — until the resulting hash meets the <TermLink href="#difficulty-adjustment">difficulty</TermLink> target (starts with enough zeros). Finding the right nonce is the core computational task of <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> <TermLink href="#mining">mining</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink> · <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## O

### One-Way Function

A property of <TermLink href="#cryptographic-hash-function">cryptographic hash functions</TermLink>: given an output hash, it is computationally infeasible to reverse-engineer the input. You can verify that "Hello" produces a given hash, but you cannot reconstruct "Hello" from the hash alone.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

## P

### PBFT

*(Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance)* — A <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus mechanism</TermLink> in which <TermLink href="#validator">validators</TermLink> communicate directly in multiple rounds of voting. Provides immediate <TermLink href="#finality">finality</TermLink> (milliseconds). Requires known, permissioned participants — not suitable for anonymous public blockchains. Used by <TermLink href="#hyperledger-fabric">Hyperledger Fabric</TermLink> for enterprise applications like HalalLedger.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Proof of Authority (PoA)

A <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus mechanism</TermLink> in which a small set of pre-approved, known <TermLink href="#validator">validators</TermLink> produce blocks by reputation rather than computational work or staked capital. Extremely fast and efficient, but fully centralized. Used in private enterprise blockchains and testnets. Similar in spirit to <TermLink href="#pbft">PBFT</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Proof of History (PoH)

Solana's <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus</TermLink> innovation. A cryptographic clock embedded in the protocol creates a verifiable, timestamped record of events, allowing <TermLink href="#validator">validators</TermLink> to agree on ordering without extensive back-and-forth communication. Enables Solana's high throughput (~65,000 TPS theoretically).

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Proof of Stake (PoS)

A <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus mechanism</TermLink> in which <TermLink href="#validator">validators</TermLink> lock up (<TermLink href="#stake">stake</TermLink>) cryptocurrency as collateral to earn the right to propose and attest to blocks. Dishonesty is punished by <TermLink href="#slashing">slashing</TermLink> the stake. Requires ~99.95% less energy than <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink>. Used by Ethereum (after <TermLink href="#the-merge">The Merge</TermLink>), Cardano, Polkadot, and others.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Proof of Work (PoW)

A <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus mechanism</TermLink> in which <TermLink href="#miner">miners</TermLink> earn the right to add a block by solving a computational puzzle — finding a <TermLink href="#nonce">nonce</TermLink> that makes the block's hash meet a <TermLink href="#difficulty-adjustment">difficulty</TermLink> target. Security is grounded in physical energy expenditure: you either did the work or you did not. Used by Bitcoin, Litecoin, and Monero.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink> · <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## S

### SHA-256

*(Secure Hash Algorithm 256-bit)* — The <TermLink href="#cryptographic-hash-function">cryptographic hash function</TermLink> used by Bitcoin. Produces a 256-bit (64 hexadecimal character) output from any input. Designed by the US National Security Agency; considered cryptographically secure.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Slashing

The automatic, protocol-enforced destruction of a portion of a <TermLink href="#validator">validator's</TermLink> <TermLink href="#stake">staked</TermLink> cryptocurrency as punishment for provably malicious behaviour (e.g., <TermLink href="#equivocation">equivocation</TermLink>). No court or regulator is involved — the punishment is baked into the protocol's math. The primary security mechanism of <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Smart Contract

A program stored on a blockchain that executes automatically when predefined conditions are met. Nobody can stop or alter it after deployment. Invented by Ethereum. Enables DeFi, NFTs, DAOs, and on-chain business logic like automated halal certification release.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain">Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?</ChapterLink>*

---

### Stake

Cryptocurrency locked as collateral by a <TermLink href="#validator">validator</TermLink> in a <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink> system. Acts as a security deposit — honest behaviour earns <TermLink href="#staking-reward">staking rewards</TermLink>, dishonest behaviour destroys it via <TermLink href="#slashing">slashing</TermLink>. On Ethereum, the minimum solo validator stake is 32 ETH.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Staking Reward

Earnings received by <TermLink href="#validator">validators</TermLink> in <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink> for honestly proposing and attesting to blocks. Equivalent in function to the <TermLink href="#block-reward">block reward</TermLink> in <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> — the economic incentive for honest participation.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Sybil Resistance

One of the three requirements a <TermLink href="#consensus-mechanism">consensus mechanism</TermLink> must satisfy. The system must make it prohibitively costly to create many fake identities and flood the network with fraudulent blocks. <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> achieves this via computational cost; <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink> achieves this via capital cost.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## T

### The Merge

Ethereum's transition from <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> to <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>, completed on September 15, 2022. One of the most complex live software upgrades in blockchain history — swapping the consensus engine of a running system without stopping it. Reduced Ethereum's energy consumption by ~99.95% overnight.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

### Transaction Fee

A fee paid by users to have their transaction prioritised and included in a block. Collected by <TermLink href="#miner">miners</TermLink> in <TermLink href="#proof-of-work-pow">Proof of Work</TermLink> and <TermLink href="#validator">validators</TermLink> in <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>, in addition to the <TermLink href="#block-reward">block reward</TermLink>. On congested networks, fees can be substantial.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*

---

## V

### Validator

In <TermLink href="#proof-of-stake-pos">Proof of Stake</TermLink>, a computer (and its operator) that <TermLink href="#stake">stakes</TermLink> cryptocurrency to participate in block proposal and <TermLink href="#attestation">attestation</TermLink>. Validators replace <TermLink href="#miner">miners</TermLink> as the block-producers in PoS systems. Dishonest validators are punished via <TermLink href="#slashing">slashing</TermLink>; offline validators receive <TermLink href="#inactivity-penalty">inactivity penalties</TermLink>. On Ethereum, each validator requires exactly 32 ETH staked.

*→ <ChapterLink href="/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms">Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms</ChapterLink>*
