# Blockchain Glossary

*Compiled from the Black Iron Quantum AI Blockchain Developer Learning Track. Updated with each new chapter.*

## A

### 51% Attack

An attack on a [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) blockchain where a single entity gains control of more than half the network's total [hashrate](#hashrate). With majority hashrate, the attacker can rewrite recent transaction history and double-spend coins. Economically impractical on large networks like Bitcoin — a one-hour attack would cost over $1 billion in electricity alone.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Attestation

In [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos) (Ethereum), the act of a [validator](#validator) voting to confirm that a proposed block is valid. A supermajority of attestations is required before a block is added to the chain.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Avalanche Effect

A property of [cryptographic hash functions](#cryptographic-hash-function): changing even a single bit of the input causes the output hash to change completely and unpredictably. There is no gradual change — it is a cliff edge. This makes hash functions ideal for tamper detection.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

## B

### Block

The fundamental unit of a blockchain. Each block contains: (1) transaction data, (2) its own hash — a fingerprint of its contents, and (3) the hash of the previous block. The previous-hash field is what creates [chaining](#chaining) and makes history tamper-evident.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Block Reward

Newly minted cryptocurrency awarded to the [miner](#miner) or [validator](#validator) who successfully adds a block to the chain. In Bitcoin, the block reward halves approximately every four years (the "halving"). Block rewards are the primary economic incentive for honest participation.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain) · [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Blockchain

A distributed ledger in which records ([blocks](#block)) are linked by cryptographic hashes and held simultaneously by thousands of independent computers. The combination of [hashing](#cryptographic-hash-function), [chaining](#chaining), and distributed [consensus](#consensus-mechanism) makes the ledger tamper-evident without requiring any central authority.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Byzantine Generals Problem

A foundational problem in distributed systems, first described by Lamport, Shostak, and Pease (1982). It asks: how can distributed participants reach agreement on a shared plan when some participants may be dishonest — and there is no way to know who? Blockchain [consensus mechanisms](#consensus-mechanism) are practical solutions to this problem.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## C

### Chaining

The design principle of storing the previous block's hash inside every new [block](#block). Altering any block changes its hash, which breaks the "previous hash" reference in the next block — cascading a chain break through every subsequent block. This makes rewriting history computationally prohibitive even if someone has the power to recalculate one block.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Consensus Mechanism

The rules by which a decentralized network of computers agrees on the true state of the ledger — specifically, which block is the legitimate next addition to the chain. Must satisfy three requirements simultaneously: [Sybil Resistance](#sybil-resistance), [Fork Resolution](#fork-resolution), and [Incentive Alignment](#incentive-alignment). Primary examples: [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) and [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos).

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Cryptographic Hash Function

A mathematical function that converts any input — a word, a file, an entire database — into a fixed-length output string. Key properties: [deterministic](#deterministic), [one-way](#one-way-function) (irreversible), and [avalanche effect](#avalanche-effect). SHA-256 is the hash function used by Bitcoin.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

## D

### Delegated Proof of Stake (DPoS)

A [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos) variant in which token holders vote to elect a fixed number of delegates (e.g., 21 on EOS) who take turns producing blocks. Faster and more efficient than standard PoS, but more centralized. Used by EOS, TRON, and BitShares.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Deterministic

A property of [cryptographic hash functions](#cryptographic-hash-function): the same input always produces the same output, every time, on every computer. "Hello" will always hash to exactly the same string.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Difficulty Adjustment

[Proof of Work's](#proof-of-work-pow) self-regulating mechanism. Bitcoin automatically recalibrates the [mining](#mining) target every 2,016 blocks (~2 weeks) to maintain a 10-minute average block time. If more [miners](#miner) join, difficulty increases. If miners leave, difficulty decreases. The network behaves like a thermostat.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Distributed Ledger

A ledger (record of transactions) held simultaneously by thousands of independent computers rather than by a single institution. Every copy is identical. A change accepted by the network is reflected in all copies; a change made to one copy without network [consensus](#consensus-mechanism) is immediately detectable.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

## E

### Economic Finality

The point at which reversing a transaction would require an attacker to spend more than the value gained — making it economically irrational. In Ethereum [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos), economic finality is reached after ~12–15 minutes; reversal would require destroying at least one-third of all staked ETH, worth tens of billions of dollars.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Equivocation

A slashable offense in [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos): when a [validator](#validator) simultaneously signs two different blocks at the same block height. This is a clear sign of malicious behavior and triggers automatic [slashing](#slashing) of the validator's [stake](#stake).

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## F

### Finality

The point at which a transaction is considered irreversible. In Bitcoin [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow), finality is reached after 6 block confirmations (~60 minutes). In Ethereum [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos), after ~12–15 minutes ([economic finality](#economic-finality)). In [PBFT](#pbft)-based systems like [Hyperledger Fabric](#hyperledger-fabric), finality is immediate — milliseconds.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Fork

When two [miners](#miner) find valid blocks at almost the same instant, the network temporarily has two competing chains. Resolved by [Nakamoto Consensus](#nakamoto-consensus) — the longer chain wins. The shorter fork is abandoned; its transactions return to the [mempool](#mempool).

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Fork Resolution

One of the three requirements a [consensus mechanism](#consensus-mechanism) must satisfy. The network must have an unambiguous rule for choosing one chain when two valid competing chains exist. In [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow): the longest chain wins ([Nakamoto Consensus](#nakamoto-consensus)). In [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos): determined by [validator](#validator) attestations and finality checkpoints.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## G

### Genesis Block

The very first block in a blockchain, with no parent — its "Previous Hash" field contains all zeros. Every subsequent block traces its lineage back to the genesis block through [chaining](#chaining).

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

## H

### Hash Function

See [Cryptographic Hash Function](#cryptographic-hash-function).

---

### Hashrate

The total computational power being applied to a [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) blockchain at a given moment, measured in hashes per second. Higher hashrate means higher security — an attacker must match or exceed the entire network's hashrate to execute a [51% Attack](#51-attack).

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Hyperledger Fabric

An enterprise-grade, permissioned blockchain platform maintained by the Linux Foundation. Uses [PBFT](#pbft) consensus. Has no native cryptocurrency. Designed for known participants (e.g., supply chain consortiums, regulatory networks). The recommended platform for HalalLedger.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain) · [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## I

### Inactivity Penalty

In Ethereum's [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos), a small penalty applied to [validators](#validator) that go offline or fail to attest. Designed to incentivize uptime alongside honesty. If the chain itself fails to finalize due to many validators going offline simultaneously, inactivity penalties escalate significantly.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Incentive Alignment

One of the three requirements a [consensus mechanism](#consensus-mechanism) must satisfy. The economic structure must make honest behavior more profitable than dishonest behavior. [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) achieves this through [block rewards](#block-reward) vs. wasted electricity. [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos) achieves this through [staking rewards](#staking-reward) vs. [slashing](#slashing).

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## L

### Liquid Staking

A service (e.g., Lido, Rocket Pool) that allows users to participate in [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos) [validation](#validator) without the 32 ETH minimum [stake](#stake) requirement. The protocol pools smaller amounts, runs validators, and distributes proportional [staking rewards](#staking-reward) to participants.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## M

### Mempool

*(Memory Pool)* — The waiting area for transactions that have been broadcast to the network but not yet included in a block. [Miners](#miner) and [validators](#validator) pick transactions from the mempool to include in the next block, typically prioritizing higher-fee transactions. Transactions from abandoned [forks](#fork) return here.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Miner

A computer (and its operator) that competes to add the next block to a [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) blockchain by finding a valid [nonce](#nonce). Miners invest in hardware and electricity; they are rewarded with [block rewards](#block-reward) and [transaction fees](#transaction-fee) when they win.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Mining

The process of finding a valid [nonce](#nonce) for a block in [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) — repeated hashing with different nonce values until the output meets the [difficulty](#difficulty-adjustment) target. Deliberately slow and computationally expensive by design. The [difficulty adjustment](#difficulty-adjustment) ensures this always takes ~10 minutes on Bitcoin regardless of how much computing power is applied.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

## N

### Nakamoto Consensus

The [fork](#fork)-resolution rule introduced by Bitcoin: always build on the longest chain. When two valid competing chains exist, [miners](#miner) extend one of them; whichever grows longer faster becomes the accepted truth. Named after Bitcoin's pseudonymous creator Satoshi Nakamoto. Requires no central authority — the math decides.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Node

Any computer participating in a blockchain network. Nodes receive, validate, and relay transactions and blocks. Full nodes store the complete blockchain history; light nodes store only block headers and rely on full nodes for transaction verification.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Nonce

*(Number Used Once)* — A number included in a block's data that [miners](#miner) vary — trying 1, 2, 3, ... — until the resulting hash meets the [difficulty](#difficulty-adjustment) target (starts with enough zeros). Finding the right nonce is the core computational task of [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) [mining](#mining).

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain) · [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## O

### One-Way Function

A property of [cryptographic hash functions](#cryptographic-hash-function): given an output hash, it is computationally infeasible to reverse-engineer the input. You can verify that "Hello" produces a given hash, but you cannot reconstruct "Hello" from the hash alone.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

## P

### PBFT

*(Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance)* — A [consensus mechanism](#consensus-mechanism) in which [validators](#validator) communicate directly in multiple rounds of voting. Provides immediate [finality](#finality) (milliseconds). Requires known, permissioned participants — not suitable for anonymous public blockchains. Used by [Hyperledger Fabric](#hyperledger-fabric) for enterprise applications like HalalLedger.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Proof of Authority (PoA)

A [consensus mechanism](#consensus-mechanism) in which a small set of pre-approved, known [validators](#validator) produce blocks by reputation rather than computational work or staked capital. Extremely fast and efficient, but fully centralized. Used in private enterprise blockchains and testnets. Similar in spirit to [PBFT](#pbft).

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Proof of History (PoH)

Solana's [consensus](#consensus-mechanism) innovation. A cryptographic clock embedded in the protocol creates a verifiable, timestamped record of events, allowing [validators](#validator) to agree on ordering without extensive back-and-forth communication. Enables Solana's high throughput (~65,000 TPS theoretically).

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Proof of Stake (PoS)

A [consensus mechanism](#consensus-mechanism) in which [validators](#validator) lock up ([stake](#stake)) cryptocurrency as collateral to earn the right to propose and attest to blocks. Dishonesty is punished by [slashing](#slashing) the stake. Requires ~99.95% less energy than [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow). Used by Ethereum (after [The Merge](#the-merge)), Cardano, Polkadot, and others.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Proof of Work (PoW)

A [consensus mechanism](#consensus-mechanism) in which [miners](#miner) earn the right to add a block by solving a computational puzzle — finding a [nonce](#nonce) that makes the block's hash meet a [difficulty](#difficulty-adjustment) target. Security is grounded in physical energy expenditure: you either did the work or you did not. Used by Bitcoin, Litecoin, and Monero.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain) · [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## S

### SHA-256

*(Secure Hash Algorithm 256-bit)* — The [cryptographic hash function](#cryptographic-hash-function) used by Bitcoin. Produces a 256-bit (64 hexadecimal character) output from any input. Designed by the US National Security Agency; considered cryptographically secure.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Slashing

The automatic, protocol-enforced destruction of a portion of a [validator's](#validator) [staked](#stake) cryptocurrency as punishment for provably malicious behavior (e.g., [equivocation](#equivocation)). No court or regulator is involved — the punishment is baked into the protocol's math. The primary security mechanism of [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos).

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Smart Contract

A program stored on a blockchain that executes automatically when predefined conditions are met. Nobody can stop or alter it after deployment. Invented by Ethereum. Enables DeFi, NFTs, DAOs, and on-chain business logic like automated halal certification release.

*→ [Chapter 1: What Is Blockchain?](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/what-is-blockchain)*

---

### Stake

Cryptocurrency locked as collateral by a [validator](#validator) in a [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos) system. Acts as a security deposit — honest behavior earns [staking rewards](#staking-reward), dishonest behavior destroys it via [slashing](#slashing). On Ethereum, the minimum solo validator stake is 32 ETH.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Staking Reward

Earnings received by [validators](#validator) in [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos) for honestly proposing and attesting to blocks. Equivalent in function to the [block reward](#block-reward) in [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) — the economic incentive for honest participation.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Sybil Resistance

One of the three requirements a [consensus mechanism](#consensus-mechanism) must satisfy. The system must make it prohibitively costly to create many fake identities and flood the network with fraudulent blocks. [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) achieves this via computational cost; [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos) achieves this via capital cost.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## T

### The Merge

Ethereum's transition from [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) to [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos), completed on September 15, 2022. One of the most complex live software upgrades in blockchain history — swapping the consensus engine of a running system without stopping it. Reduced Ethereum's energy consumption by ~99.95% overnight.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

### Transaction Fee

A fee paid by users to have their transaction prioritized and included in a block. Collected by [miners](#miner) in [Proof of Work](#proof-of-work-pow) and [validators](#validator) in [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos), in addition to the [block reward](#block-reward). On congested networks, fees can be substantial.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*

---

## V

### Validator

In [Proof of Stake](#proof-of-stake-pos), a computer (and its operator) that [stakes](#stake) cryptocurrency to participate in block proposal and [attestation](#attestation). Validators replace [miners](#miner) as the block-producers in PoS systems. Dishonest validators are punished via [slashing](#slashing); offline validators receive [inactivity penalties](#inactivity-penalty). On Ethereum, each validator requires exactly 32 ETH staked.

*→ [Chapter 2: Consensus Mechanisms](/docs/cs-book/part-8-blockchain/consensus-mechanisms)*
