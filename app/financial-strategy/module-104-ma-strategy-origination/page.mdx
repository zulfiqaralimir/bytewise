# Module 104 — M&A Strategy & Deal Origination

M&A transactions don't happen spontaneously. They originate from strategic thinking: a company identifies that it needs capabilities it doesn't have, a market it can't enter organically, or scale it can't build fast enough. The investment banker's job in origination is to be the catalyst — to identify these strategic needs before the client does, frame the opportunity compellingly, and secure a mandate before a competitor does. This module covers the strategic rationale behind M&A, how bankers identify targets and buyers, how to construct an M&A pitch, and how mandates are structured.

## Learning Objectives

- Articulate the strategic rationale framework for M&A transactions
- Distinguish between synergistic and non-synergistic M&A motivations
- Conduct a systematic target identification for a buy-side client
- Identify and prioritize potential buyers for a sell-side client
- Build a compelling M&A pitch book
- Structure an M&A advisory mandate agreement

---

## 1. Why Companies Do M&A

### The Strategic Rationale Framework

Every M&A transaction has a stated and an unstated rationale. The IB banker must understand both to structure a compelling pitch.

**Revenue synergies (top-line):**
- **Market access:** The acquirer buys a target's distribution network, customer relationships, or geographic presence it would take years to build organically
- **Product/capability adjacency:** Add a complementary product line or technology to cross-sell to existing customers
- **Market share consolidation:** In mature industries, buying a competitor removes pricing pressure and increases market power

**Cost synergies (bottom-line):**
- **Procurement scale:** A larger combined entity negotiates better terms from suppliers
- **Overhead elimination:** Combined headquarters, back-office, IT, and compliance functions
- **Manufacturing rationalization:** Close the least efficient plants; utilize remaining capacity more fully

**Strategic/defensive rationale:**
- **Blocking a competitor:** Acquire before a rival does; remove an emerging threat
- **Talent/technology acquisition:** "Acqui-hire" in tech; buy a team or patent portfolio
- **Vertical integration:** Acquire a supplier (backward integration) or a customer/distributor (forward integration)

**Financial rationale (PE):**
- Buy undervalued assets, improve operations, sell at a higher multiple (LBO)
- Portfolio diversification (less common, debated as value-creating)

### Why M&A Fails

Most academic studies show that 50–70% of acquisitions destroy value for the acquirer's shareholders. Common reasons:
- **Overpayment:** Winner's curse in competitive auctions; optimistic synergy estimates
- **Integration failure:** Cultural clashes, system incompatibility, talent departure post-deal
- **Strategic misfit:** The "strategic rationale" was weak from the start
- **Management distraction:** Post-merger integration consumes executive bandwidth
- **Leverage:** LBOs fail when business underperforms debt service requirements

## 2. Target Identification (Buy-Side)

### The Systematic Screening Process

When a company (or PE firm) asks a banker to "identify acquisition targets," the process is:

**Step 1 — Define strategic criteria:**
Work with the client to define:
- Industry/sector focus
- Geographic scope
- Size range (revenue, EBITDA, deal value)
- Business model requirements (asset-heavy/light, B2B/B2C)
- Financial profile (growth stage, profitability threshold)
- Ownership (family-owned, listed, PE-backed, subsidiary of larger group)

**Step 2 — Universe screen:**
Using deal databases (Capital IQ, Mergermarket, Bloomberg), identify all companies meeting the criteria. This typically produces a list of 30–100 companies.

**Step 3 — Qualitative filter:**
Reduce the universe to 10–20 names by applying qualitative filters:
- Strategic fit scoring: how well does each target complement the acquirer?
- Owner motivation: is the owner likely to sell? (Family succession issues, PE fund lifecycle, financial distress)
- Regulatory clearance: would competition authorities block the deal?
- Execution complexity: cross-border, minority stake, full acquisition?

**Step 4 — Priority ranking:**
Tier the list:
- Tier 1: High priority — strong strategic fit, likely seller, actionable in 6–12 months
- Tier 2: Medium priority — good fit but uncertain seller motivation or timing
- Tier 3: Watch list — interesting but not immediately actionable

### Pakistan Buy-Side Target Identification

**Data challenge:** Pakistan lacks comprehensive private company databases. Research tools include:
- PSX company announcements and annual reports (for listed targets)
- SECP company registry (for basic corporate information)
- Industry associations (APTMA for textiles, PCA for cement, etc.)
- Network intelligence (the banker's relationships are the primary database for private company information)
- Pakistan Business Council and regional chambers
- Local press (Profit Magazine, Dawn Business, The News on Sunday)

## 3. Buyer Identification (Sell-Side)

### Types of Buyers

When a company mandates a bank to find a buyer, the banker maps the buyer universe:

**Strategic buyers (corporates):**
- Direct competitors: highest synergy potential, but antitrust risk; may be excluded
- Adjacent industry players: complementary businesses; cross-sell opportunity
- International companies entering the market: the acquirer buys market access
- Vertical integration partners: suppliers, distributors

**Financial buyers (PE firms):**
- Require a business that fits LBO characteristics (stable cash flows, leverage capacity)
- PE returns are IRR-driven; constrained by return hurdle rates
- PE buyers are always present in an auction as a floor / price benchmark
- Pakistan PE: Abraaj (defunct), NMT Capital, Crosby Capital, Acumen, SEAF; Gulf PE: Gulf Capital, Investcorp, Waha Capital, regional family PE arms

**Family offices and HNWIs:**
- Significant in Pakistan (major business families: Mansha, Saigol, Dawood, Adamjee, Hashwani)
- Not return-constrained in the same way as PE; may have strategic rather than financial motivation
- Gulf family offices (Al Futtaim, Olayan, Alshaya) are active buyers

### Building the Buyer List

The banker presents the client with a prioritized buyer list showing:
- Buyer name and profile
- Strategic rationale for acquisition (one paragraph per buyer)
- Estimated deal capacity / financial ability to close
- Any known sensitivities (regulatory, cultural, competitive concerns)
- Recommended contact approach (chairman-to-chairman, banker-to-CFO, formal process)

**Important:** The seller typically has veto rights over which buyers are contacted. Some sellers exclude direct competitors, require confidentiality about the process, or restrict contact to specific geographies.

## 4. The M&A Pitch

### Purpose of the Pitch

An M&A pitch (or "tombstone pitch" before any transaction is identified, or a "live deal pitch" once a target/buyer is identified) serves to:
- Demonstrate the banker's knowledge of the client's industry and strategic position
- Present a specific idea (acquisition target or sale process)
- Persuade the client to grant a mandate

### Pitch Book Structure — Sell-Side Mandate

**Section 1: Executive Summary (2–3 pages)**
- The opportunity: why now is the right time to run a sale process
- Estimated valuation range
- Process recommendation

**Section 2: Company Overview**
- Business description, competitive position, financial summary
- Key investment highlights (what makes this company attractive to buyers)

**Section 3: Market Context**
- Industry trends, M&A activity in the sector, recent comparable transactions
- Why strategic buyers or PE are active in this space

**Section 4: Preliminary Valuation**
- Football field: trading comps, precedent transactions, DCF range

**Section 5: Buyer Universe**
- Prioritized list of potential acquirers with rationale
- Map of buyers by type (strategic vs. PE) and geography

**Section 6: Process Recommendation**
- Broad auction vs. targeted process vs. bilateral negotiation
- Timeline (typically 20–28 weeks)
- Bank credentials and team

### Pitch Book Structure — Buy-Side Mandate

**Section 1: Strategic Context**
- Client's strategic gaps and acquisition thesis
- Why M&A vs. organic growth

**Section 2: Target Universe**
- Screened target list with financial profiles
- Prioritized tiers

**Section 3: Focus Target Analysis**
- Detailed analysis of 1–3 priority targets
- Preliminary valuation
- Synergy estimate (high level)

**Section 4: Process Considerations**
- How to approach each target; negotiation dynamics
- Regulatory considerations

## 5. The M&A Mandate

### Mandate Letter (Engagement Letter) Components

When a client formally engages a bank, the mandate letter specifies:

**Scope of engagement:**
- Buy-side or sell-side
- Specific transaction (or ongoing advisory)
- Exclusivity (is the bank the sole advisor, or one of several?)

**Fees:**
- **Retainer:** Monthly fee during the engagement (PKR 500K–2M/month in Pakistan; $50K–200K/month in Gulf)
- **Success fee:** The main fee, paid only if a transaction closes
- **Success fee formula:** Typically a percentage of deal value, often using a Lehman-type formula (Module 94)
- **Break fee:** Sometimes the client pays a reduced fee if the process is launched but no deal closes

**Pakistan vs. Gulf fee norms:**
| Market | Success Fee (mid-market) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pakistan | 1.0–2.5% of deal value | Lower than global; limited competition |
| Gulf (UAE/Saudi) | 0.5–1.5% of deal value | Competitive market; larger deal sizes |
| Global bulge bracket | 0.25–0.75% of deal value | Very large transactions |

**Confidentiality:** The bank agrees to keep client information confidential; conflict-of-interest provisions.

**Termination:** Notice period; fees owed if client terminates the engagement.

## Self-Assessment

1. You are a Pakistani investment banker. Engro Corporation approaches you with a mandate: "We want to acquire a mid-size FMCG company in Pakistan to complement our food business."

   (a) Define the screening criteria for this target search. What industry, size, financial profile, and ownership type criteria would you apply?
   (b) Using the strategic rationale framework, identify the top three strategic reasons Engro would want to make this acquisition. For each reason, name a specific capability or asset the target would need to provide.
   (c) Engro asks: "Should we look at companies in Bangladesh and Sri Lanka too?" Evaluate this geographic expansion of the scope — what are the key factors that would determine whether to include South Asian targets alongside Pakistani ones?
   (d) You identify a strong target: a privately-held Pakistani dairy and beverages company owned by a founder in his 70s. What signals would suggest the owner is motivated to sell, and how would you approach him to open deal discussions?

2. You are advising a Pakistan-based logistics company on its sale to a strategic buyer:

   (a) Build a buyer universe. Name three specific categories of strategic buyers and for each category, explain the strategic rationale. Then describe what PE buyer characteristics would make them interested in this business.
   (b) The client's CEO says: "I don't want direct Pakistani competitors to know we're running a sale process." How would you design the process to honor this request while still running a competitive auction?
   (c) The pitch book includes a preliminary valuation of PKR 8–12B (EV/EBITDA 7–9x). The client says it's worth PKR 15B. How do you handle this disagreement in the pitch meeting without losing the mandate?
   (d) Draft a one-paragraph "strategic rationale" section for the pitch book's executive summary that explains why a Gulf logistics company (the primary strategic buyer target) would want to acquire this Pakistani business.
