# Module 101 — LBO Valuation & Sponsor Returns

An LBO (Leveraged Buyout) is a PE firm's acquisition of a company using significant debt, with the target company's own cash flows servicing that debt. The logic: by using leverage, the equity investor puts in less capital and earns a higher return on what they invested — provided the business generates enough cash to repay debt and the exit multiple holds. This module covers how to build an LBO model, how PE firms calculate returns, and what distinguishes a good LBO candidate from a poor one.

## Learning Objectives

- Explain the mechanics of an LBO and why leverage amplifies returns
- Identify characteristics of a good LBO candidate
- Build a simplified LBO model (sources & uses, debt waterfall, sponsor returns)
- Calculate IRR and MOIC from entry and exit assumptions
- Understand the PE value creation framework
- Apply LBO analysis to Pakistan and Gulf private equity deal context

---

## 1. The LBO Concept

### Why Leverage Amplifies Returns

**Without leverage:**
- Buy a company for $100M (100% equity)
- Sell 5 years later for $150M
- Equity gain: $50M on $100M invested = 50% total return = ~8.4% IRR

**With 60% leverage:**
- Buy a company for $100M (40% equity = $40M, 60% debt = $60M)
- Sell 5 years later for $150M
- Debt repaid: $60M (assume no amortization for simplicity)
- Equity proceeds: $150M − $60M = $90M
- Equity gain: $50M on $40M invested = 125% total return = ~18.0% IRR

The leverage amplified the return from 8.4% to 18.0% on the same business performance. This is the core logic of LBOs.

**The risk:** If the business deteriorates and the $150M exit doesn't materialize — or if cash flows can't service the debt — the equity is wiped out first.

### Good LBO Characteristics

A company is a strong LBO candidate if it has:

| Characteristic | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Stable, predictable cash flows | Debt must be serviced; volatile cash flows = default risk |
| Low existing debt | More room to add leverage |
| Strong market position | Pricing power, defensible revenue |
| Asset-light or asset-heavy with collateral | Either way to support debt |
| Limited capex requirements | Free cash flow available for debt repayment |
| Clear exit path | IPO, strategic sale, or secondary PE sale in 3–7 years |
| Experienced management (or replaceable) | PE firms often install operating partners |
| Cost reduction opportunity | Operational improvement drives value |

**Companies that are poor LBO candidates:**
- High capex cyclical businesses (steel mills, airlines)
- Businesses with volatile revenue (early-stage, contract-dependent)
- Companies with existing high leverage
- Heavily regulated businesses with limited pricing flexibility
- Companies dependent on a single founder who won't stay post-acquisition

## 2. LBO Model Structure

### Sources and Uses Table

Every LBO model starts with a Sources & Uses table — where does the money come from (sources) and where does it go (uses)?

**Example: PKR 20B acquisition of a Pakistani FMCG company**

| Sources | PKR (B) | % |
|---|---|---|
| Senior secured debt | 8.0 | 40% |
| Mezzanine / subordinated debt | 2.0 | 10% |
| PE sponsor equity | 10.0 | 50% |
| **Total Sources** | **20.0** | **100%** |

| Uses | PKR (B) | % |
|---|---|---|
| Purchase price (equity) | 17.5 | 87.5% |
| Assumed / refinanced debt | 0.0 | 0% |
| Transaction fees & expenses | 1.5 | 7.5% |
| Minimum cash to balance sheet | 1.0 | 5.0% |
| **Total Uses** | **20.0** | **100%** |

**Entry multiple check:**
```
Enterprise Value = Purchase Price + Assumed Net Debt = PKR 17.5B + PKR 0B = PKR 17.5B
Entry EV/EBITDA = PKR 17.5B / PKR 2.5B EBITDA = 7.0x
```

### Debt Structure

PE deals typically use multiple layers of debt, each with different risk, seniority, and cost:

| Tranche | Typical Leverage (x EBITDA) | Interest Rate | Amortization |
|---|---|---|---|
| Revolving credit facility (RCF) | ~0.5–1.0x | Floating + 1.5–2.5% | None (drawn as needed) |
| Senior secured term loan A | ~2.0–3.0x | Floating + 2.0–3.5% | Amortizing (5–7 year) |
| Senior secured term loan B | ~3.0–4.5x | Floating + 3.5–5.0% | Bullet maturity |
| Mezzanine / second lien | ~4.5–5.5x | Floating + 7–12% | PIK interest option |
| PIK (Payment in Kind) notes | ~5.5–6.5x | Fixed 12–16%, PIK | Bullet |

**Pakistan LBO debt context:**
Pakistan's PE market is smaller and less mature than Gulf or Western markets. Leveraged lending infrastructure (institutional term loan B market, high-yield bonds) doesn't exist at the same scale. Pakistan PE deals typically use:
- Senior bank debt (MCB, HBL, UBL structured finance desks): 3–4x EBITDA
- Mezzanine: rarely available from local market; sometimes from DFIs (IFC, ADB)
- Higher equity contribution: 50–60% equity vs. 30–40% in mature markets

**Gulf LBO debt context:**
UAE/Saudi PE deals can access international term loan B markets for larger deals ($200M+). Local GCC banks (Emirates NBD, FAB, Saudi National Bank) provide senior debt on sizeable deals. Leverage typically 4–5x EBITDA for quality assets.

### The Debt Waterfall

Cash flows service debt in priority order (senior first, junior last):

```
EBITDA
− Cash interest (senior secured, then mezzanine)
− Tax
− Maintenance capex
= Cash available for debt repayment

Apply first to: Revolver → Term Loan A amortization → Excess cash sweep
```

**Cash sweep:** Most LBO agreements include a "cash sweep" — if the company generates cash above required amortization, the excess sweeps against the debt principal. This accelerates deleveraging and improves equity returns.

## 3. Return Calculation: IRR and MOIC

### The Two Return Metrics

**MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital):**
```
MOIC = Total Equity Proceeds at Exit / Initial Equity Investment
```

Simple and intuitive — if you invested PKR 10B and received PKR 22B back, your MOIC is 2.2x.

**IRR (Internal Rate of Return):**
The annualized compounded return on the equity investment. Accounts for the holding period — a 2.5x MOIC over 3 years is much better than 2.5x MOIC over 7 years.

```
PE Target IRR thresholds:
- Good: 20–25% IRR
- Great: 25–35% IRR
- Outstanding: 35%+ IRR
```

**Quick MOIC-to-IRR reference:**

| MOIC | 3 years | 4 years | 5 years | 6 years | 7 years |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 2.0x | 26% | 19% | 15% | 12% | 10% |
| 2.5x | 36% | 26% | 20% | 16% | 14% |
| 3.0x | 44% | 32% | 25% | 20% | 17% |
| 3.5x | 52% | 37% | 28% | 23% | 19% |

## 4. Worked Example: Pakistan FMCG Company LBO

### Setup

**Company:** A Pakistani packaged food company
- LTM Revenue: PKR 25B
- LTM EBITDA: PKR 3.0B (12% margin)
- Entry EV/EBITDA: 7.0x → Enterprise Value PKR 21B
- Net debt at entry: PKR 0B (clean balance sheet)
- Purchase price (equity): PKR 21B

**Financing:**
- Senior debt: PKR 9.0B (3.0x EBITDA) at 19% (KIBOR + 2%)
- PE equity: PKR 12.0B
- Transaction fees: PKR 1.5B (funded from equity)
- **Total equity invested: PKR 13.5B**

### Operating Projections (PKR B)

| Year | Revenue | EBITDA | D&A | Interest | Tax | FCFF | Debt Repaid |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (Entry) | 25.0 | 3.0 | — | — | — | — | — |
| 1 | 28.0 | 3.6 | 0.8 | 1.7 | 0.6 | 1.5 | 1.5 |
| 2 | 30.8 | 4.0 | 0.8 | 1.4 | 0.8 | 2.0 | 2.0 |
| 3 | 33.9 | 4.5 | 0.9 | 1.1 | 0.9 | 2.6 | 2.6 |
| 4 | 35.6 | 4.8 | 0.9 | 0.5 | 1.1 | 3.3 | 3.3 (debt cleared) |
| 5 | 37.4 | 5.1 | 1.0 | — | 1.2 | 3.9 | — |

**Debt balance:** Starts at PKR 9.0B, repaid progressively from cash sweeps. By Year 4, debt is fully repaid.

### Exit Assumptions

**Year 5 exit at 7.5x EV/EBITDA:**
```
Exit EBITDA: PKR 5.1B
Exit EV/EBITDA: 7.5x (slight premium to entry — margin improvement)
Exit Enterprise Value: PKR 38.3B
Less: Remaining debt: PKR 0B (fully repaid)
Exit Equity Value: PKR 38.3B
```

### Returns

```
Equity invested: PKR 13.5B
Equity received at exit: PKR 38.3B
MOIC: 38.3 / 13.5 = 2.84x
IRR (Year 5): ~23%
```

**Value creation bridge:**

| Driver | EV Contribution (PKR B) |
|---|---|
| EBITDA growth (PKR 3.0B → PKR 5.1B) | +15.8 |
| Multiple expansion (7.0x → 7.5x) | +1.5 |
| Debt paydown (reduces equity claim dilution) | +9.0 |
| Transaction fees drag | −1.5 |
| **Total equity value created** | **+24.8** |

## 5. PE Value Creation Framework

### The Three Levers

**1. Financial engineering (leverage):**
Using debt to amplify equity returns. The benefit is greatest when cost of debt is low relative to the business's ROE. In Pakistan with high KIBOR, the leverage benefit is partially offset by high financing costs.

**2. Operational improvement (EBITDA growth):**
- Revenue growth: expand distribution, new product lines, international entry
- Margin improvement: procurement savings, overhead reduction, plant efficiency
- Working capital optimization: reduce DSO, improve DIO, maintain DPO

**3. Multiple expansion:**
Buying at a lower multiple and selling at a higher multiple. This happens when:
- The business has improved its quality/scale at exit
- Market conditions improve (multiple expansion across all similar companies)
- The business is repositioned for a more strategic buyer who values it differently

**The PE industry has become more operationally focused:** In the 1980s–1990s, most PE returns came from leverage and multiple expansion. Today, top PE firms (KKR, Blackstone, Advent) create most of their value through genuine operational improvement.

### Gulf PE Context

GCC private equity is significant and growing:
- **Abraaj Group (Dubai-based):** Was the largest EM-focused PE firm; collapsed in 2018 in a fraud scandal — a cautionary tale
- **Investcorp (Bahrain):** One of the longest-standing Gulf PE firms; manages $50B+
- **Gulf Capital (UAE):** Mid-market Gulf PE
- **Olayan Group, Alshaya Group:** Family conglomerates that often act as PE-like investors in regional consumer deals

**Saudi Vision 2030 PE activity:**
Saudi Vision 2030 has catalyzed a massive increase in PE activity. PIF (Public Investment Fund) co-invests with global PE firms and has its own direct investment program.

## Self-Assessment

1. A PE firm is evaluating the acquisition of a Pakistani industrial distributor:
   - LTM EBITDA: PKR 1.8B
   - Entry EV/EBITDA: 6.5x → Enterprise Value PKR 11.7B
   - Financing: 45% senior debt (KIBOR + 2.5%, current KIBOR 14%), 55% PE equity
   - Transaction fees: PKR 0.5B

   (a) Build the Sources & Uses table. Calculate total equity invested (including fees).
   (b) If EBITDA grows 12% per year for 5 years and the company exits at 6.0x EV/EBITDA (slight multiple compression), with all debt repaid by Year 5, calculate the exit equity value, MOIC, and approximate IRR.
   (c) The PE firm's investment committee requires a minimum 20% IRR. Using the MOIC-to-IRR table, what MOIC must the investment generate over a 5-year hold to clear this hurdle?
   (d) A limited partner (LP) asks: "Your returns look good but KIBOR has dropped 400bps since entry. How does this change the returns?" Explain the two effects of KIBOR decline on LBO returns.

2. Value creation bridge analysis:

   A Gulf PE firm acquired a UAE-based logistics company 4 years ago at 8.0x EV/EBITDA with entry EBITDA of $40M. They are now preparing to exit. Exit EBITDA is $65M. The exit buyer has offered 9.5x EV/EBITDA. Initial debt was 50% of entry EV and has been repaid to 20% of exit EV.

   (a) Calculate entry and exit Enterprise Values. Calculate equity value at entry and exit.
   (b) Decompose the equity return into: (i) EBITDA growth contribution, (ii) multiple expansion contribution, (iii) debt paydown contribution.
   (c) The PE firm contributed 50% equity at entry. Calculate MOIC and IRR over 4 years.
   (d) The PE firm originally planned a 5-year hold. Why might they exit after 4 years instead? Identify three factors that would accelerate an exit decision.
