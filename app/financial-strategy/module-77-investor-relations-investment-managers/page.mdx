# Module 77 — Investor Relations for Investment Managers

Raising and retaining capital is as critical as generating returns. The best investment process is worthless if investors will not allocate to your fund, and returns alone do not keep LPs — relationships, transparency, and professional communication do. This module covers how to build and run investor relations for a professional investment management firm, applied throughout to FERROQUANT Capital.

## Learning Objectives

- Design a capital raising strategy targeting appropriate investor types for a Pakistan-focused quant fund
- Prepare a Due Diligence Questionnaire (DDQ) that satisfies institutional standards
- Understand ILPA reporting principles and implement them for FERROQUANT
- Negotiate side letters with institutional LPs
- Build an IR calendar and communication framework
- Navigate the specific dynamics of raising capital from Gulf SWFs and family offices

## 1. Capital Raising Strategy

### Investor Segmentation

Not all investors are appropriate for every fund. Before raising capital, define your target investor:

| Investor Type | Typical Ticket | Decision Timeline | Sophistication | Requirements |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gulf SWF (ADIA, QIA, Mubadala) | USD 50–200M | 12–24 months | Very high | Rigorous ODD, GIPS, global prime broker |
| Gulf family office | USD 5–25M | 3–9 months | High | Relationship-first, Arabic/Urdu rapport |
| Pakistani HNWI / family office | PKR 50–500M | 1–6 months | Medium | Trust in manager, less formal process |
| Pakistani institutional (insurance, pension) | PKR 500M–2B | 6–18 months | Medium-high | SECP approval, board sign-off |
| Fund of funds | USD 10–50M | 6–12 months | Very high | Track record minimum 2 years |
| Endowments / foundations | USD 10–50M | 12–18 months | High | Mission alignment, ESG screening |

**FERROQUANT's realistic early-stage investor profile:**
- First 18 months: Pakistani HNWIs and family offices (softer requirements, relationship-based)
- Year 2–3: Pakistani institutional investors (insurance, NBFC, corporate treasury)
- Year 3+: Gulf family offices (reputation building phase)
- Year 5+: Gulf SWFs (requires multi-year track record, full GIPS compliance)

### The Capital Raising Process

**Phase 1 — Preparation (before first meeting):**
- Finalize fund documents: PPM (Private Placement Memorandum), LPA, subscription agreement
- Prepare marketing materials: pitch deck, one-pager, DDQ template
- Build GIPS-compliant track record presentation
- Complete all regulatory licensing (SECP NBFC, SBP approvals)

**Phase 2 — Pipeline development:**
- Identify target investors through warm introductions (much more effective than cold outreach)
- Placement agents: third-party capital raisers who access institutional LPs for a fee (typically 1% of committed capital, 0.5% of NAV annually in some cases)
- Conference presence: Milken Global, SuperReturn, Pakistan pension/insurance conferences
- LinkedIn and professional network

**Phase 3 — Investor meetings:**
- Introduction meeting: pitch the strategy, team, and differentiation
- Deep-dive meeting: detailed investment process, risk management
- ODD meeting: operations, technology, compliance (investor's ODD team)
- Investment committee: final allocation decision by investor

**Phase 4 — Closing:**
- Legal review of LPA, subscription documents
- Side letter negotiation
- Wire of capital, issue of LP units/shares

### Placement Agents

Placement agents are regulated firms that introduce institutional investors to fund managers. They are particularly valuable for:
- Accessing Gulf SWFs (very hard to approach without introduction)
- Navigating the institutional sales process in foreign markets
- Adding credibility to a newer manager

**Fees:** Typically 1–2% of capital raised as a one-time fee, sometimes plus a trailer (ongoing annual fee of 0.25–0.50% of referred AUM).

**Regulatory requirement:** Placement agents must be properly licensed — SEC-registered in the US, DFSA-licensed in Dubai, FCA-authorized in the UK. SECP has no specific placement agent license; they operate under existing broker-dealer frameworks.

## 2. DDQ Preparation

### What a DDQ Is

A Due Diligence Questionnaire (DDQ) is a standardized document that institutional investors use to assess a fund manager. It covers investment process, risk management, operations, compliance, team, and performance.

The ILPA (Institutional Limited Partners Association) publishes a standard DDQ template widely used by institutional LPs. Completing a thorough DDQ is the entry ticket to serious institutional capital.

### FERROQUANT DDQ — Key Sections

**Section 1 — Firm Overview**
- Firm background: founded, ownership, regulatory status (SECP NBFC license number)
- AUM: current, peak, and 12-month trend
- Number of investment professionals vs total staff
- Key person risk: who are the key individuals, what are their succession arrangements?

**Section 2 — Investment Team**
- Portfolio managers: educational background, years of experience, prior firms
- Analysts: research coverage, rotation policy
- Risk team: independence from portfolio management
- Turnover: has anyone left in the last 3 years? Why?

**Section 3 — Investment Process**
- Universe definition: how is the investable universe defined?
- Signal generation: what factors/signals are used?
- Portfolio construction: how are signals converted to positions?
- Risk management: position limits, sector limits, stop-loss rules
- Execution: how are orders executed to minimize market impact?
- Trade decision authority: who approves trades above certain sizes?

**Section 4 — Risk Management**
- Risk metrics reported daily: VaR, factor exposures, sector concentrations, liquidity
- Risk limits: hard limits (breach = mandatory reduction) vs soft limits (breach = review)
- Historical limit breaches: were there any? What happened?
- Leverage limits: maximum gross and net exposure
- Liquidity stress test: how long to liquidate the portfolio at 20% of ADV?

**Section 5 — Operations & Technology**
- Custodian, administrator, auditor, legal counsel (names, contacts)
- OMS and PMS systems used
- Reconciliation process: frequency, who reviews, how breaks are resolved
- Cybersecurity: MFA, encryption, penetration testing frequency

**Section 6 — Compliance**
- SECP NBFC registration details
- Personal account dealing policy
- Conflicts of interest policy
- Material litigation: any current or historical?
- Regulatory inquiries: any current or historical?

**Section 7 — Performance**
- GIPS-compliant composite presentation
- Attribution analysis: allocation, selection, and interaction effects
- Risk-adjusted metrics: Sharpe, IR, Sortino, Calmar, max drawdown
- Drawdown history: largest 3 drawdowns, duration, recovery time

**Presentation tip:** The DDQ is also a marketing document. Every answer should be honest AND present FERROQUANT in the strongest accurate light. Weak sections (short track record, small team) should be framed with compensating strengths.

## 3. ILPA Reporting Standards

### What ILPA Is

The Institutional Limited Partners Association (ILPA) represents institutional investors in private equity and hedge funds. Their reporting templates have become the de facto standard for professional LP-GP communication.

### ILPA Fee Reporting Template

ILPA's fee reporting template standardizes how management fees, performance fees, portfolio company fees, and expense allocations are disclosed. Key disclosures:

**Management fees:**
- Gross management fee
- Fee offsets (portfolio company monitoring fees that reduce management fee)
- Net management fee paid by LP

**Performance fees:**
- Performance fee calculation methodology
- HWM details
- Performance fee paid in the period
- Cumulative performance fee paid since inception

**Expenses:**
- Organizational expenses (amortized over fund life)
- Operating expenses (audit, legal, insurance)
- Portfolio expenses (deal costs, advisory fees)
- Broken deal expenses
- Whether expenses are shared with affiliated funds

**Importance of ILPA compliance:** Major institutional LPs (CalPERS, APG, CPPIB) now require ILPA-compliant reporting as a condition of investment. For Gulf SWFs, ILPA compliance signals professionalism and alignment with LP interests.

### ILPA Capital Account Statement

Every LP should receive a capital account statement showing:
- Opening NAV
- Capital contributions (calls)
- Capital distributions (returns of capital and profit)
- Management fees charged
- Performance fees charged
- Unrealized/realized P&L
- Closing NAV

This is the LP's official record of their investment in the fund. It must be audited annually.

## 4. Side Letters

### What Side Letters Are

A side letter is a separate agreement between the GP (fund manager) and a specific LP that modifies or supplements the standard LPA terms for that investor. Side letters are common — virtually every institutional LP negotiates one.

### Common Side Letter Provisions

**Most Favored Nation (MFN) clause:**
The LP has the right to elect the benefit of any more favorable terms granted to any other LP. If FERROQUANT gives LP A a 10bps management fee discount, LP B with an MFN clause can elect the same discount.

**Implication for FERROQUANT:** Be careful what you grant as "favorable terms" — under MFN, it may be available to all LPs.

**Fee concessions:**
- Management fee discount for large early investors ("anchor LP" terms)
- Performance fee reduction (e.g., 15% instead of 20%) for certain investors
- Fee caps for very large allocations

**Co-investment rights:**
The LP has the right (not obligation) to participate alongside the fund in specific investments, often on a no-fee, no-carry basis. Extremely valuable for large institutional LPs — they get access to the fund's deal flow without paying fees on that capital.

**Reporting accommodations:**
- Specific report formats
- Tax reporting requirements (FATCA/CRS certificates, US K-1 for US LPs)
- Audit access rights
- Regulatory reporting (e.g., LP needs specific data for Solvency II calculation)

**AIFMD transparency:**
European LPs subject to AIFMD may require specific disclosures about leverage, liquidity, and risk management that are required by their own regulators.

**Redemption terms:**
- Shorter notice period for large LPs
- Guaranteed redemption liquidity (GP commits to source liquidity)
- Carve-out from gate provisions

### Side Letter Register

Maintain a centralized register of all side letters and the specific rights granted. Before granting any side letter term, check:
1. Is the term consistent with the LPA (some terms cannot be overridden by side letter)?
2. What MFN obligations does this trigger?
3. Is the term operationally feasible?
4. Does granting this term disadvantage other LPs (fiduciary concern)?

## 5. LP Communication Calendar

### Principles of Effective LP Communication

**Be proactive, not reactive:** Communicate bad news before investors ask. A fund that tells investors about a drawdown before they see it in the NAV is building trust; a fund that goes silent during a drawdown destroys it.

**No surprises:** Investors should never learn material information about the fund from someone other than the manager.

**Distinguish between material and immaterial:** Not every blip in performance requires a call. A 2% drawdown in a volatile strategy is noise. A 15% drawdown in a month that was supposed to be defensive is material — call every LP.

### FERROQUANT IR Calendar

**Monthly:**
- NAV notification (within 5 business days of month-end)
- Brief performance commentary (2–3 paragraphs: return, attribution, market context, positioning)
- Portfolio exposure summary (sector weights, net/gross, top 10 positions — with appropriate disclosure)

**Quarterly:**
- Full quarterly letter (4–8 pages):
  - Market and macro review
  - Portfolio review: what worked, what didn't, and why
  - Strategy outlook
  - Any operational updates
  - Risk metrics (VaR, max drawdown, Sharpe since inception)
- ILPA-compliant capital account statement
- Fee disclosure statement

**Annual:**
- Audited financial statements (within 120 days of year-end)
- Annual letter: full-year review, strategy evolution, outlook
- Annual meeting (for significant LPs): in-person or video meeting with portfolio manager

**Ad hoc:**
- Material events: drawdown > 10% from recent peak, key person change, regulatory inquiry, strategy change
- These require immediate communication — within 24–48 hours

### Handling a Difficult Period

When performance is bad:
1. **Do not hide:** Report NAV on time, do not delay
2. **Communicate what happened:** Be specific — which positions, which strategy elements
3. **Explain the thesis:** Is the investment thesis still intact or has it broken down?
4. **Describe the response:** What have you changed (if anything) and why?
5. **Avoid promises:** Do not promise recovery timelines — you don't know

**Example (FERROQUANT drawdown letter excerpt):**
> "FERROQUANT Pakistan Equity Fund declined 8.4% in October, bringing year-to-date performance to +3.2%. The KSE-100 declined 6.2% over the same period. Our additional loss versus benchmark was driven by three factors: [specific attribution]. The strategy's underlying thesis — that value and momentum factors are mis-priced on PSX — remains intact based on [evidence]. We have reduced exposure in [specific area] given [specific reasoning], and our current positioning is described below..."

## 6. Gulf SWF and Family Office Engagement

### Gulf SWF Dynamics

Gulf Sovereign Wealth Funds (ADIA, QIA, PIF, Mubadala, ADIC, IPIC) are among the most sophisticated institutional investors in the world. Engaging them requires:

**Multi-year relationship building:**
SWFs rarely invest on first contact. Build relationships through conferences (SALT, SuperReturn MENA), industry networks, and warm introductions before presenting formally.

**Principal access:**
Gulf SWF investment decisions are ultimately made by people — the CIO and portfolio managers. Access to principals (not just relationship managers) is essential. This requires building genuine professional relationships over years.

**Process alignment:**
SWFs have rigorous investment approval processes. Understand the process before starting:
- Who reviews the DDQ? (usually a dedicated manager research team)
- Who does the investment committee presentation?
- What is the approval authority for this allocation size?
- What are the standard documentation requirements?

**Customization:**
Gulf SWFs often want customized mandates rather than standard fund participation. FERROQUANT may need to offer a separately managed account (SMA) structure for large Gulf allocations — they own the underlying securities, managed by FERROQUANT under an investment management agreement.

### Gulf Family Office Dynamics

Gulf family offices (Al-Futtaim, Majid Al Futtaim, Olayan, Almarai family, etc.) are different from SWFs:
- **Decision-making:** Faster, often by 2–3 family principals
- **Relationship primacy:** The family principal relationship is everything — warm introduction from a trusted party is almost essential
- **Discretion:** Many family offices do not publicize their investments — respect confidentiality
- **Pakistan connection:** Families with Pakistan business interests (CPEC adjacency, Pakistan manufacturing) may be more open to Pakistan-focused investment funds
- **Majlis culture:** In Saudi/Gulf context, relationship meetings may happen in informal settings (majlis). Patience, respect for hierarchy, and building personal trust before talking business are non-negotiable.

### Pakistan Diaspora as LP Source

The Pakistani diaspora in the Gulf is significant — estimated 4–5 million Pakistanis in UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait. Many are successful professionals and entrepreneurs with:
- Emotional connection to Pakistan investment
- Understanding of the market and its dynamics
- USD savings looking for higher returns than Gulf bank deposits

**FERROQUANT outreach strategy:**
- Pakistan professionals' networks in Dubai/Abu Dhabi (LUMS alumni, Karachi/Lahore professional networks)
- Pakistan chambers of commerce in UAE and Saudi Arabia
- Social media and financial content (LinkedIn, financial education content) to build awareness

## Self-Assessment

1. FERROQUANT Capital has PKR 2B AUM after 18 months of operation, a track record of +32% cumulative, and a Sharpe ratio of 0.72. You want to raise an additional PKR 3B over the next 18 months.

   (a) Prioritize the following three investor types based on what is achievable in 18 months: (i) ADIA (Gulf SWF), (ii) Pakistani insurance company, (iii) Dubai-based Pakistani family office managing USD 500M. Justify your prioritization.
   (b) What are the three most important things you need to put in place before approaching the Pakistani insurance company? Be specific about regulatory, documentation, and track record requirements.
   (c) A placement agent offers to introduce FERROQUANT to 5 Gulf family offices in exchange for 1.5% of capital raised plus 0.5% annual trailer. On a PKR 3B raise at PKR/USD 280, evaluate whether this is worth the cost.
   (d) One investor asks for a separately managed account (SMA) rather than a fund investment. What are the advantages and disadvantages for FERROQUANT of accepting an SMA mandate?

2. A Gulf SWF is reviewing FERROQUANT's DDQ. Their ODD team has flagged three concerns:
   - Concern A: FERROQUANT's administrator is the CFO of the management company (not independent)
   - Concern B: The fund has only 18 months of audited track record
   - Concern C: The valuation policy for any OTC instruments is vague
   
   (a) Which of these three concerns is most serious and potentially a hard stop for the investment? Why?
   (b) How would you address Concern A without abandoning it outright (you cannot afford an independent administrator yet)?
   (c) Draft a 3-sentence response to Concern B that presents the situation honestly but constructively.
   (d) Draft a valuation policy for OTC instruments (3–4 sentences) that would satisfy a sophisticated allocator.

3. FERROQUANT has two LP side letter requests:
   
   **LP1 (Gulf family office, PKR 500M):** Wants a 25bps management fee discount (from 1.5% to 1.25%) and a Most Favored Nation clause.
   **LP2 (Pakistani insurance company, PKR 1.5B):** Wants quarterly liquidity with 30-day notice (standard fund terms are quarterly with 90-day notice), a 20% gate carve-out, and co-investment rights on any deals above PKR 200M.
   
   (a) Calculate the annual revenue impact of granting LP1's fee discount. Given LP1's ticket size, is this reasonable?
   (b) What does the MFN clause in LP1's side letter mean for every other LP that has negotiated favorable terms? Describe the operational implication.
   (c) LP2's 30-day notice request creates a liquidity mismatch for the fund (you need 90 days to liquidate in an orderly manner). How would you handle this negotiation?
   (d) LP2 wants co-investment rights on deals above PKR 200M. For FERROQUANT's quant PSX equity strategy (where all positions are liquid market trades), is this provision meaningful? What type of fund would this be most relevant for?
