# Module 120 — Liability Management & Debt Restructuring

When a company's debt profile becomes problematic — too expensive, wrong maturity profile, too much for the business to service — it needs to actively manage its liabilities. Liability management encompasses proactive exercises (tender offers, exchange offers, consent solicitations) done from a position of relative strength, as well as reactive restructurings done under financial distress. This module covers the full spectrum.

## Learning Objectives

- Execute a tender offer and exchange offer for corporate bonds or loans
- Understand consent solicitation mechanics
- Explain the debt-for-equity swap and when it is used
- Identify and address the holdout problem in debt restructuring
- Apply Pakistan TFC consent solicitation and restructuring mechanics
- Analyze Gulf debt restructuring case studies (NMC Health, DAMAC)

---

## 1. Tender Offer

A tender offer is an invitation by the issuer to bondholders to sell their bonds back to the issuer at a specified price (typically at a premium to face value).

**Why issuers do tender offers:**
- Refinance at lower rates (buy back high-coupon bonds; issue new lower-coupon bonds)
- Extend maturity profile (replace bonds maturing soon with new longer-dated bonds)
- Reduce debt (if company has excess cash)
- Remove restrictive covenants (buy out bonds with tight covenants before undertaking a transaction)

**Tender offer mechanics:**
1. Issuer announces tender offer at a specific price (e.g., 105 cents on the dollar)
2. Tender period: typically 20–30 business days
3. Bondholders tender their bonds or decline
4. Issuer accepts tendered bonds (up to a specified maximum); pays cash

**Early bird premium:** Bondholders who tender early (within first 10 days) typically receive a 1–2 cent premium over the base tender price — incentivizes participation.

---

## 2. Exchange Offer

Instead of paying cash, the issuer offers new securities in exchange for existing securities:
- New bonds for old bonds (different coupon, maturity, or terms)
- Equity for bonds (debt-for-equity swap)

**Distressed exchange offer:** When a company cannot afford to repay bonds at maturity, it offers bondholders new bonds (longer maturity, lower coupon) or equity. Bondholders face the choice between accepting the exchange or risking a worse outcome in bankruptcy.

**Pakistan context:** Pakistan's TFC market has seen exchange offers when issuers faced maturity wall difficulties — particularly during 2008 and 2022–2023 economic stress periods.

---

## 3. Consent Solicitation

A consent solicitation asks bondholders to approve amendments to the bond's terms (indenture) without buying back the bonds.

**Common amendments:**
- Remove a restrictive covenant (e.g., negative pledge, restricted payments) to allow a new transaction
- Extend a covenant waiver period
- Modify event of default provisions
- Change financial maintenance covenant thresholds

**Voting threshold:** Typically requires 50–75% of outstanding bonds to consent (varies by indenture).

**Quorum and payment:** Issuers typically offer a consent fee (e.g., 25–50bps of face value) to compensate bondholders for granting consents.

## 4. The Holdout Problem

### Why Restructuring Is Hard

**Holdout problem:** In any debt restructuring, each individual creditor has an incentive to hold out (refuse to accept the restructuring terms) while hoping other creditors agree. If 90% of creditors accept and the remaining 10% hold out:
- The 10% holdouts may retain full par claims while accepting creditors received discounted new securities
- The restructured company is still viable (benefiting holdouts who get paid in full at the expense of cooperative creditors)

**This creates a classic prisoner's dilemma** — individual rationality leads to collective failure.

### Solving the Holdout Problem

**Contractual solutions:**
- **Collective Action Clauses (CACs):** Sovereign bond terms allowing a supermajority (typically 75%) to bind all bondholders to restructuring terms
- **Exit consent:** Creditors who accept the exchange simultaneously vote to remove covenants from the old bonds — making holdout bonds less valuable

**Legal solutions:**
- **Cramdown (Chapter 11 US / Administration UK):** Court can impose restructuring terms on dissenting creditors if a majority approve and the plan passes the "best interests" test
- **Pakistan Companies Act 2017 scheme of arrangement:** Court-sanctioned scheme can bind dissenting creditors (75% by value must approve; court then sanctions)

## 5. Debt-for-Equity Swap

When a company cannot service its debt, lenders may agree to convert part of their debt claims into equity. This:
- Eliminates debt service (no more interest payments on converted debt)
- Gives lenders upside if the business recovers
- Dilutes existing shareholders massively (existing equity may be nearly wiped out)

**When debt-for-equity swaps occur:**
- Insolvent company (equity = 0 before restructuring; lenders are the economic owners)
- Lenders believe the business has value as a going concern
- Alternative (liquidation) is worse for lenders

**Gulf example: NMC Health (2020)**
NMC Health (UAE-based healthcare group, listed on LSE) discovered $6.6B of hidden debt in 2020. The company was placed into administration. Creditor banks (including FAB, Emirates NBD) agreed to a debt-for-equity conversion as part of the restructuring — banks became the owners of the hospital group.
