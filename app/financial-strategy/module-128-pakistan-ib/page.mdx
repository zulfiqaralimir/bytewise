# Module 128 — Pakistan IB: Deals, Regulations & Opportunities

Pakistan's investment banking market is underserved relative to its economic size — a $350B GDP economy with a fragmented, mostly domestic IB industry, limited institutional depth, and an M&A pipeline constrained by family ownership and political uncertainty. Yet the opportunities are significant: a young population of 240M, a growing middle class, CPEC infrastructure, underpenetrated financial services, and a diaspora with capital to deploy. This module synthesizes the Pakistan IB landscape.

## Learning Objectives

- Understand the structure and key players in Pakistan's IB industry
- Navigate SECP, CCP, SBP, and PSX regulatory frameworks for transactions
- Identify the landmark deals that define Pakistan's M&A history
- Analyze emerging deal flow themes (digital banking, renewable energy, consumer, FinTech)
- Build a competitive intelligence map of Pakistan IB firms

## 1. Pakistan IB Industry Structure

### Key Players

**Full-service investment banks (NBFC-IB licensed):**
- **Arif Habib Limited:** Largest and most prestigious Pakistan IB firm; strong in ECM, fixed income, and M&A advisory; part of Arif Habib Group
- **JS Global Capital:** Strong in equity research, brokerage, and capital markets; part of JS Group
- **Next Capital:** Boutique IB with M&A and ECM capabilities
- **BMA Capital:** Mid-size IB with strong fixed income and equity capabilities

**Commercial banks with IB divisions:**
- HBL Investment Banking
- UBL Fund Managers (asset management)
- MCB Securities

**International banks present in Pakistan (limited IB):**
- Standard Chartered Pakistan: project finance and structured finance
- Deutsche Bank (limited presence): DCM and trade finance
- Citibank Pakistan: corporate banking and structured solutions

**Big 4 accounting firms (transaction advisory):**
KPMG, EY, Deloitte, PwC Pakistan are major providers of financial due diligence, transaction advisory, and valuation services — often competing with IB firms for advisory mandates.

### Fee Economics

Pakistan's IB fee market is compressed vs. global standards:
- M&A advisory: 0.75–2.0% of deal value for mid-market
- IPO: 2.0–3.5% total fees (underwriting + selling commission)
- Rights issues: 1.0–2.5%
- TFC/Sukuk issuance: 0.5–1.5%

A typical mid-market Pakistan M&A deal (PKR 5–15B) generates PKR 50–300M in fees — challenging economics for building a dedicated IB team.

## 2. Regulatory Framework for Pakistan IB

### SECP Licensing

Investment banking services in Pakistan are regulated by SECP under the NBFC (Non-Banking Finance Companies) framework:
- **NBFC-IB (Investment Banking):** For M&A advisory, underwriting, and ECM
- **NBFC-IE (Investment Finance):** Includes asset finance
- Minimum capital: PKR 200M paid-up capital for NBFC-IB

### PSX Mechanics

PSX (Pakistan Stock Exchange) operates:
- **Main Board:** For established companies meeting minimum listing requirements
- **Emerging Companies Board:** For smaller/newer companies (lighter requirements)
- **Growth Enterprise Market (GEM):** For startups and high-growth companies
- Trading hours: Monday–Friday, 9:30 AM – 3:30 PM (Karachi local time)
- Settlement: T+2 (trade date + 2 business days)

**Circuit breakers:** PSX limits intraday movement — ±7.5% on individual stocks; ±5% index movement triggers trading halt.

### Competition Commission of Pakistan (CCP)

CCP must be notified of mergers/acquisitions meeting thresholds:
- Combined annual turnover exceeds PKR 1 billion, OR
- Combined value of assets exceeds PKR 1 billion
- Notification must be filed before closing (pre-merger notification)
- Phase 1 review: 30 days; Phase 2: up to 90 additional days

CCP has rarely blocked transactions but has imposed conditions (e.g., divestitures in cement sector).

## 3. Landmark Pakistan M&A Transactions

### The Deals That Define Pakistan's M&A History

**MCB Bank privatization (1991):**
Government sold 26% of MCB to the Mansha family (Nishat Group). One of Pakistan's most successful privatizations — MCB is now one of Pakistan's most profitable banks.

**Engro Foods acquisition by FrieslandCampina (2016):**
$452M acquisition of 51% stake in Engro Foods (Olper's, Omore). Pakistan's largest consumer sector M&A deal. Advisor: international banks + local advisors.

**HBL partial privatization (2004):**
26% stake sold to Aga Khan Fund for Economic Development. Largest Pakistan financial sector M&A deal at time of transaction.

**PTCL privatization (2006):**
26% sold to Etisalat (UAE) for $2.6B — Pakistan's largest single foreign direct investment. Transaction has been controversial (Etisalat alleged non-delivery of PTCL assets).

**K-Electric (KESC) privatization (2005):**
Sold to AES (US), later transferred to Abraaj Group. Ongoing saga: Shanghai Electric Group agreed to acquire for $1.77B in 2016 but deal still unresolved.

**Careem acquisition (2019):**
Uber acquired Careem (Dubai-based, Pakistan operations) for $3.1B. Not a "Pakistan deal" per se, but the largest exit involving a company with major Pakistan operations.

## 4. Emerging Deal Flow Themes

### Digital Banking & FinTech
5 new digital banking licenses issued by SBP in 2023. These entities will need:
- Capital raises (each requires PKR 1B+ minimum capital)
- Technology partnerships and potential M&A
- Potential consolidation as the market matures (5 licenses may become 2–3 viable banks)

### Renewable Energy
Pakistan's renewable energy sector is one of the fastest-growing deal flow areas:
- Solar and wind IPPs raising project finance
- Operational renewable assets attracting PE and infrastructure fund buyers
- CPEC renewable energy component growing

### Consumer/FMCG
Pakistan's consumer market: 240M people, growing middle class, young demographics.
- Multinational FMCG companies acquiring local brands (Nestlé, Unilever, P&G)
- Gulf investors acquiring Pakistani consumer companies
- QSR (Quick Service Restaurant) franchise consolidation

### IT/Software Services Export
Pakistan's IT exports growing rapidly ($2.6B in FY2023):
- Domestic IT companies with Gulf/North America client bases are acquisition targets
- International IT companies acquiring Pakistani engineering talent centers
- Systems Limited (PSX-listed) as potential acquisition target or serial acquirer

## Self-Assessment

1. You are pitching for the sell-side mandate on a PKR 8B transaction (sale of a Pakistani FMCG company to a Gulf strategic buyer). A Pakistani IB firm (Arif Habib) and an international bank (Standard Chartered) are pitching against you.
   (a) What credentials and capabilities would differentiate your pitch? Be specific — what does your firm have that competitors don't?
   (b) The client's CFO asks: "Why should we use a local IB firm vs. a bulge bracket international bank?" Make the case for using a local firm.
   (c) Standard Chartered offers to underwrite the transaction (commit to finding a buyer). What are the implications of accepting an underwritten mandate vs. a best-efforts mandate?
   (d) The CFO also asks about fees. Propose a fee structure (retainer + success fee formula) that is market-appropriate for Pakistan.

2. A foreign PE fund is entering Pakistan for the first time and wants to understand the regulatory landscape:
   (a) Walk them through the three main regulators they will encounter (SECP, CCP, SBP) and what each requires for a typical acquisition.
   (b) The fund's LP agreement requires all investments to be in USD. The target generates PKR revenues. How do they structure the investment to comply with SBP's foreign investment regulations while maintaining USD-equivalent exposure?
   (c) Pakistan's political environment is volatile. What governance protections should the fund negotiate in the shareholder agreement to protect against a change in government policy that affects their investment?
   (d) The fund plans to exit in 5 years. What are the three most likely exit routes in Pakistan, and which would you recommend for a PKR 3B investment?
