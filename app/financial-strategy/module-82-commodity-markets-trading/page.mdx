# Module 82 — Commodity Markets & Trading

Commodities are the raw inputs of the global economy — energy, metals, and agricultural products. They are also a distinct asset class with unique structural features: physical delivery, storage costs, seasonal demand, geopolitical concentration risk, and weather dependence. For FERROQUANT, commodities offer both diversification benefits and systematic trading opportunities through the futures market.

## Learning Objectives

- Explain the cost of carry model and how it prices commodity futures
- Distinguish contango from backwardation and identify their drivers
- Calculate roll yield and explain why it dominates commodity returns over time
- Apply the theory of storage and convenience yield to commodity pricing
- Understand weather derivatives and their use in agricultural risk management
- Identify tradable systematic premia in commodity futures markets
- Navigate Pakistan's commodity markets (PMEX, agricultural commodities, energy imports)

## 1. Commodity Market Structure

### Physical vs. Derivatives Markets

**Physical (spot) market:** Actual commodity is bought and sold for immediate delivery. Buyers are end users (refineries buying crude, mills buying wheat), sellers are producers (oil companies, farmers).

**Futures market:** Standardized contracts to buy/sell a commodity at a future date at a price agreed today. 95%+ of futures contracts are closed out before delivery — used for price discovery and hedging, not physical delivery.

**Key commodity exchanges:**
| Exchange | Location | Key Contracts |
|---|---|---|
| CME/NYMEX | New York | Crude oil (WTI), natural gas, gold, copper, grains |
| ICE | London/Atlanta | Brent crude, natural gas, sugar, coffee, cocoa |
| LME | London | Aluminum, copper, zinc, nickel, tin, lead |
| SHFE | Shanghai | Copper, aluminum, gold, rubber, rebar |
| PMEX | Karachi | Gold, crude oil, palm oil (Pakistan) |

### Commodity Market Participants

**Hedgers (commercial participants):**
- **Producers:** Oil companies, mining companies, farmers — sell futures to lock in sale prices, protecting against price falls
- **Consumers:** Airlines (jet fuel), food manufacturers (wheat), copper wire makers — buy futures to lock in input costs, protecting against price rises

**Speculators (financial participants):**
- **Commodity Trading Advisors (CTAs):** Systematic trend-followers, typically long commodities in uptrends
- **Commodity index funds:** Passive long exposure via futures rolling (S&P GSCI, Bloomberg Commodity Index)
- **Hedge funds:** Fundamental and quantitative strategies across commodity complex
- **FERROQUANT:** Quantitative strategies, carry trades, momentum, curve trades

**Risk transfer:** Hedgers pay a risk premium to speculators for taking on commodity price risk. This structural risk transfer is the theoretical basis for commodity risk premia.

## 2. Commodity Futures Pricing: The Cost of Carry Model

### Futures Pricing Formula

For a commodity with no convenience yield or storage costs (like a financial asset), the futures price should equal the spot price compounded at the risk-free rate:
```
F = S × e^(r × T)
```

For a commodity with storage costs (u) and convenience yield (y):
```
F = S × e^((r + u − y) × T)
```

Where:
- **r** = risk-free interest rate (financing cost)
- **u** = storage cost rate (warehousing, insurance, spoilage — expressed as % of spot price per year)
- **y** = convenience yield (the non-monetary benefit of holding the physical commodity)
- **T** = time to futures maturity (in years)

### Contango and Backwardation

**Contango:** F > S (futures price > spot price)
- Occurs when: storage costs + financing costs > convenience yield
- The futures curve slopes upward (near-dated contracts cheaper than far-dated)
- Common in: crude oil (large storage costs), natural gas (storage and seasonality), base metals

**Backwardation:** F < S (futures price < spot price)
- Occurs when: convenience yield > storage costs + financing costs
- The futures curve slopes downward (near-dated contracts more expensive than far-dated)
- Common in: agricultural commodities at harvest time, crude oil during supply shocks
- Signal: the market is willing to pay a premium for physical commodity now (scarcity, urgency)

**Full contango:** F = S × e^((r + u) × T) — the maximum contango when convenience yield = 0.

**Crude oil futures curve (illustrative):**
```
Spot:  $80/barrel
1-month future:  $80.50  (mild contango)
3-month future:  $81.50
6-month future:  $83.00
12-month future: $85.00
24-month future: $87.00  (full contango)
```

## 3. Roll Yield: The Hidden Driver of Commodity Returns

### Why Roll Yield Dominates

Most commodity futures investors never take delivery. They hold a "rolling" position — buying the front month contract and rolling it to the next month before expiry.

**The roll:** Before the front month expires, sell the front month and buy the next month contract.

**Roll yield:** The return earned (or lost) from rolling from one contract to the next.

```
Roll Yield = (F_near − F_far) / F_near    [if rolling from near to far month]
```

In backwardation:
- Near month trades at a PREMIUM to the far month
- Rolling means: sell high (near month), buy low (far month)
- **Positive roll yield** — rolling is profitable

In contango:
- Near month trades at a DISCOUNT to the far month
- Rolling means: sell low (near month), buy high (far month)
- **Negative roll yield** — rolling is expensive ("roll bleed")

**Why this matters enormously:**
The S&P GSCI index (the main commodity index) lost approximately 0% total return from 2000–2014 while spot commodity prices roughly doubled. The reason: severe negative roll yield in crude oil and natural gas (both in contango) consumed all the spot price gains.

**Roll yield decomposition of commodity total return:**
```
Total Return = Spot Return + Roll Return + Collateral Return
```

- Spot Return: change in spot commodity price
- Roll Return: gain/loss from rolling futures positions
- Collateral Return: risk-free rate earned on the margin posted (treasury bills)

**Implication for quant strategy:** A commodity futures position that earns positive roll yield (backwardated markets) has a structural tailwind. A position in deep contango has a structural headwind.

### Measuring Roll Yield

**Front-to-second-month roll yield (annualized):**
```
Annualized Roll Yield = [(F_1 − F_2) / F_2] × (12 / months_between)
```

**Example — Crude oil:**
- WTI front month (M1): $83.00
- WTI second month (M2): $84.20
- Monthly roll cost: (83.00 − 84.20) / 84.20 = −1.43% per month
- Annualized roll bleed: −1.43% × 12 = −17.1% per year

This is the "drag" a passive long crude oil ETF would experience if spot prices stayed flat — 17% annual loss from rolling alone.

## 4. Convenience Yield and the Theory of Storage

### Convenience Yield

Convenience yield (y) is the implicit benefit of holding the physical commodity rather than a futures contract. It captures:
- **Production flexibility:** A refinery holding crude oil can adjust production schedules
- **Supply assurance:** A manufacturer holding copper inventory avoids production shutdowns if spot prices spike
- **Embedded optionality:** The option to sell into a sudden price spike is only available with physical inventory

**High convenience yield → backwardation:**
When convenience yield is high (inventory is low, supply is tight), the market pays a premium for physical commodity now. Backwardation signals tight current supply.

**Low convenience yield → contango:**
When inventory is abundant, the convenience yield is near zero (there's no urgency to hold physical). Storage costs dominate, pushing the curve into contango.

### Theory of Storage (Kaldor-Working)

The **theory of storage** says that the shape of the futures curve is determined by inventory levels:

```
y (convenience yield) = max(0, c − u)
```

Where c is the marginal value of a unit of inventory (falls as inventory rises, rises as inventory falls) and u is the physical storage cost.

**The Working Curve:** Plots the basis (spot − futures price) against inventory levels. At low inventory, backwardation is strong. At high inventory, full contango. This non-linear relationship is one reason commodity futures curves change shape dramatically in supply shocks.

**FERROQUANT application:** Track crude oil inventory data (EIA weekly in the US, SECP/PSO data in Pakistan) as a leading indicator of crude oil futures curve shape and roll yield.

## 5. Systematic Commodity Strategies

### 1. Commodity Carry (Roll Yield Strategy)

**Principle:** Go long backwardated commodities (positive carry), go short contangoed commodities (negative carry).

**Construction:**
1. For each commodity in the universe, calculate the 1-month annualized roll yield
2. Rank commodities by roll yield
3. Long top quintile (highest roll yield / most backwardated)
4. Short bottom quintile (most negative roll yield / deepest contango)
5. Rebalance monthly

**Historical performance:** Commodity carry has generated positive Sharpe ratios (~0.5–0.8) historically, with returns uncorrelated to equity momentum.

**Risks:**
- Roll yield is backward-looking — curve shape can shift quickly on inventory data or supply news
- Position concentration in energy (largest weight in most commodity universes)
- Crowding: many CTAs run similar strategies, leading to correlated unwinds

### 2. Commodity Momentum (Trend Following)

**Principle:** Commodity prices trend over medium horizons (1–12 months). CTAs exploit these trends systematically.

**Typical implementation:**
1. Calculate 12-month (or 3/6/12-month average) return signal
2. Go long commodities with positive momentum, short those with negative momentum
3. Size positions by inverse volatility (risk parity across commodities)
4. Rebalance monthly or weekly

**Why trends exist in commodities:**
- Slow supply response: a copper supply deficit cannot be solved immediately — new mines take 5–7 years to build, so supply shortfalls persist
- Inventory draw/build cycles: once inventory starts declining (rising prices signal physical tightness), the trend can persist for months
- Hedger behavior: producers aggressively hedge when prices rise (sell futures), but this selling is gradual, not instantaneous

### 3. Commodity Term Structure Strategy

**Principle:** Exploit predictable patterns in how the commodity futures curve reshapes over time.

**Curve steepening trade:**
- If a commodity is in backwardation but spot supply tightness is easing (inventory building), the curve will likely flatten (front month weakens relative to back months)
- Short front month, long back months

**Seasonal roll strategy:**
- Natural gas has predictable seasonality (heating season demand in winter)
- Buy the March–April spread (shoulder season, often contango) and sell the November–January spread (winter, often backwardation)
- Capture the seasonal roll premium systematically

### 4. Commodity Risk Premia: The Hedging Pressure Hypothesis

**Theory (Cootner/Keynes):** Commodity producers are natural sellers of futures (hedging production). Speculators are risk-takers who buy futures. Producers must offer a risk premium to attract speculators, causing futures to be priced below expected future spot prices.

**Backwardation as a risk premium:** When futures are in backwardation, a speculator who goes long earns the risk premium that producers pay to offload price risk.

**Empirical evidence:** Mixed. Markets where producers are dominant hedgers (crude oil, natural gas) tend to show positive long-side returns. Markets where consumers dominate hedging (copper, aluminum) tend to show negative long-side returns from hedging pressure.

## 6. Weather Derivatives

### Weather Risk in Commodities

Weather is the dominant fundamental driver of agricultural commodity prices, natural gas demand (heating/cooling), and hydroelectric power generation.

**Weather risk exposures:**
- **Natural gas:** Cold winters increase heating demand, driving up gas prices; mild winters = weak demand
- **Corn/wheat:** Drought or flooding during growing season can devastate crop yields
- **Hydroelectric power (Pakistan):** Low water levels in Tarbela/Mangla dams reduce power generation capacity
- **FERROQUANT:** Long agricultural commodity positions face weather-driven price spikes

### Weather Derivative Instruments

**Heating Degree Days (HDD) and Cooling Degree Days (CDD):**
Standard weather indices used in energy markets:
```
HDD_daily = max(0, 65°F − Average Daily Temperature)  [US, Fahrenheit]
CDD_daily = max(0, Average Daily Temperature − 65°F)
```

Monthly HDD/CDD accumulates daily readings. High HDD = cold month → high gas demand.

**Weather derivative types:**
- **HDD/CDD swaps:** Pay fixed HDD/CDD, receive realized HDD/CDD (or vice versa) → hedge energy demand uncertainty
- **Temperature options:** Call/put on monthly average temperature
- **Rainfall swaps:** Pay fixed rainfall index, receive actual rainfall → hedge agricultural revenue
- **Catastrophe bonds (CAT bonds):** Triggered by specified weather events (hurricane making landfall, earthquake) — principal at risk if trigger occurs

**Pricing challenge:** Weather derivatives cannot be priced by no-arbitrage (no tradeable underlying). Actuarial methods are used, combining historical weather data with pricing models.

### Pakistan Agricultural Weather Derivatives

Pakistan's agriculture sector (wheat, cotton, rice, sugarcane) is highly sensitive to:
- **Monsoon variability:** Deficient or excessive rainfall in Sindh/Punjab
- **Temperature extremes:** Heat stress on wheat during grain-fill in March–April
- **Water availability:** Indus River flow for irrigation (linked to Himalayan snowmelt)

**SECP and agriculture derivatives:** PMEX lists some agricultural futures (wheat, maize). Weather derivatives remain undeveloped in Pakistan's formal market. Agricultural insurance (PCIC — Pakistan Central Insurance Company) provides some weather risk transfer for farmers.

**FERROQUANT application:**
- Monitor IMD/Pakistan Met Department seasonal forecasts as signals for CBOT wheat/corn positioning
- Consider agricultural commodity positions when El Niño/La Niña forecasts suggest supply disruptions in Pakistan or global agricultural markets
- Long wheat futures as a hedge when FERROQUANT has Pakistan credit exposure to agricultural companies sensitive to crop yields

## 7. Energy Commodities Deep Dive

### Crude Oil: Brent vs. WTI

**WTI (West Texas Intermediate):**
- Delivered at Cushing, Oklahoma (landlocked pipeline hub)
- Lighter, sweeter crude (higher gasoline yield)
- Primary US crude benchmark

**Brent Crude:**
- North Sea crude, seaborne delivery
- Global benchmark (used for pricing ~70% of world's crude, including Pakistan's RLNG imports)
- Typically trades at a slight premium to WTI due to global pricing relevance

**Brent-WTI spread:** Varies with US domestic supply/demand and shipping logistics. A widening spread signals US supply glut (Cushing filling up) or global demand strength.

### Natural Gas

**The storage cycle:** Natural gas is injected into storage April–October (injection season) and withdrawn October–March (heating season). This creates predictable seasonality in the futures curve.

**Henry Hub (US benchmark):** Most liquid natural gas futures market. Highly volatile — natural gas prices have shown greater percentage volatility than crude oil.

**LNG pricing:** Pakistan imports LNG priced off the JKM (Japan Korea Marker) or Brent-linked formula. PSO/SSGC manage LNG import contracts. JKM/Henry Hub spread indicates LNG arbitrage economics.

### Pakistan Energy Commodities

**Pakistan's energy exposure:**
- Pakistan imports ~50% of its oil needs as crude or refined products (PSO is the dominant importer)
- LNG imports through Port Qasim LNG terminal (SSGC, SNGPL)
- Coal imports for power generation (China-Pakistan CPEC power projects)
- Hydro (Tarbela, Mangla, Warsak) provides ~25% of electricity generation

**Circular debt problem:** Pakistan's power sector accumulated PKR 2.3 trillion+ in "circular debt" — unpaid bills cascading from consumers → DISCOs → CPPA-G → IPPs → fuel suppliers. This makes Pakistani energy companies high-credit-risk despite their strategic importance.

**FERROQUANT energy positioning:**
- Global crude oil view expressed through NYMEX/ICE WTI/Brent futures
- Pakistan rupee-denominated exposure: PSO, OGDCL, PPL equities as proxy for oil & gas earnings
- PIB yield curve trades incorporating Pakistan's import bill sensitivity to oil prices (higher oil = larger current account deficit = rupee pressure = higher domestic rates)

## 8. Metals Markets

### Precious Metals

**Gold:** The "fear trade" — gold typically rises during USD weakness, geopolitical uncertainty, and real interest rate declines.

**Gold-rate relationship:**
```
Gold price is inversely related to US real interest rates (TIPS yield)
Real Rate = Nominal Rate − Inflation Expectations
```

Rising real rates raise the opportunity cost of holding gold (no yield) → gold falls.
Falling real rates or negative real rates → gold rises.

**Silver:** Industrial metal + store of value. More volatile than gold. Gold-silver ratio (oz of silver to buy 1 oz of gold) mean-reverts — a high ratio suggests silver is cheap relative to gold.

**Pakistan gold market:** Pakistan is a major gold importing country. PSX has no gold ETF — Pakistanis access gold through jewelry, physical bullion, or PMEX gold futures. SBP holds USD gold reserves (~$4–5B).

### Base Metals (LME Metals)

**Copper:** Often called "Dr. Copper" — its price is a leading indicator of global industrial activity. Copper demand is driven by construction (China), EVs (electrification), and infrastructure.

**Aluminum:** Energy-intensive to produce (smelting requires massive electricity). High energy prices → aluminum production cuts → aluminum price rises. Used in packaging, aerospace, automotive.

**LME forward curve:** LME trades daily forwards from spot to 3 months, then monthly out to 63 months. Cash-3-month spread (the "cash-to-three") is the key indicator of tightness in physical metal markets.

**Base metals and China:** China consumes 50–60% of global copper and aluminum. Chinese PMI data, housing starts, and infrastructure spending are the key fundamental drivers of base metals prices.

## 9. Commodity Indices and Passive Investing

### Major Commodity Indices

| Index | Weighting | Key Features |
|---|---|---|
| S&P GSCI | Production-weighted | Heavy energy tilt (~70% energy) |
| Bloomberg Commodity Index | Diversified | Capped sector weights, 15% max single commodity |
| DBIQ Optimum Yield | Roll-optimized | Selects best roll point on the futures curve |
| SummerHaven SDCI | Term structure-based | Only holds backwardated commodities |

**The index roll schedule:** Passive commodity indices roll their positions on a fixed schedule (GSCI rolls over 5 business days mid-month). Large roll volumes are visible in advance, creating price pressure on near-dated contracts just before the roll (front-month weakens as index sellers pile up).

**Alpha opportunity:** Anticipating and front-running index roll schedules is a classic commodity trading strategy — short the near month (which the index is selling) and long the next month (which the index is buying) just before the roll window.
