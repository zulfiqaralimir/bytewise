# Module 103 — Valuation Football Field & Fairness Opinions

Every IB valuation culminates in two outputs: the football field chart (a visual summary of what the business is worth across different methodologies) and, in formal M&A transactions, a fairness opinion (a written opinion from the bank that the deal consideration is fair from a financial point of view). Understanding how to build, weight, and present these outputs — and the legal liability that comes with them — is essential for anyone working on M&A advisory.

## Learning Objectives

- Build a valuation football field from multiple valuation methodologies
- Weight different methodologies appropriately for different deal contexts
- Present valuation output to a board of directors
- Understand the structure and content of a fairness opinion
- Explain the legal and reputational liability that comes with issuing a fairness opinion
- Apply these outputs to Pakistan and Gulf M&A transactions

---

## 1. The Valuation Football Field

### What It Is

The football field is a horizontal bar chart showing the implied enterprise value (or equity value per share) range from each valuation methodology. Each methodology produces a range — not a single point — and the bars sit side by side, creating a visual "field" of value.

**Why it's called a football field:** The overlapping ranges of bars look like yard lines on an American football field.

### Structure of the Football Field

**Standard methodologies shown (top to bottom):**
1. Trading Comps (comparable company analysis)
2. Precedent Transactions
3. DCF (base case range)
4. LBO Analysis (minimum price a PE buyer would pay)
5. 52-week trading range (for public companies)
6. Analyst price targets (for public companies)
7. **Transaction price** (shown as a single vertical line or separate bar)

**Each bar shows:**
- Low end: 25th percentile multiple × metric, or conservative DCF assumptions
- High end: 75th percentile multiple × metric, or optimistic DCF assumptions
- Sometimes: mean or median marked within the bar

### Reading the Football Field

**Good sign:** The transaction price line falls within or above the overlap zone of multiple methodologies — validating the deal price.

**Red flag:** The transaction price falls above the high end of all methodologies — the buyer may be overpaying.

**Red flag for seller:** The transaction price falls below the low end of most methodologies — the seller may be getting a poor deal.

**Typical ordering:** Precedent transactions usually produce the highest range (control premium + synergies), trading comps the next highest, DCF varies widely, LBO often produces the lowest range (constrained by debt service and return requirements).

## 2. Building the Football Field — Worked Example

### Target Company: A Pakistani Pharmaceutical Company

**LTM Financials:**
- Revenue: PKR 20B
- EBITDA: PKR 4.8B
- Net Income: PKR 3.2B
- Net Debt: PKR 3.0B

**Results from each methodology:**

**Trading Comps (pharma peers, Pakistan + South Asia):**
- EV/EBITDA range: 9.0x – 12.0x
- Implied EV: PKR 43B – PKR 58B
- Less net debt: Implied Equity Value: **PKR 40B – PKR 55B**

**Precedent Transactions (pharma M&A, MENA + South Asia, last 7 years):**
- EV/EBITDA range: 11.0x – 15.0x (control premium embedded)
- Implied EV: PKR 53B – PKR 72B
- Less net debt: Implied Equity Value: **PKR 50B – PKR 69B**

**DCF (WACC 16–18%, terminal growth 6–8%):**
- Implied Enterprise Value range: PKR 48B – PKR 65B
- Less net debt: Implied Equity Value: **PKR 45B – PKR 62B**

**LBO Analysis (target IRR 20–25%, 5-year hold):**
- Maximum price a PE buyer could pay while hitting return targets
- Implied Equity Value: **PKR 38B – PKR 50B**

**Football Field Summary:**

| Methodology | Equity Value Low (PKR B) | Equity Value High (PKR B) |
|---|---|---|
| LBO Analysis | 38 | 50 |
| Trading Comps | 40 | 55 |
| DCF | 45 | 62 |
| Precedent Transactions | 50 | 69 |
| **Transaction price (proposed)** | **55** | **55** |

**Reading this football field:** The proposed transaction price of PKR 55B sits within the DCF range, at the 75th percentile of trading comps, and below the precedent transactions range. This is a reasonable deal price — not a steal, not an overpayment.

## 3. Weighting Methodologies

### No Universal Weights

Different deals call for different methodology emphasis:

| Deal Type | Primary Methodology | Secondary | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Public company takeover | Trading comps + Precedent transactions | DCF | Market prices are observable |
| Private company sale | DCF + Precedent transactions | Trading comps | No observable market price |
| PE acquisition (LBO) | LBO analysis + DCF | Trading comps | Return discipline drives price |
| Distressed sale | Liquidation value | DCF | Going concern questionable |
| Bank / FIG deal | P/BV precedent transactions | — | EV/EBITDA doesn't work for banks |
| E&P company | NAV | Precedent transactions | Reserve base drives value |

**Pakistan-specific weighting considerations:**
- Precedent transactions are limited in Pakistan's M&A market — few truly comparable deals exist
- DCF carries more weight but requires careful WACC calibration for Pakistan risk
- Trading comps are available for PSX-listed companies but peer sets are sometimes thin

### The Senior Banker's Judgment

Ultimately, the senior banker presents a "recommended range" — a subset of the full football field that represents the most defensible value range for the specific deal context. This recommended range is what goes into the board presentation and fairness opinion.

## 4. Fairness Opinions

### What a Fairness Opinion Is

A fairness opinion is a formal written opinion issued by an investment bank to a company's board of directors, stating that the financial consideration in a proposed transaction is fair from a financial point of view to the shareholders.

**Who gets one:** The target company's board (to protect directors from shareholder litigation alleging they sold too cheap), and sometimes the acquirer's board (for large deals where dilution to acquirer shareholders is a concern).

**Who issues it:** A bank that is advising on the deal (buy-side or sell-side advisor) or, for greater independence, a second bank hired specifically to render the fairness opinion.

### Structure of a Fairness Opinion

A typical fairness opinion letter contains:

1. **Engagement description:** Who retained the bank, for what purpose, the date of the opinion
2. **Information reviewed:** Financial statements, projections, deal documents, board materials, public information
3. **Assumptions and limitations:** The opinion is based on information as of a specific date; the bank does not independently verify financials; forward-looking projections are provided by management
4. **Methodology summary:** Brief description of valuation approaches applied (without full detail — full detail is in the "fairness opinion presentation" delivered to the board)
5. **Opinion statement:** "Based on and subject to the foregoing, it is our opinion that, as of the date hereof, the [Merger Consideration] is fair, from a financial point of view, to [the shareholders]"
6. **Limitations on use:** The opinion is for the board's use only; it may not be relied upon by third parties

### What Fairness Opinions Do NOT Do

- They do not say the deal is the best possible deal or the maximum achievable price
- They do not address the strategic merits of the transaction
- They are point-in-time (market conditions after the opinion date are irrelevant)
- They do not protect shareholders from losses if the deal later looks overpriced

## 5. IB Liability in Fairness Opinions

### The Liability Risk

Banks issuing fairness opinions face significant reputational and legal risk:
- Shareholders who believe the deal was mispriced can sue the directors — and name the bank's opinion as insufficient
- If the bank's analysis was sloppy, biased, or based on incorrect data, it faces legal liability
- Conflict of interest is a major issue: a bank advising the seller and issuing the fairness opinion has a financial incentive (completion fee) to opine the deal is fair

### Conflict of Interest Disclosure

Fairness opinions must disclose:
- The bank's advisory relationship and fees (especially contingent completion fees)
- Any other financial relationships between the bank and the parties
- Whether the bank provided financing to the acquirer

**Pakistan context:** SECP's M&A regulations require an independent financial advisor for PSX-listed company acquisitions. The advisor's opinion on consideration adequacy functions similarly to a Western fairness opinion, though the regulatory framework is less developed.

**Gulf context:** UAE SCA (Securities and Commodities Authority) and Saudi CMA require independent valuations for related-party transactions and delisting offers. The legal framework for fairness opinion liability is less litigious than the US but reputational risk remains.

## 6. Presenting Valuation to a Board

### Board Presentation Structure

The IB valuation presentation to a board typically covers:

1. **Executive summary:** Transaction overview, recommended range, opinion conclusion
2. **Company overview:** Business summary, recent financial performance
3. **Market overview:** Trading history (52-week range, volume), analyst coverage
4. **Trading comps:** Full comps table with methodology explanation
5. **Precedent transactions:** Deal table with methodology explanation
6. **DCF:** Key assumptions, sensitivity table, output
7. **Football field:** Visual summary across all methodologies
8. **Fairness conclusion:** The bank's opinion statement

**Board presentation tone:**
- Present analysis as a range of outcomes, not a single answer
- Explain which methodology the board should weight most heavily and why
- Acknowledge where the analysis is most sensitive to assumptions
- Be transparent about limitations (limited precedent transactions, Pakistan risk in DCF, etc.)

## Self-Assessment

1. You are advising on the acquisition of a Pakistani cement company. The buyer (a Gulf strategic acquirer) has offered PKR 48B equity value. You have completed the following analysis:
   - Trading comps (Pakistan cement peers): Equity Value PKR 35B – PKR 48B
   - Precedent transactions (regional cement M&A): Equity Value PKR 42B – PKR 58B
   - DCF (WACC 17%, terminal growth 6–8%): Equity Value PKR 40B – PKR 55B
   - LBO analysis: Not applicable (strategic buyer)
   - 52-week trading range (target is listed on PSX): PKR 30B – PKR 44B

   (a) Build the football field table. Where does the proposed offer price of PKR 48B sit relative to each methodology?
   (b) The target's board wants to know if they should accept or negotiate for more. Based solely on the football field, what would you advise?
   (c) The 52-week trading range implies a much lower value (PKR 30B–44B). The seller argues this should be excluded from the football field "because the market has been depressed." Evaluate this argument — when is excluding the trading range appropriate?
   (d) Which methodology should carry the most weight in this specific transaction? Justify your choice.

2. You are issuing a fairness opinion for the target company's board in a Pakistani privatization transaction (government selling a 51% stake in a state-owned FMCG company):

   (a) The selling bank (advising the government/seller) is also being asked to issue the fairness opinion. What conflict of interest exists, and how should it be managed?
   (b) The bank's fairness opinion will state the consideration is "fair from a financial point of view." A minority shareholder asks: "Does that mean it's the highest possible price?" Write the two-paragraph response explaining what the fairness opinion does and does not cover.
   (c) One of the precedent transactions you are relying on is from 2016 — Pakistan's economic conditions were significantly better. How would you handle this in the fairness opinion and in the board presentation?
   (d) After the fairness opinion is issued, the acquirer announces it will reduce workforce by 30%, significantly reducing the target's value to employees. Does this change the fairness opinion? Why or why not?
