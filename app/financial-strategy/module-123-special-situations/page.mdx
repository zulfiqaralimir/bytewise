# Module 123 — Special Situations: Spin-offs, Activism & Contested Deals

Special situations refers to a collection of non-standard M&A and corporate advisory scenarios where value is created or contested through atypical means — activist pressure, hostile bids, forced restructurings, or strategic corporate actions like spin-offs. These situations require a different skillset than standard M&A: part finance, part litigation strategy, part public relations. This module covers the major special situations scenarios.

## Learning Objectives

- Design and execute a spin-off or separation advisory
- Understand the activist investor toolkit and how boards respond
- Execute defense tactics against a hostile takeover
- Apply Pakistan SECP Takeover Regulations 2017 to contested acquisitions
- Analyze Gulf contested deal dynamics (limited hostile M&A history; family control norms)

---

## 1. Spin-Off Advisory

### Why Spin-Offs Are Special Situations

Spin-offs (covered structurally in Module 107) are "special situations" when they are contested, accelerated by activist pressure, or involve complex separation mechanics.

**Spin-off advisory workstreams:**
- **Separation analysis:** How entangled are the two businesses? Shared services (IT, HR, finance, legal) must be disentangled.
- **Standalone cost assessment:** What additional costs does the spun-off entity incur when no longer part of the parent? ("Stranded costs" at the parent; "new standalone costs" at the SpinCo)
- **Capital structure for SpinCo:** How much debt does SpinCo receive? What is its standalone credit profile?
- **Management team:** Who goes to SpinCo vs. staying at RemainCo?
- **Transition Service Agreements (TSAs):** Agreements for the parent to continue providing services to SpinCo for a defined period post-separation (typically 12–24 months)

## 2. Shareholder Activism

### The Activist Playbook

Activist investors (hedge funds with concentrated stakes) push for corporate changes to unlock shareholder value:

| Activist Demand | Logic |
|---|---|
| Spin-off a division | Conglomerate discount elimination |
| Return cash (buyback/dividend) | Company has excess cash earning low returns |
| Replace CEO/CFO | Management underperformance |
| Block a merger | Acquirer is overpaying |
| Force a sale of the whole company | Company is undervalued; strategic buyer will pay more |
| Cost reduction | Overhead is bloated |

**Activist campaigns in Gulf:**
Relatively rare due to family control structures — major Gulf companies have controlling family shareholders who own 40–60%+ and cannot be outvoted by an activist with 5–10%. Activism is more prevalent in companies with dispersed ownership.

**Pakistan activism:**
Minimal formal shareholder activism on PSX. The SECP minority shareholder rights framework provides some protection, but concentrated family ownership makes activist campaigns largely ineffective.

### How Boards Respond to Activists

1. **Engage:** Meet with the activist privately; understand their concerns; implement reasonable requests
2. **Communicate:** Strengthen investor relations; articulate the value creation plan clearly
3. **Defensive measures:** Where legally permissible, use defensive tactics
4. **Proxy fight:** Contested board election if activist tries to replace directors

## 3. Hostile Takeover

### Mechanics

A hostile takeover is an acquisition attempt that the target company's board has rejected, but the acquirer pursues directly with shareholders.

**Two routes:**
1. **Tender offer:** Acquirer makes a direct offer to shareholders to buy their shares at a premium (bypassing the board)
2. **Proxy contest:** Acquirer nominates alternative directors to the target's board; if they win the board seats, the new board approves the deal

### Defensive Measures

**Poison pill (shareholder rights plan):**
When any shareholder exceeds a threshold (e.g., 15%), existing shareholders (except the acquirer) can buy new shares at a 50% discount — massively diluting the acquirer's stake. This makes hostile acquisition prohibitively expensive.

**Staggered board:**
Directors serve 3-year staggered terms. Even if the acquirer wins a proxy vote, they can only replace 1/3 of the board per year — making hostile takeover slow and expensive.

**White knight:**
The target board invites a friendly acquirer to make a competing bid — the "white knight" beats out the hostile bidder.

**Pac-Man defense:**
The target launches a counteroffer to acquire the acquirer. Extremely rare.

### Pakistan Takeover Regulations

SECP's Listed Companies (Substantial Acquisition of Voting Shares and Takeovers) Regulations 2017:

**Mandatory offer trigger:** Any person acquiring 25% or more of voting shares must make a mandatory offer (MTO) to all remaining shareholders.

**MTO price:** The higher of:
- The offer price in the triggering transaction
- Highest price paid by the acquirer in the last 6 months
- 6-month VWAP of the target share on PSX

**Competitive bids:** A third party can make a competing offer within 21 days of the initial announcement.

**Irrevocability:** Once an MTO is announced, it cannot be withdrawn except with SECP permission.

**Squeeze-out:** If the acquirer reaches 90% ownership, they can compulsorily acquire remaining shares at the offer price.

## 4. Gulf Contested Deals

Gulf M&A is predominantly friendly — family businesses sell to other family businesses or institutional buyers by mutual agreement. Hostile bids are extremely rare because:
- Controlling family shareholders typically own supermajority stakes
- Cultural norms: hostile bids are seen as aggressive and relationship-damaging
- Regulatory complexity: SCA (UAE), CMA (Saudi) — regulators prefer orderly processes

**The closest Gulf equivalent:** Government-directed bank mergers (First Abu Dhabi Bank formation, National Commercial Bank + Samba) — technically "friendly" but effectively directed by Abu Dhabi and Saudi authorities with limited board discretion.

They are demanding: (a) spin-off of Lucky Cement's international operations into a separately listed entity, and (b) PKR 15B special dividend. (a) What defensive options are available to the board (consider UK Takeover Code restrictions)? (b) The acquirer escalates to a tender offer directly to shareholders. If 45% of shareholders tender, does the acquirer succeed? (c) The board invites a white knight. What should the white knight's offer include to successfully defeat the hostile bidder?
