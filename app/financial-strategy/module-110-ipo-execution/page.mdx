# Module 110 — IPO Execution

An IPO (Initial Public Offering) is the moment a private company becomes a public one — selling shares to institutional and retail investors for the first time. It is simultaneously a capital raise, a liquidity event for existing shareholders, and a fundamental transformation in how the company is governed and scrutinized. For investment bankers, executing an IPO is one of the most complex and high-profile assignments in ECM (Equity Capital Markets). This module covers the full IPO process from decision to first day of trading.

## Learning Objectives

- Assess IPO readiness and optimal timing
- Understand the 6–12 month IPO preparation process
- Draft and structure the prospectus
- Design the analyst research and roadshow process
- Execute bookbuilding, pricing, and allocation
- Manage post-IPO obligations and stabilization
- Apply IPO knowledge to PSX listings and Gulf exchange IPOs (Tadawul, DFM, ADX)

---

## 1. The IPO Decision

### Why Companies List

**Primary objectives:**
- **Capital raising:** Issue new shares to raise growth capital (primary offering)
- **Shareholder liquidity:** Existing shareholders (founders, PE investors) sell shares (secondary offering)
- **Currency for M&A:** Listed shares can be used as acquisition currency (stock deals)
- **Brand and credibility:** Listed status enhances corporate profile with customers, suppliers, and partners
- **Employee retention:** Listed stock options / share awards become more valuable and liquid

**Often both:** Most IPOs combine a primary component (company raises capital) and a secondary component (existing shareholders partially exit). The balance matters — a high secondary component (founders selling large stakes) signals lower confidence in future growth; a high primary component signals reinvestment.

### IPO Readiness Assessment

Before committing to an IPO, the company must assess readiness across dimensions:

| Dimension | IPO-Ready | Not IPO-Ready |
|---|---|---|
| Financial reporting | 3 years of audited IFRS financials | Unaudited or non-IFRS only |
| Corporate governance | Independent board, audit committee | Founder-controlled, no independent directors |
| Management team | Complete team, track record | Key man dependency, gaps at CFO/CEO |
| Business model | Proven, scalable, clear unit economics | Still in pilot or dependent on one contract |
| Market conditions | Bull market, sector in favor | Bear market, IPO window closed |
| Size | Sufficient market cap for institutional interest | Too small for institutional liquidity |

**Pakistan PSX IPO size threshold:** Generally need PKR 5B+ market cap to attract meaningful institutional investor interest. Smaller companies can list but liquidity will be thin.

**Gulf IPO minimum sizes:**
- Tadawul (Saudi): SAR 200M+ is typical minimum for institutional investor interest; NOMU platform allows smaller companies
- DFM/ADX (UAE): $50M+ free float for meaningful liquidity

## 2. Preparation Phase (6–12 Months)

### Selecting the IPO Team

**Global coordinators / bookrunners:**
The lead investment banks that run the entire IPO process: structuring, prospectus, research, roadshow, bookbuilding, allocation. For a Pakistani IPO: Arif Habib Limited, JS Global Capital, Next Capital, or a combination. For a Gulf IPO: Emirates NBD Capital, First Abu Dhabi Bank, Goldman Sachs, HSBC (for international tranche).

**Legal advisors:**
- Issuer's counsel: advises the company on disclosure, prospectus, regulatory compliance
- Underwriter's counsel: advises the banks (conflict of interest management, comfort letters)
- Pakistan: Haidermota, Vellani & Vellani, Mohsin Tayebaly & Co
- Gulf: Freshfields, Linklaters, Clifford Chance (international); Al Tamimi (regional)

**Auditors:**
- Big 4 preferred for institutional credibility
- Provide comfort letters (confirming financial statements are accurate)
- Issue auditor's report included in prospectus

**Reporting accountants:**
Prepare the "accountants' report" — a more detailed financial verification than a standard audit.

**PR / investor relations advisors:**
Manage media relations, investor communications, and analyst briefings.

### Financial Preparation

**Converting to IFRS:**
Most Pakistani private companies use local GAAP or partial IFRS compliance. IPO requires full IFRS compliance for at least the last 3 years.

**Audit quality upgrade:**
If the company was audited by a small local firm, upgrade to Big 4 for at least the last year (ideally 3 years for the prospectus financials).

**Financial systems:**
ERP systems, management reporting, budgeting processes must be investor-grade. Institutional investors expect quarterly management accounts within 20 working days of quarter end.

**Financial projections:**
The company prepares 3-year financial projections for internal use (included in the VDR for cornerstone investors; not in the public prospectus).

### Corporate Governance Overhaul

For PSX listing: SECP Code of Corporate Governance 2019 requirements:
- Minimum 1/3 independent directors on the board
- Audit committee (majority independent)
- HR & Remuneration Committee
- Risk Management Committee (for larger companies)
- Chief Financial Officer must be a qualified professional
- Company Secretary must be a qualified person

**Gulf governance:** Each exchange has its own corporate governance codes (DFM, ADX, Tadawul listing rules). Family-controlled companies listing in the Gulf typically retain voting control through different share classes.

## 3. The Prospectus

### What the Prospectus Is

The prospectus is the definitive legal document for an IPO. It is reviewed and approved by the relevant regulator (SECP for PSX; SCA for UAE; CMA for Saudi Arabia) before being distributed to investors.

**The prospectus is a legal document:** Every statement in it is a representation by the issuer and the underwriting banks. If the prospectus contains a material misstatement or omission, both the company and the banks face liability (civil and criminal in many jurisdictions).

### Prospectus Structure

**Part 1: Summary**
Key facts about the company, the offer, and the risk factors. Must be readable by retail investors.

**Part 2: Risk Factors**
Every material risk the company faces — industry, regulatory, operational, financial, country. Pakistan-listed companies typically have extensive risk factors covering:
- PKR exchange rate volatility
- Energy costs and circular debt
- Inflation and interest rate environment
- Political and regulatory risk
- Concentrated shareholder structure

**Part 3: Business Description**
Company history, products, markets, competitive position, strategy, operations.

**Part 4: Management's Discussion and Analysis (MD&A)**
Detailed analysis of the last 3 years' financial performance. Revenue drivers, margin analysis, capex, working capital. The most read section by institutional investors.

**Part 5: Financial Statements**
3 years of audited IFRS financial statements with notes.

**Part 6: Offer Structure**
Number of shares offered, offer price range, use of proceeds, selling shareholders, underwriting arrangements.

**Part 7: Regulatory Approvals and Listing Requirements**
Evidence of all required regulatory approvals, compliance with listing rules.

## 4. Analyst Research and the Roadshow

### Research Reports

**Pathfinder/pilot fishing analyst note:**
Before the formal roadshow, banks' equity research analysts publish a research note on the company. These notes are distributed to institutional investors to generate interest before the formal bookbuild.

**Research restrictions:**
- Analysts at underwriting banks cannot publish research during the "blackout period" before the IPO (to avoid market manipulation)
- Post-IPO: research analysts cannot recommend "sell" on a stock they underwrote (conflict of interest)

### The Management Roadshow

The CEO and CFO (typically) spend 2–3 weeks visiting institutional investors (fund managers, pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies) to present the investment case:

**Roadshow structure:**
- Group presentations: 10–20 investors in a room (hotel ballroom, roadshow venue)
- One-on-one meetings: 30–60 minute meetings with key institutional investors
- Videoconference meetings: for investors in geographies not visited in person

**Pakistan IPO roadshow:**
For PSX IPOs, the roadshow typically covers:
- Karachi (3–4 days: major institutional investors, mutual funds, insurance companies)
- Lahore (1–2 days)
- Islamabad (1 day)
- Dubai (1–2 days for Gulf-based Pakistani diaspora investors and regional funds)

**Gulf IPO roadshow:**
Adds London, New York, Singapore, and Hong Kong for international institutional investors.

**The management presentation ("investor day" deck):**
1. Company overview and investment highlights
2. Market opportunity (TAM, growth drivers)
3. Business model and competitive moat
4. Financial performance (last 3 years) and guidance
5. Use of IPO proceeds and growth strategy
6. Management team bios

## 5. Bookbuilding, Pricing, and Allocation

### The Bookbuilding Process

Bookbuilding is the process by which the IPO price is determined through demand from institutional investors:

1. **Price range is set:** Based on valuation analysis (comps, DCF), a price range is communicated (e.g., PKR 50–60 per share)
2. **Book opens:** Investors submit "indications of interest" — non-binding orders specifying price and quantity
3. **Book is monitored daily:** Bookrunners track demand at different price points
4. **Pricing decision:** At the close of bookbuilding, the bookrunners and the issuer decide the final offer price based on demand

**Oversubscription:**
A 5–10x oversubscribed book is healthy. 20x+ oversubscription (common for hot IPOs) is managed by pricing at the top of the range and rationing allocation.

**Undersubscription:**
If the book doesn't fill, the bookrunners may:
- Reduce the offer price (if within the range)
- Withdraw the IPO ("pulled" or "postponed")
- Place the remaining shares with cornerstone investors or underwriters

### Cornerstone and Anchor Investors

**Cornerstone investors (common in Asia/Gulf):**
Large institutional investors who commit to a specific number of shares at the IPO price before bookbuilding opens. Their participation is disclosed in the prospectus. Gives other investors confidence that large, credible investors have done due diligence and are committed.

**Anchor investors:**
Similar to cornerstone but the commitment is made just before the book opens; disclosed after pricing.

**Pakistan IPO institutional investors:**
- National Investment Trust (NIT)
- Mutual funds: JS Investments, MCB-Arif Habib, UBL Funds, Al Meezan
- Insurance companies: State Life, Jubilee Life, EFU
- Provident and pension funds

**Gulf IPO major institutional investors:**
- Saudi: PIF (Public Investment Fund), SABIC Pension Fund, General Organization for Social Insurance (GOSI)
- UAE: ADIA, Mubadala, Emirates Investment Authority, Abu Dhabi Pension Fund
- Qatar: QIA, Qatar Pension Fund

### Allocation

After pricing, shares are allocated to investors. Allocation decisions involve:

**Institutional allocation principles:**
- Quality investors: long-term holders > short-term flippers (hedge funds who flip on day 1)
- Geographic diversity: spread across domestic and international investors
- Sector specialists: investors with sector expertise who understand the business
- Relationship: banks reward investors who participate actively in their deals

**Retail allocation:**
Pakistani IPOs typically reserve 20–25% for retail investors (SECP requirement). Gulf exchanges also have retail allocation requirements.

**Pakistan retail subscription process:**
Retail investors subscribe through online portals (CDC: Central Depository Company), commercial banks, or the issuer's registrar. If oversubscribed, retail allotment is balloted.

## 6. Stabilization and Post-IPO Obligations

### Price Stabilization

After IPO, the share price often trades below the offer price in initial trading ("broken IPO"). Banks have a mechanism to support the price:

**Greenshoe option (over-allotment option):**
- Banks sell 15% more shares than the IPO size (the overallotment)
- If price falls below offer price: banks buy shares in the market to support price (using overallotment proceeds), then close the short position
- If price rises: banks exercise the greenshoe option (buy additional shares from the issuer at offer price), permanently increasing the IPO size by 15%

**Stabilization period:** Typically 30 days post-listing.

### Post-IPO Obligations

Listed companies face ongoing obligations:
- Quarterly financial reporting (PSX: within 30 days of quarter end)
- Annual report (PSX: within 120 days of fiscal year end)
- Continuous disclosure: any material information that could affect share price must be disclosed immediately
- Insider trading restrictions: directors and senior management cannot trade during blackout periods before earnings releases
- Related party transactions: require board approval and SECP disclosure
- Dividend policy communication

**Gulf post-IPO:**
Similar obligations under DFM/ADX/Tadawul listing rules. Saudi Arabia's CMA has some of the region's strictest continuous disclosure requirements.

## 7. Pakistan PSX IPO Context

### PSX Market Structure

Pakistan Stock Exchange (PSX) was formed in 2016 through the merger of the Karachi Stock Exchange, Lahore Stock Exchange, and Islamabad Stock Exchange. Key statistics (2024):
- Listed companies: ~550
- Market capitalization: ~$30–35B (varies with PKR/USD)
- Daily turnover: PKR 20–40B (varies)
- Foreign investor ownership: ~15–20% of free float

**PSX new listings (recent):**
Pakistan's IPO market is episodic — strong years (2017, 2021) followed by quieter periods. Sectors with recent/pending IPO activity: renewable energy, fintech, logistics, healthcare.

**SECP IPO approval process:**
Company submits draft prospectus to SECP → SECP review (typically 4–8 weeks) → SECP approval → prospectus published → subscription opens.

## Self-Assessment

1. A Pakistani pharmaceutical company is planning a PSX IPO. The company has PKR 18B revenue, PKR 4.5B EBITDA (25% margin), and three years of Big 4-audited IFRS financials:

   (a) The founding family currently owns 100% of the company. They want to sell 30% in the IPO (20% secondary — founders selling, 10% primary — company raising capital). A pension fund investor asks: "Why is 20% secondary? Does the family not believe in the future?" How should the management respond?
   (b) In the MD&A section of the prospectus, the CFO wants to include a statement: "We expect revenue to grow 20% in the next fiscal year." The lawyers advise against including this. Explain the legal risk and what alternative language could be used.
   (c) The bookbuilding process runs for 5 days. On day 3, the book is 2x covered at the bottom of the range (PKR 120/share) but only 0.5x covered at the top (PKR 140/share). What does this tell you about investor demand, and how would you advise the company on pricing?
   (d) Calculate the implied market cap at PKR 130/share if there are 200M total shares (including the new 10% primary shares). Is this IPO large enough to attract meaningful institutional investor interest on PSX?

2. A Saudi consumer goods company (Tadawul-listed) is planning to acquire a UAE competitor via a merger. The deal will be structured as a share exchange — Saudi company shareholders will receive Saudi company shares, UAE shareholders will receive Saudi company shares too (after the merger):

   (a) This is essentially a cross-border merger financed by Saudi shares. What are the two most complex regulatory approvals required and why?
   (b) The Saudi company's shares are trading at SAR 45. The UAE company's shares are trading at AED 18. The agreed exchange ratio is 0.42 Saudi shares per UAE share. Calculate the implied offer price in AED terms (SAR/AED rate: 1.02). Is there a premium or discount being offered?
   (c) Between announcement and closing (expected 6 months), the Saudi company's share price falls 15%. What happens to the implied offer value for UAE shareholders? How could this have been addressed in the deal structure?
   (d) Post-merger, the combined entity wants to list the shares on both Tadawul and DFM (dual listing). What are the key operational requirements for maintaining a dual listing, and what is the primary benefit to the combined company?
