# Module 100 — Discounted Cash Flow Valuation

DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) valuation is the foundational intrinsic value methodology in investment banking. The premise is elegant: a business is worth the present value of all the cash it will ever generate for its owners. The execution is where analysts earn their keep — every assumption in the model (revenue growth, margins, capex, WACC, terminal value) requires judgment, and small changes in assumptions can swing the valuation 20–40%.

## Learning Objectives

- Build a 5-year free cash flow forecast from a financial model
- Calculate WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital) using CAPM and Pakistan/Gulf market data
- Calculate terminal value using both the Gordon Growth Model and Exit Multiple Method
- Build a sensitivity analysis table (WACC vs. terminal growth rate)
- Interpret and present DCF output in an IB pitch book
- Understand the limitations and common manipulations of DCF

---

## 1. The DCF Framework

### The Core Equation

```
Intrinsic Value = Σ [FCF_t / (1 + WACC)^t]  +  Terminal Value / (1 + WACC)^n
```

Where:
- **FCF_t** = Free Cash Flow in year t
- **WACC** = Weighted Average Cost of Capital (the discount rate)
- **n** = length of the explicit forecast period (typically 5–10 years)
- **Terminal Value** = value of all cash flows beyond year n

**The two phases of DCF:**
1. **Explicit forecast period (years 1–5 or 1–10):** Detailed year-by-year projections of revenue, margins, capex, and working capital. You model each driver explicitly.
2. **Terminal value:** A single number capturing all value beyond the forecast period. Typically represents 60–80% of total DCF value — which is why terminal value assumptions are so important and so disputed.

### Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF)

DCF in IB typically uses FCFF — the cash available to both debt and equity holders — which is then discounted at WACC (which reflects the blend of debt and equity costs).

```
FCFF = EBIT × (1 − Tax Rate)
     + Depreciation & Amortization
     − Capital Expenditure
     − Change in Net Working Capital
```

Or equivalently:

```
FCFF = NOPAT + D&A − Capex − ΔNWC

NOPAT = Net Operating Profit After Tax = EBIT × (1 − t)
```

**Why use FCFF rather than net income?**
- Net income is affected by capital structure (interest expense)
- FCFF is capital-structure neutral — consistent with using WACC as the discount rate
- D&A is added back (non-cash); capex and WC changes are deducted (actual cash outflows)

## 2. Building the Explicit Forecast

### Revenue Forecasting

**Bottom-up approach (preferred):**
1. Volume growth: unit sales driven by market size and market share assumptions
2. Price growth: inflation pass-through, competitive pricing dynamics
3. Revenue = Volume × Price

**Top-down approach:**
1. Total addressable market (TAM) growth rate
2. Company's market share trajectory
3. Revenue = TAM × Market Share

**For Pakistani companies:**
Always model revenue in PKR. If the company has dollar-denominated revenue (exporters, dollar-linked contracts like IPPs), model in USD then convert at the projected exchange rate.

**Pakistan-specific revenue drivers:**
- Inflation: Pakistan's CPI has averaged 15–25% in 2022–2024; FMCG and services companies can pass through inflation to pricing
- Population growth: ~2% annually, relevant for consumer staples
- Currency depreciation: PKR/USD has moved from ~160 to ~280 over 2020–2024; exporter revenue benefits, importer costs rise

### Margin Forecasting

**EBITDA margin expansion/contraction drivers:**
| Factor | Drives Margin Up | Drives Margin Down |
|---|---|---|
| Scale | Fixed costs spread over more revenue | — |
| Input costs | Falling commodity prices | Rising coal, energy prices |
| Pricing power | Pass-through pricing | Competitive pressure |
| Operational efficiency | Process improvement | New capacity ramp-up costs |
| Currency | For exporters: PKR depreciation | For importers: PKR depreciation |

**Pakistan margin pitfall:** Energy costs (gas, electricity, furnace oil) are a major COGS component for manufacturers. Include NEPRA tariff assumptions and circular debt risk explicitly.

### Capex and D&A

**Maintenance capex:** The minimum capex to keep existing assets running. Approximated as: Depreciation × (1 + inflation).

**Growth capex:** Investment in new capacity. Model explicitly for announced projects; use a % of incremental revenue as a proxy for unannounced expansion.

**D&A:** Typically modeled as a % of gross fixed assets, or from an asset schedule if you have the asset base.

### Working Capital Changes

```
Change in NWC = NWC_t − NWC_{t−1}
```

A growing business typically has increasing NWC (more receivables, more inventory) → negative impact on FCF.

**Modeling approach:** Hold DSO, DIO, and DPO constant at normalized levels. Apply to growing revenue and COGS to project WC balances, then calculate the annual change.

## 3. WACC Calculation

### The WACC Formula

```
WACC = (E/V) × Ke  +  (D/V) × Kd × (1 − t)
```

Where:
- E = market value of equity
- D = market value of debt
- V = E + D (total capital)
- Ke = cost of equity
- Kd = cost of debt (pre-tax)
- t = marginal tax rate

### Cost of Equity: CAPM

```
Ke = Rf  +  β × (Rm − Rf)  +  CRP
```

**Risk-free rate (Rf):**
- Use the long-term government bond yield in the same currency as the cash flows
- Pakistan (PKR model): Pakistan 10-year PIB yield (~12–14% in 2024 market)
- Gulf (USD model): US 10-year Treasury yield (~4.0–4.5% in 2024)
- Note: Pakistan's Rf is high because it already incorporates country risk in the government bond yield

**Beta (β):**
- Measures the stock's sensitivity to market movements
- β = 1.0: moves exactly with the market
- β &gt; 1.0: more volatile than market (cyclical companies)
- β &lt; 1.0: less volatile (utilities, staples)
- **If target is private:** Use average unlevered beta of public comparables, then re-lever for the target's capital structure

**Unlevering and relevering beta:**
```
β_unlevered = β_levered / [1 + (1 − t) × (D/E)]
β_relevered = β_unlevered × [1 + (1 − t) × (D/E)_target]
```

**Equity risk premium (ERP or Rm − Rf):**
- Historical long-run premium of equities over risk-free rate
- Pakistan: Damodaran estimates ~7.5–8.5% for Pakistan (2024)
- UAE: ~5.5–6.5%
- Saudi Arabia: ~5.5–7.0%

**Country risk premium (CRP):**
- Additional premium for investing in an emerging market vs. a developed market
- Pakistan CRP: ~4–6% (based on CDS spreads and Damodaran sovereign default spread method)
- UAE/Saudi CRP: ~1–2% (investment grade sovereigns, strong balance sheets)
- **If using Pakistani government bond as Rf, CRP is already embedded — do not double-count**

### Cost of Debt (Kd)

- For publicly traded bonds: yield-to-maturity on the company's outstanding debt
- For bank loans: the weighted average interest rate on drawn credit facilities
- For private companies: reference lending rates (KIBOR + spread for Pakistan; SOFR + spread for USD)

**Pakistan cost of debt context:**
KIBOR 3-month was ~22% in early 2024 before SBP rate cuts began. A typical large Pakistani manufacturing company might borrow at KIBOR + 1.5–2.5%, giving a Kd of ~20–22% at peak rates. As of mid-2025, with rate cuts, this has declined meaningfully.

### Tax Rate

Use the company's effective marginal tax rate. Pakistan corporate tax: 29% standard; 35% for banking companies; concessional rates for export-oriented sectors.

### Sample WACC Calculation — Pakistani Industrial Company

**Inputs:**
| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Risk-free rate (Rf) | 12.0% | Pakistan 10-year PIB yield |
| Equity beta | 0.95 | PSX peer group average, relevered |
| Equity risk premium | 8.0% | Pakistan EM premium |
| Country risk premium | 0% | Already in Rf (PIB yield) |
| Cost of equity (Ke) | 19.6% | = 12.0% + 0.95 × 8.0% |
| Pre-tax cost of debt (Kd) | 18.5% | KIBOR 3M + 2.5% spread |
| Tax rate | 29% | Standard Pakistan corporate tax |
| After-tax Kd | 13.1% | = 18.5% × (1 − 29%) |
| Debt / (Debt + Equity) | 35% | Target capital structure |
| Equity / (Debt + Equity) | 65% | Target capital structure |
| **WACC** | **17.3%** | = 65% × 19.6% + 35% × 13.1% |

## 4. Terminal Value

### Method 1: Gordon Growth Model (Perpetuity Growth)

```
Terminal Value = FCF_{n+1} / (WACC − g)
```

Where:
- FCF_{n+1} = free cash flow in the first year beyond the forecast period
- g = perpetual growth rate

**Choosing the growth rate (g):**
- g must be less than WACC (otherwise the formula breaks — infinite value)
- g should approximate the long-run nominal GDP growth of the economy
- Pakistan: nominal GDP growth ~10–14% (real ~3–4% + inflation ~8–10%) — but terminal growth in a DCF is often capped lower to be conservative
- Typical range for Pakistan PKR model: 6–10%
- For USD-denominated models: 2–4% (US/global nominal GDP)

**Terminal value as % of total DCF:** In Pakistan high-WACC environments, the terminal value is a smaller % of total value because future cash flows are discounted more heavily. In low-rate USD models, terminal value can be 75–85% of total value.

### Method 2: Exit Multiple Method

```
Terminal Value = EBITDA_n × Exit Multiple
```

Where:
- EBITDA_n = EBITDA in the last year of the forecast
- Exit Multiple = the EV/EBITDA multiple at which the business will be sold or valued at end of forecast

**Choosing the exit multiple:**
- Use the current trading comps range (or a slight discount for conservatism)
- This method anchors the terminal value to market pricing — which is why IB practitioners often prefer it
- Drawback: it's circular (uses market multiples to determine value, which the comps analysis already did)

**Implied terminal growth rate check:**
After using Exit Multiple method, always solve for the implied g:
```
Implied g = WACC − [FCF_{n+1} / Terminal Value]
```
If the implied g is unreasonably high (e.g., 15% for a Pakistan company), the exit multiple is too generous.

## 5. Worked Example: Cherat Cement DCF

### Assumptions

| Item | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 | Year 4 | Year 5 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Revenue (PKR B) | 30.0 | 33.0 | 36.3 | 38.1 | 39.9 |
| Revenue growth | — | +10% | +10% | +5% | +5% |
| EBITDA margin | 25% | 26% | 27% | 27% | 28% |
| EBITDA (PKR B) | 7.5 | 8.6 | 9.8 | 10.3 | 11.2 |
| D&A (PKR B) | 1.2 | 1.3 | 1.4 | 1.5 | 1.6 |
| EBIT (PKR B) | 6.3 | 7.3 | 8.4 | 8.8 | 9.6 |
| Tax (29%) | 1.8 | 2.1 | 2.4 | 2.6 | 2.8 |
| NOPAT (PKR B) | 4.5 | 5.2 | 6.0 | 6.2 | 6.8 |
| + D&A | 1.2 | 1.3 | 1.4 | 1.5 | 1.6 |
| − Capex | 2.5 | 2.5 | 2.0 | 1.8 | 1.8 |
| − ΔNWC | 0.3 | 0.4 | 0.4 | 0.2 | 0.2 |
| **FCFF (PKR B)** | **2.9** | **3.6** | **5.0** | **5.7** | **6.4** |

**WACC: 17.3%** (from sample calculation above)

### Discounting FCF

| Year | FCFF (PKR B) | Discount Factor (1/1.173^t) | PV (PKR B) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 2.9 | 0.853 | 2.5 |
| 2 | 3.6 | 0.727 | 2.6 |
| 3 | 5.0 | 0.620 | 3.1 |
| 4 | 5.7 | 0.529 | 3.0 |
| 5 | 6.4 | 0.451 | 2.9 |
| **Sum PV of FCF** | | | **14.1** |

### Terminal Value (Gordon Growth, g = 7%)

```
FCF_{Year 6} = 6.4 × 1.07 = PKR 6.8B
Terminal Value = 6.8 / (17.3% − 7.0%) = 6.8 / 10.3% = PKR 66.0B
PV of Terminal Value = 66.0 × 0.451 = PKR 29.8B
```

### Enterprise Value

```
PV of FCF (years 1–5):  PKR 14.1B
PV of Terminal Value:   PKR 29.8B
Enterprise Value:       PKR 43.9B

Less: Net Debt:         PKR 6B
Equity Value:           PKR 37.9B
```

**Cross-check against comps:** Module 98 showed Cherat trading at 4.9x EV/EBITDA. At LTM EBITDA of PKR 7B (approximately), that's PKR 34B EV — close to the DCF result of PKR 44B, suggesting the DCF assumptions imply modest growth and margin improvement above current run-rate.

## 6. Sensitivity Analysis

A single-point DCF output is misleading — the true output is a range driven by key assumption uncertainty. Standard IB sensitivity: WACC (±1–2%) vs. terminal growth rate (±1–2%).

### WACC vs. Terminal Growth Rate — Enterprise Value (PKR B)

| | g = 5% | g = 6% | g = 7% | g = 8% | g = 9% |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| WACC = 15.3% | 46.2 | 51.8 | 59.4 | 70.3 | 87.2 |
| WACC = 16.3% | 41.5 | 45.6 | 51.1 | 58.9 | 70.4 |
| **WACC = 17.3%** | **37.7** | **40.8** | **43.9** | **48.5** | **55.7** |
| WACC = 18.3% | 34.5 | 37.1 | 39.9 | 43.5 | 48.8 |
| WACC = 19.3% | 31.8 | 34.0 | 36.3 | 39.2 | 43.4 |

**Reading the table:** The base case (WACC 17.3%, g 7%) gives EV PKR 43.9B. The range across reasonable assumptions is PKR 34–56B. This is the valuation range you would present to the client.

## 7. DCF Limitations and Common Manipulations

### Legitimate Limitations

- **Garbage in, garbage out:** DCF is only as good as the underlying projections. Optimistic revenue and margin forecasts (especially in management projections) will always give a high DCF value.
- **Terminal value dominance:** In many models, terminal value is 60–80% of total value. A 1% change in terminal growth rate swings the output significantly.
- **WACC sensitivity:** A 1% change in WACC changes enterprise value by 5–15% — WACC inputs (beta, ERP, CRP) involve significant estimation uncertainty.
- **Ignores optionality:** DCF captures committed cash flows well but misses option value in early-stage or resource companies.

### Common Manipulations to Watch For

- **Management projections are always optimistic:** Sellside IB mandates typically use management's own projections (or management-guided forecasts). These are almost always optimistic. An experienced buyer always haircuts them.
- **Running terminal growth rate close to WACC:** Any g within 2% of WACC will generate astronomical terminal value. Flag this immediately.
- **Using NTM EBITDA as the terminal value base:** If the last forecast year EBITDA is unusually high (step-change in profitability), using it for terminal value inflates value.
- **Selectively excluding capex:** Some analysts model "normalized" capex well below actual capex to boost FCF. Check capex/D&A ratio — it should be close to 1.0 for a steady-state business.

   (a) Calculate FCFF for each of the 5 years. Show your workings clearly.
