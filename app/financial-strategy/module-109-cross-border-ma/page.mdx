# Module 109 — Cross-Border M&A & Emerging Market Deals

Cross-border M&A layers additional complexity on top of domestic deal execution. When a UAE company acquires a Pakistani business, or a Western PE fund buys a Gulf asset, the deal involves multiple legal systems, currencies, regulators, and cultural expectations. Mastering cross-border M&A is a competitive advantage — most dealmakers are competent at domestic transactions; far fewer can execute across borders reliably. This module covers the unique dimensions of cross-border and emerging market M&A.

## Learning Objectives

- Identify the key additional risk dimensions in cross-border M&A
- Structure cross-border deals to minimize tax friction and regulatory risk
- Apply emerging market valuation adjustments (country risk, currency, illiquidity)
- Understand Pakistan's inbound and outbound M&A framework
- Navigate Gulf deal culture and relationship-based dealmaking
- Analyze CPEC-related M&A and Chinese investment in Pakistan

---

## 1. Cross-Border M&A Risk Dimensions

### The Seven Complexity Layers

**1. Currency risk:**
When the buyer's reference currency (e.g., USD, AED) differs from the target's operating currency (PKR, EGP, INR), the buyer faces:
- Transaction risk: PKR depreciates between announcement and closing → equity value in USD falls
- Translation risk: post-acquisition, PKR earnings translate to fewer USD in consolidated accounts
- Economic risk: PKR depreciation may change the competitive position of the target's business

**Hedging options:**
- Forward FX contracts (limited availability for PKR beyond 6 months)
- Cross-currency swaps
- Natural hedge: finance the acquisition in PKR (if possible) to match currency of cash flows

**2. Regulatory and legal complexity:**
- Multiple competition authority filings (Pakistan CCP + home country + any other jurisdiction)
- Foreign investment restrictions in sensitive sectors (media, banking, telecoms, defense)
- Different legal systems (common law in Pakistan/UAE/UK; civil law in continental Europe)
- Employment law differences (redundancy rights, union requirements)

**3. Tax structuring:**
Cross-border M&A requires a holding structure that minimizes withholding tax on dividends, interest, and capital gains. The holding company jurisdiction matters enormously.

**4. Political and sovereign risk:**
- Currency controls: can the buyer repatriate dividends and proceeds from Pakistan?
- Regulatory reversal: can the target's regulatory approvals be revoked?
- Political stability: will a change of government affect the deal?
- Conflict risk: escalation of regional conflicts

**5. Cultural integration:**
Different management cultures, decision-making styles, communication norms. Gulf companies acquiring Pakistani businesses must navigate the Pakistani workforce's expectations and the founder's role post-acquisition.

**6. Accounting and reporting standards:**
- Pakistan companies: IFRS-compliant (for listed companies); private companies may use IAS or local GAAP
- Converting to acquirer's GAAP/reporting format is a significant integration task
- Auditor changes post-acquisition (acquirer's auditor may not be in Pakistan)

**7. Corruption and compliance risk:**
Pakistan and many EM countries score lower on Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index. Acquirers must conduct anti-corruption due diligence (Foreign Corrupt Practices Act for US acquirers; UK Bribery Act for UK acquirers; broadly applicable for all multinationals).

## 2. Holding Company Structures for Cross-Border M&A

### Why the Holding Structure Matters

The holding company jurisdiction determines:
- Withholding tax on dividends flowing up to the acquirer
- Capital gains tax on eventual exit
- Access to double tax treaties (DTTs)
- Ease of repatriation

**Typical cross-border M&A structure:**

```
Acquirer (UAE/UK/Singapore HoldCo)
         |
Intermediate HoldCo (Mauritius, Netherlands, Singapore)
         |
Pakistan Operating Company
```

**Why Mauritius is popular for South Asia:**
Pakistan-Mauritius DTT: 10% withholding on dividends (vs. 15% domestic rate). Capital gains on shares in Pakistani companies held through Mauritius may be taxed in Mauritius at 0% (Mauritius does not tax capital gains).

**DIFC structures for Gulf investors:**
Dubai International Financial Centre (DIFC) entities offer:
- No withholding tax on dividends
- Common law legal framework
- Recognition in major jurisdictions
- Used widely by Gulf-based PE funds and SWFs investing regionally

**Pakistan's DTT network:**
Pakistan has double tax treaties with: UK, USA, China, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Turkey, Mauritius, Netherlands, and 40+ other countries. Each DTT provides different withholding rates for dividends, interest, and royalties.

## 3. Emerging Market Valuation Adjustments

### Why Standard DCF Underestimates EM Risk

A standard DCF with a WACC of 12% (appropriate for a US company) cannot be applied to a Pakistani company — it ignores the additional risks:

**Explicit EM risk adjustments:**

**1. Country risk premium (CRP):**
Damodaran method: CRP = Default Spread × (Equity Volatility / Bond Volatility)
- Pakistan 2024: CRP ~4–6%
- Adding to CAPM: Ke = Rf + β(ERP) + CRP

**2. Currency risk in USD-denominated DCF:**
If building a USD DCF for a PKR-earning company:
- Convert PKR cash flows to USD at projected exchange rates
- Use USD WACC (lower Rf, US ERP) — but this ignores PKR-specific risk
- Alternative: keep model in PKR with PKR WACC; present USD equivalent using spot rate

**3. Illiquidity premium:**
Private companies in Pakistan have limited exit options:
- PSX is smaller and less liquid than developed market exchanges
- PE exits: limited secondary PE buyers; IPO market for non-large-cap companies is thin
- Add 2–4% illiquidity premium to discount rate for private company Pakistani assets

**4. Political risk discount:**
For highly politically sensitive businesses (utilities, media, government-contracted businesses), add a specific political risk premium reflecting probability and impact of regulatory reversal.

**5. Scenario analysis:**
Rather than a single-point DCF, EM valuations should include explicit scenarios:
- Base case: current macro trajectory continues
- Bear case: currency crisis (PKR to 400/USD), elevated interest rates
- Bull case: IMF program success, ratings upgrade, capital inflows

## 4. Pakistan Inbound M&A

### Regulatory Framework for Foreign Acquisition

**Board of Investment (BOI):**
Pakistan welcomes FDI in most sectors under the Investment Policy 2013 (updated periodically). 100% foreign ownership is allowed in most manufacturing and services sectors.

**Restricted/prohibited sectors:**
- Arms and munitions (prohibited for foreigners)
- Print media (maximum 25% foreign ownership)
- Broadcasting media (subject to PEMRA approval)
- Banks and DFIs: SBP approval required; maximum 49% foreign ownership in individual bank without SBP special approval

**Repatriation guarantee:**
Under Pakistan's investment policy, foreign investors can repatriate:
- Capital (at prevailing exchange rate at time of repatriation)
- Dividends and profits
- Capital gains

In practice, SBP approval is required for large repatriations; PKR convertibility has been constrained during balance-of-payments crises (2022–2023).

### Pakistan Inbound M&A Deal Flow

**Historical inbound transactions:**
- FrieslandCampina acquired Engro Foods (2016) — $452M, largest Pakistan food M&A
- Careem acquired by Uber (technically Uber acquired a UAE company operating in Pakistan) — $3.1B (2019)
- Abraaj Group invested in K-Electric — ultimately collapsed in 2018 fraud scandal
- KEPCO (Korea) and other Chinese companies in power sector (CPEC)
- International E&P companies (ENI, OMV, MOL) in Pakistan oil & gas

**Gulf inbound M&A themes:**
Gulf investors (particularly UAE and Saudi) are among the most active acquirers in Pakistan:
- Financial services: ENBD, Al Rajhi Bank, Meezan (Aga Khan Development Network)
- Consumer: Pakistan's young, growing population attracts Gulf FMCG companies
- Real estate: Dubai-style residential development projects
- Infrastructure: Port of Karachi, logistics

## 5. CPEC-Related M&A

### CPEC Overview

China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) is a $62B+ infrastructure program:
- Power plants (coal, hydro, solar) — most CPEC phase 1 investment
- Road and rail infrastructure
- Gwadar Port development
- Special Economic Zones (SEZs)

### M&A Dimensions of CPEC

**Power sector transactions:**
Chinese companies (CMEC, PowerChina, Huaneng) own equity in Pakistan IPPs under CPEC. These stakes are periodically:
- Refinanced (new debt at lower rates as Pakistan credit improves)
- Partially sold to financial investors seeking stable infrastructure returns
- Transferred between Chinese entities as corporate restructurings occur

**Gwadar-related acquisitions:**
China Overseas Port Holdings (COPH) operates Gwadar Port and has an SEZ. Pakistani and international companies establishing in the SEZ may involve M&A.

**Due diligence challenges for CPEC assets:**
- Chinese company financial transparency is limited
- Power purchase agreements with WAPDA/CPPA-G are complex and exposed to circular debt
- Sovereign guarantee documentation requires specialist legal review
- Repatriation of proceeds to China has its own SAFE (State Administration of Foreign Exchange) implications

## 6. Gulf Deal Culture and Relationship Dynamics

### How Gulf M&A Differs

**Relationship first:**
In the Gulf, significant deals are initiated and progressed through personal relationships — between chairmen, CEOs, or well-connected intermediaries. An IB pitch to a CFO who doesn't know you may go nowhere. A chairman-level introduction creates an entirely different dynamic.

**Family office dynamics:**
Many Gulf acquirers are family offices or family-controlled conglomerates (Al Futtaim, Olayan, Alshaya, Majid Al Futtaim). These buyers have:
- Long investment horizons (they don't need to exit in 5 years like PE)
- Relationship-based due diligence (trust the people as much as the numbers)
- Board governance that may require family patriarch approval regardless of the formal process

**Ramadan and holiday timing:**
Gulf M&A processes slow significantly during Ramadan, Eid al-Fitr, and Eid al-Adha. National Day holidays in UAE (December 2–3) and Saudi Arabia (September 23) also slow activity. Factor these into process timelines.

**Negotiation style:**
Gulf negotiators tend to be:
- Direct on major commercial terms once a relationship is established
- Less aggressive on legal documentation than US/UK counterparties
- Willing to move quickly once a decision is made at the top
- Relationship reputation is important — don't table extreme positions that damage trust

## Self-Assessment

1. A Dutch multinational consumer goods company is acquiring a Pakistani FMCG company for EUR 200M (approximately PKR 60B):

   (a) List the five most significant cross-border risk dimensions for this specific transaction. For each, describe one specific mitigation the acquirer should take.
   (b) The acquirer wants to repatriate dividends annually after closing. Design the optimal holding structure (HoldCo jurisdiction) to minimize withholding tax. Which Pakistan-Mauritius DTT provisions are relevant?
   (c) The PKR has depreciated 40% against the EUR over the last 3 years. How should the acquirer build this currency risk into the valuation? Calculate the impact: if PKR depreciates a further 20% before closing, what is the EUR-equivalent purchase price (original: EUR 200M at PKR 300/EUR)?
   (d) Due diligence reveals the target company paid PKR 25M in "facilitation payments" to government officials to obtain a distribution license 3 years ago. What are the legal implications for the Dutch acquirer under the UK Bribery Act (applicable since the acquirer has UK operations)? What should the buyer demand in the SPA?

2. A Saudi FMCG conglomerate wants to acquire a Pakistani food company. The deal is being pitched by the Pakistani IB firm's managing director, who has a personal relationship with the Saudi company's CEO:

   (a) The Pakistani banker's teaser was sent to the Saudi CEO directly (bypassing the Saudi CFO and corporate development team). A Western-trained analyst on the Pakistani team says this is "unprofessional — we should go through proper channels." Evaluate this view in the context of Gulf deal culture.
   (b) The process is in second round. The Saudi team visits Karachi for management presentations during Ramadan. The CEO of the Pakistani target is fasting and proposes to hold the meeting after iftar (8:30 PM). How should the IB banker manage this situation?
   (c) The Saudi buyer's legal team (a Saudi law firm) is slow to mark up the SPA and has proposed that Saudi law governs the agreement. The Pakistani seller's lawyers insist on English law. What is the commercial rationale for each position? What is the market standard governing law for Pakistan M&A transactions with Gulf buyers?
   (d) The Saudi buyer's chairman has verbally agreed to the deal price but the formal offer hasn't been submitted. The IB banker receives a competing bid from a UAE buyer 20% higher. What should the banker advise the Pakistani seller to do?
