# Module 102 — Sum-of-Parts & NAV Valuation

When a company operates multiple distinct businesses — a fertilizer division, a food company, a power plant, and a real estate arm — no single valuation multiple can capture the whole picture. Sum-of-Parts (SOTP) valuation values each business segment separately, then adds them up. It is the correct methodology for conglomerates, holding companies, diversified industrials, real estate companies, and E&P firms. This module covers SOTP construction, NAV methodology for asset-heavy businesses, and the holding company discount phenomenon.

## Learning Objectives

- Identify when SOTP is the appropriate valuation methodology
- Value each business segment using the correct methodology for that segment
- Calculate Net Asset Value (NAV) for real estate and E&P companies
- Apply and explain the holding company discount
- Build a full SOTP for Engro Corporation (Pakistan's largest listed conglomerate)

---

## 1. When to Use SOTP

SOTP is appropriate when:
- The company has multiple business segments with different growth profiles, margins, and risk levels
- Individual segments would trade at materially different multiples if listed separately
- The company has significant non-operating assets (listed equity stakes, investment property)
- A single-multiple approach would obscure value (e.g., a pharma company with a mature generic drugs division and a high-growth biotech division)

**Single-multiple approaches fail for conglomerates** because applying one EV/EBITDA multiple averages across segments that deserve completely different multiples. A 7x EBITDA multiple applied to Engro Corporation ignores that its petrochemicals business might deserve 5x while its food business deserves 12x.

---

## 2. SOTP Methodology

### Step 1 — Identify and Separate Segments

Define the distinct business segments:
- Use management's segment reporting (IFRS 8 requires segment disclosure)
- Further sub-divide if two businesses within one reported segment have different economics
- Separate out: operating businesses, financial assets, listed equity stakes, real estate holdings, net debt

### Step 2 — Assign a Valuation Methodology to Each Segment

| Segment Type | Preferred Methodology |
|---|---|
| Profitable operating business (FMCG, industrial) | EV/EBITDA comps |
| High-growth business | DCF or EV/Revenue |
| Financial institution (bank, insurance) | P/BV or P/E comps |
| Real estate | NAV (cap rate or DCF of rental income) |
| E&P / natural resources | NAV (2P reserve DCF) |
| Listed equity stake | Market value × shares held |
| Unlisted minority stake | Cost or recent transaction price |
| Net debt | Book value (current balance sheet) |

### Step 3 — Value Each Segment

Apply segment-level EBITDA (or revenue, book value, NAV) × selected multiple from relevant comparables.

**Key discipline:** Use comparables specific to each segment's industry — not the parent company's overall multiple.

### Step 4 — Sum the Parts and Apply Conglomerate Discount

```
SOTP Enterprise Value = Σ (Segment EV or Value)
Less: Net Debt (at parent + subsidiary level)
= SOTP Equity Value

Holding Company Discount Applied: 10–25%
= Adjusted Equity Value
```

## 3. Holding Company Discount

### Why Holding Companies Trade at a Discount

Listed holding companies and conglomerates almost universally trade at a discount to the sum of their parts. This discount reflects:

1. **Conglomerate complexity premium:** Investors prefer pure-play businesses they can analyse simply. Complexity is penalized.
2. **Management cost:** The holding company headquarters is an overhead cost on top of subsidiary management.
3. **Capital allocation risk:** Investors worry that management will make value-destroying acquisitions with subsidiary dividends.
4. **Illiquidity of unlisted stakes:** Private subsidiary stakes are worth less than their theoretical value because they can't be sold instantly.
5. **Double leverage:** If the holding company has debt and subsidiaries also have debt, the equity at the top is riskier.

**Typical holding company discount range:**
- Transparent, well-governed conglomerate: 10–15%
- Complex, opaque structure: 20–30%
- Deeply complex with related-party concerns: 30–40%

**Pakistan context:** Most PSX-listed conglomerates (Engro, Lucky Group, Nishat Group, Dawood Hercules) trade at holding company discounts of 15–30%. The discount is larger in Pakistan due to lower governance standards and related-party transaction concerns.

## 4. NAV Methodology

### NAV for Real Estate Companies

```
NAV = Market Value of Property Portfolio
    + Other Assets (cash, receivables)
    − Liabilities (debt, payables)
    = Net Asset Value
```

**Valuing the property portfolio:**
- Direct capitalization: Property Value = Annual Net Operating Income / Cap Rate
- DCF of rental cash flows: Discount projected rents + terminal value at a real estate WACC

**Cap rate benchmarks (Pakistan):**
| Property Type | Cap Rate Range |
|---|---|
| Prime Karachi/Lahore commercial | 6–8% |
| Industrial / warehouse | 8–10% |
| Retail mall | 7–9% |
| Residential | 4–6% |

**Gulf cap rates are lower** (Dubai Grade A office: 5–6%) reflecting lower country risk and deeper institutional investor base.

### NAV for E&P Companies

Oil and gas companies are valued on the present value of their proven (1P) and probable (2P) reserves:

```
NAV = PV10 of 2P Reserves (discounted at 10%)
    + Exploration upside (risked)
    − Net Debt
    = NAV per share
```

**PV10:** Industry convention to discount E&P cash flows at 10% regardless of company WACC — enables comparison across companies.

**Pakistan E&P NAV context:**
OGDCL and PPL publish reserve reports; analysts build field-by-field models with production decline curves, operating costs, and realized price assumptions. Key variables: Brent price, PKR/USD rate, government take (royalties + windfall levy), and production decline rates.

## 5. Worked Example: Engro Corporation SOTP

Engro Corporation is Pakistan's largest listed conglomerate with businesses spanning fertilizers, petrochemicals, food, power, and digital/services.

### Segment Overview

| Segment | Business | Engro Ownership | Listed? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Engro Fertilizers (EFERT) | Urea fertilizer manufacturer | ~56% | Yes (PSX) |
| Engro Polymer & Chemicals (EPCL) | PVC and caustic soda | ~56% | Yes (PSX) |
| Engro Foods | Dairy, ice cream (Olper's, Omore) | ~100%* | No |
| Engro Elengy Terminal | LNG regasification | ~56% | No |
| Engro Powergen Qadirpur | Gas-based power plant | ~100% | No |
| Corporate (HoldCo) | Parent overhead, net debt | — | — |

*Note: Engro Foods was majority sold to FrieslandCampina in 2016; Engro retained a minority stake. This is illustrative of the structure.

### SOTP Valuation (Illustrative, PKR Billions)

**Engro Fertilizers (EFERT):**
- LTM EBITDA: PKR 35B
- Peer multiple (fertilizer sector): 6.5x EV/EBITDA
- Segment EV: PKR 228B
- Engro's 56% share: PKR 128B

**Engro Polymer & Chemicals (EPCL):**
- LTM EBITDA: PKR 8B
- Peer multiple (petrochemicals, Pakistan): 5.5x EV/EBITDA
- Segment EV: PKR 44B
- Engro's 56% share: PKR 25B

**LNG Terminal (Elengy):**
- Contracted cash flows (take-or-pay): DCF value ~PKR 18B
- Engro's 56% share: PKR 10B

**Power plant (Qadirpur):**
- Take-or-pay power purchase agreement: DCF value ~PKR 8B
- Engro's 100% share: PKR 8B

**Corporate net debt:**
- HoldCo net debt: (PKR 12B)

### SOTP Summary

| Segment | Engro's Share (PKR B) |
|---|---|
| Engro Fertilizers | 128 |
| Engro Polymer | 25 |
| LNG Terminal | 10 |
| Power | 8 |
| Corporate net debt | (12) |
| **SOTP Equity Value (undiscounted)** | **159** |
| Holding company discount (20%) | (32) |
| **SOTP Equity Value (adjusted)** | **127** |

**Cross-check against market cap:** If Engro Corporation's market cap is PKR 100–110B, the stock is trading at a ~15–20% discount to SOTP — consistent with the typical Pakistan conglomerate discount.
