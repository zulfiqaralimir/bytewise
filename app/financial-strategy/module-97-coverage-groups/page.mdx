# Module 97 — IB Industry Coverage Groups

Every major IB transaction starts with an industry coverage banker who understands the sector deeply enough to identify strategic opportunities before companies themselves recognize them. Coverage banking is where general IB skill meets deep domain expertise. This module covers the major IB coverage groups — what they do, what metrics drive valuation in each sector, what deals look like, and what the Pakistan and Gulf opportunity looks like in each vertical.

## Learning Objectives

- Explain the role and function of each major IB coverage group
- Apply sector-specific valuation metrics for each industry
- Understand the deal dynamics unique to each coverage vertical
- Identify current and emerging deal flow themes in each sector for Pakistan and Gulf
- Know which coverage group to target for a career based on your sector interest

---

## 1. Financial Institutions Group (FIG)

### What FIG Covers

FIG advises banks, insurance companies, asset managers, leasing companies, and fintech firms. It is the most technically complex coverage group because financial institutions have fundamentally different financial statements from industrial companies — their "inventory" is loans, their "cost of goods" is interest expense, and their primary risk is credit risk, not operational risk.

### FIG Valuation Metrics

| Metric | Why It's Used |
|---|---|
| Price-to-Book (P/BV) | Banks' assets are marked to market; P/BV reflects the premium/discount to net asset value |
| Price-to-Tangible Book (P/TBV) | Excludes intangible assets (goodwill from acquisitions) — cleaner for banks |
| Price-to-Earnings (P/E) | Standard profitability metric |
| Return on Equity (ROE) | Banks are leveraged; ROE is the key profitability driver |
| Net Interest Margin (NIM) | Spread between lending and deposit rates — the core banking spread |
| Cost-to-Income Ratio (CIR) | Operating efficiency: lower is better |
| Non-Performing Loan Ratio (NPL%) | Credit quality: rising NPLs signal balance sheet stress |

**Why EBITDA doesn't work for banks:** Interest is a core revenue item (not a financing cost) for banks. EBITDA, which adds back interest, is meaningless for financial institutions.

### Pakistan FIG Deal Flow

**Bank consolidation (SBP-directed):**
SBP has pushed bank mergers to improve capital adequacy and reduce the fragmented banking sector. Notable transactions: National Bank of Pakistan acquisitions, Silk Bank merger discussions, Islamic banking consolidation.

**Islamic banking growth:**
Pakistan's Islamic banking sector (~30% market share) is growing faster than conventional banking. Islamic bank M&A (Meezan Bank, Bank Islami, MCB Islamic) and capital raises are recurring deal themes.

**Insurance privatization:**
State Life Insurance (the largest insurer, government-owned) is a long-standing privatization candidate. Any privatization would be the largest Pakistani financial sector deal in a decade.

**FinTech advisory:**
Easypaisa/Mobilink (largest mobile money platform), JazzCash, Nayapay, and the 5 digital banking licensees (2023) all have capital-raising or M&A needs.

### Gulf FIG Deal Flow

**Bank mega-mergers:** The Gulf has produced some of the world's largest bank mergers — NBAD + FGB = First Abu Dhabi Bank ($229B assets), National Commercial Bank + Samba = Saudi National Bank. More consolidation expected as Vision 2030 and regional competition drive scale requirements.

**Insurance consolidation:** Gulf insurance sector is fragmented; regulators are pushing consolidation. Takaful (Islamic insurance) M&A is active.

**Asset manager M&A:** GCC asset managers are attracting global buyer interest given the pool of investable Gulf wealth.

## 2. Technology, Media & Telecom (TMT)

### What TMT Covers

TMT covers software and internet companies, hardware, media (broadcasting, publishing, streaming), and telecommunications providers. It is the fastest-evolving coverage group — deal dynamics change with every technology cycle (mobile, cloud, AI, fintech).

### TMT Valuation Metrics

| Metric | Why It's Used |
|---|---|
| EV/Revenue | For high-growth, low-margin or loss-making companies where EBITDA is not meaningful |
| EV/ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) | For SaaS/subscription businesses — ARR is more predictable than total revenue |
| EV/EBITDA | For profitable, mature tech or telecom companies |
| EV/Subscribers (for telecom) | Scale metric; subscriber count drives revenue visibility |
| NTM (Next Twelve Months) multiples | Tech is forward-looking — buyers pay for future growth |

**Rule of 40 (SaaS benchmark):** Revenue growth % + EBITDA margin % should exceed 40% for a healthy SaaS business.

### Pakistan TMT Deal Flow

**Telecom:**
- Jazz/Mobilink (VEON-owned), Telenor Pakistan, Zong (China Mobile), Ufone (PTCL/ETISALAT) — all large MNOs with potential M&A (further consolidation, tower sales, data business carve-outs)
- Fiber broadband rollout — PTCL expansion, potential acquisition of regional ISPs

**Tech/FinTech:**
- Careem (Uber-acquired, Pakistan operations) — Pakistan's most famous tech exit
- Airlift (raised $85M Series B, then failed — important lesson)
- Nayapay, Sadapay, Finja — neobanks and digital wallets seeking Series B/C funding
- Pakistan software services exporters (ARPATECH, Systems Limited) — potential acquisition targets for Gulf/global buyers

**Media:**
- Pakistan's media sector (Geo, ARY, Dunya News) is largely private and informally structured — limited M&A activity
- OTT streaming (Vidly TV, local streaming platforms) at early stage

### Gulf TMT Deal Flow

**Careem Technologies:** Dubai-based ride-hailing super-app acquired by Uber for $3.1B (2019) — the Gulf's largest tech exit and still the benchmark.

**Majid Al Futtaim, Noon.com (Amazon-equivalent):** E-commerce M&A and investment active in Gulf.

**ADNOC AI partnerships:** ADNOC has made significant investments in AI and data companies to support its operations.

**Saudi tech sector:** Vision 2030 is building a domestic tech sector; Saudi-focused tech funds and M&A are growing rapidly.

## 3. Energy & Natural Resources

### What Energy Coverage Does

Energy coverage advises oil and gas companies (upstream E&P, midstream, downstream), utility companies, renewable energy developers, and natural resource companies (mining, metals, agriculture).

### Energy Valuation Metrics

| Metric | Why It's Used |
|---|---|
| EV/EBITDA | Standard for midstream and downstream |
| EV/EBITDAX | E&P-specific: adds back exploration expense (X = exploration) |
| EV/Proven Reserves ($/BOE) | Upstream E&P: values the reserve base directly |
| EV/Production ($/BOE per day) | Upstream E&P: values the production rate |
| NAV (Net Asset Value) | E&P: sum of present value of all reserve cash flows |
| EV/MW (for power plants) | Installed generation capacity |
| P/BV | For regulated utilities (fair value of regulated asset base) |

### Pakistan Energy Deal Flow

**E&P sector:**
- OGDCL, PPL, MARI (major national E&P companies) — periodic strategic alliances, farm-ins, JV formations with international E&P
- Private E&P: MOL Pakistan, ENI Pakistan, CNOOC Pakistan — exploration license sales and farm-outs

**Power sector:**
- IPP privatization: Some RLNG-based and coal-based IPPs are seeking investors
- Renewable energy: Solar/wind IPPs are being developed at scale (Reon Energy, Zorlu Energy Pakistan, Yellow Door Energy)
- Circular debt resolution: Any structural fix to Pakistan's circular debt creates M&A opportunities in the DISCO (distribution company) sector

**CPEC energy projects:**
Chinese-funded coal and hydropower projects under CPEC — some seeking refinancing or partial divestiture.

### Gulf Energy Deal Flow

**ADNOC listings:** ADNOC's strategy of listing subsidiaries (ADNOC Drilling, ADNOC Gas, ADNOC Logistics) on ADX generates significant ECM advisory work.

**Saudi Aramco:** The world's most profitable company and the largest IPO in history ($29.4B in 2019). Ongoing capital market activity, sukuk issuances, and strategic acquisitions.

**Energy transition M&A:** Gulf SWFs investing globally in renewable energy (ACWA Power, Masdar) generate M&A advisory opportunities.

## 4. Consumer & Retail

### What Consumer Coverage Does

Consumer coverage advises food and beverage companies, FMCG, retail chains, consumer goods manufacturers, luxury brands, and hospitality companies.

### Consumer Valuation Metrics

| Metric | Why It's Used |
|---|---|
| EV/EBITDA | Standard profitability metric |
| EV/Revenue | For high-growth consumer brands |
| P/E | Widely used for stable, dividend-paying FMCG |
| EV/EBITDA − Capex | For consumer brands with significant capital investment |
| Brand value (intangible) | Consumer deals often include brand valuations as a separate component |

**Sector benchmarks:** FMCG companies typically trade 10–18x EBITDA; quick-service restaurant chains 10–15x; premium consumer brands 15–25x+.

### Pakistan Consumer Deal Flow

Pakistan's consumer sector is one of the most active for M&A:

- **Nestle Pakistan, Unilever Pakistan:** Periodic restructuring of local operations, portfolio rationalization
- **Engro Foods:** Sold to FrieslandCampina (Netherlands) — Pakistan's largest consumer M&A transaction
- **Agility Logistics Pakistan, TCS (The Courier Service):** E-commerce logistics consolidation
- **Quick service restaurants:** KFC, McDonald's, Pizza Hut franchise consolidation; local chains (Hardee's, Subway) PE-backed growth
- **FMCG consolidation:** Reckitt, P&G, Henkel Pakistan — ongoing portfolio adjustments

## 5. Real Estate

### What Real Estate Coverage Does

Real estate IB advises on commercial real estate M&A, hotel transactions, developer financing, REIT listings, infrastructure M&A, and real estate private equity.

### Real Estate Valuation Metrics

| Metric | Why It's Used |
|---|---|
| NAV (Net Asset Value) | Value of the property portfolio at market prices |
| Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate) | NOI / Property Value — industry standard yield metric |
| Funds From Operations (FFO) | REIT equivalent of earnings — adds back non-cash D&A |
| Price/FFO | P/E equivalent for REITs |
| LTV (Loan-to-Value) | Leverage metric for real estate financing |
| Development yield | Projected NOI on cost of development |

**Cap rate formula:**
```
Cap Rate = Net Operating Income / Property Value
```

A 7% cap rate on a commercial building generating PKR 70M NOI implies a PKR 1B property value.

### Pakistan Real Estate Deal Flow

Pakistan real estate is a massive sector (estimated 35–40% of household wealth) but is largely informal and cash-based. Formal IB activity is limited but growing:

- **DHA (Defence Housing Authority) and Bahria Town:** Large developers; periodic fundraising and land sale transactions
- **REIT framework:** SECP has a Real Estate Investment Trust framework; Arif Habib REIT is Pakistan's largest; more REITs expected
- **Commercial real estate:** Office and retail space in Karachi/Lahore/Islamabad attracting institutional interest
- **Mall of Lahore, Packages Mall:** Retail real estate development finance

### Gulf Real Estate Deal Flow

Dubai real estate is among the most active transaction markets globally:

- **Emaar Properties (UAE):** One of the world's largest developers. Periodic M&A and capital market activity.
- **DAMAC Properties:** Luxury developer with periodic capital market activity.
- **Saudi NEOM and Vision 2030 projects:** NEOM City ($500B megaproject) requires massive financing and advisory — infrastructure and real estate advisory.
- **Qatar World Cup legacy projects:** Hospitality M&A post-FIFA World Cup.

## 6. Industrials & Chemicals

### Pakistan and Gulf Industrial Deal Flow

**Pakistan:**
- Cement: Lucky Cement, Cherat Cement, DG Khan — sector has seen capacity additions; potential consolidation as oversupply builds
- Fertilizers: Engro Fertilizers, FFC, Fauji Fertilizer Bin Qasim — mature, high-dividend; limited M&A unless strategic
- Chemicals: ICI Pakistan (now Arham Company after AkzoNobel divestiture) — multinational parent divestiture generates advisory work
- Textiles: NML, Nishat Chunian, Gul Ahmed — export-focused; some PE interest in consolidation

**Gulf:**
- SABIC (Saudi Basic Industries): One of the world's largest chemical companies, majority-owned by Saudi Aramco. Generates petrochemical M&A.
- SIPCHEM, Solvay (Gulf): Chemical sector consolidation driven by Saudi Vision 2030.
- Industrialization program: UAE and Saudi industrial parks create advisory needs for foreign industrial companies entering the Gulf.

## Self-Assessment

1. You are a FIG coverage banker at a Pakistani investment bank. You receive a mandate from SBP to advise on the merger of two mid-tier commercial banks (Bank A: PKR 350B assets; Bank B: PKR 280B assets):
   
   (a) Which valuation metrics would you use to value each bank individually? Explain why EBITDA-based metrics are inappropriate.
   (b) The combined entity would have PKR 630B in assets. List the three key areas of financial analysis you would perform to assess the merger's value creation.
   (c) A Pakistani conglomerate has made an unsolicited approach to acquire Bank B's retail banking business only (not the corporate banking book). Explain the concept of a "carve-out" and identify two structural challenges in carving out a retail banking book.
   (d) Draft a one-paragraph "deal rationale" for the IB pitch book explaining why the merger makes strategic sense for both banks' shareholders.

2. You are a TMT coverage banker advising a Pakistani SaaS company (HR software, PKR 450M ARR, 35% growth, −5% EBITDA margin) on a potential sale:
   
   (a) The company argues it should be valued at 6x ARR. Using the Rule of 40 framework, assess whether this valuation is reasonable. What factors would drive the multiple up or down?
   (b) Identify three categories of potential acquirers and explain what each type of acquirer would value most about this company.
   (c) The company had one quarter where revenue grew 80% due to a large government contract (PKR 60M, now complete). How should this be handled in the financial presentation to buyers?
   (d) A Gulf strategic buyer (UAE HR software consolidator) offers 5x ARR. A Pakistani PE firm offers 4x ARR with a management equity rollover. Walk through the trade-offs the founder should consider in choosing between the two offers.
