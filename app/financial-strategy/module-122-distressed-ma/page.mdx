# Module 122 — Distressed M&A

Distressed M&A is the acquisition of companies in financial distress — at prices reflecting the distress, with unique legal and structural considerations. Buyers in distressed M&A include strategic acquirers looking for discounted assets, private equity firms running "special situations" funds, and creditors who convert their debt claims into equity ownership. This module covers the mechanics, buyer perspective, and regional case studies.

## Learning Objectives

- Explain pre-packaged insolvency (pre-pack) and when it is used
- Understand credit bidding — how creditors acquire companies through their debt claims
- Execute a distressed M&A auction (363 sale equivalent)
- Manage key issues: management retention, customer communication, employee protection
- Apply distressed M&A knowledge to Pakistan corporate distress
- Analyze NMC Health restructuring as a Gulf distressed M&A case study

---

## 1. Why Distressed M&A Is Different

### Key Differences from Normal M&A

| Feature | Normal M&A | Distressed M&A |
|---|---|---|
| Price | Fair market value | Distressed discount (30–60% below normal) |
| Seller | Company/shareholders | Insolvency administrator, secured creditors |
| Due diligence | 4–8 weeks, full access | Compressed (days to 2 weeks); limited data |
| Reps & warranties | Extensive | Minimal or none ("as-is" sale) |
| Employees | Retain all or negotiate | Risk of mass departure; uncertain obligations |
| Customers | Rarely affected | May cancel contracts on insolvency event |
| Timing | 3–6 months | Days to weeks in some cases |
| Legal complexity | High | Extremely high (insolvency law + M&A) |

### Why Buy Distressed Companies?

**Cheap assets:** A business worth $200M in normal circumstances may be acquired for $80M in distress — a 60% discount.

**Clean acquisition (asset purchase):** In distressed situations, buyers often buy specific assets rather than the legal entity — leaving behind liabilities.

**Competitive moat preserved:** Sometimes the underlying business is good; only the capital structure is broken.

## 2. Pre-Packaged Insolvency (Pre-Pack)

A pre-pack involves negotiating and agreeing the sale of a business before the formal insolvency process begins. The moment the company enters insolvency (administration, liquidation), the pre-negotiated sale is immediately executed.

**Why pre-packs work:**
- No loss of going concern value during a public sale process
- Management, employees, customers, and suppliers experience minimal disruption
- Existing creditors agree to the sale before it happens
- The sale price was negotiated in advance

**The administrator's role:**
The insolvency administrator (not the company's management) legally controls the company in administration. The pre-pack is presented to the administrator as the best available option for maximizing creditor recovery.

**Criticism:** Pre-packs can be seen as unfair to unsecured creditors if the secured creditors pre-arranged the sale to themselves (a "connected party" pre-pack).

## 3. Credit Bidding

Credit bidding allows a secured creditor to bid for the assets securing their loan using the debt claim itself as currency — rather than paying cash.

**Mechanics:**
1. Company is in insolvency; its assets are being sold
2. Secured creditor holds $100M of debt secured by specific assets
3. At the auction, the creditor bids "$100M" — their bid is the face value of their debt claim
4. If the creditor wins, they receive the assets; their debt claim is extinguished

**Why credit bidding matters:**
- Creditors can acquire businesses at the debt floor without needing cash
- Effectively converts debt to equity/ownership
- Sets a floor price in distressed auctions (no one can outbid the secured creditor below the debt face value without the creditor choosing to credit bid)

**Pakistan context:** Credit bidding is not formally established in Pakistan insolvency law (unlike US Chapter 11 or UK). However, the practical outcome of Pakistani bank enforcement against secured assets achieves a similar result — banks take possession of collateral.

## 4. Distressed Auction Mechanics

### The 363 Sale (US) Equivalent

In the US, Section 363 of the Bankruptcy Code allows a debtor to sell assets quickly with court approval, free and clear of existing liens and claims. The equivalent processes exist in UK (administration) and other common law jurisdictions.

**Distressed auction process:**
1. **Stalking horse bid:** Buyer (often a current creditor) negotiates a floor bid before the auction. Gets break-up fee (1–3% of deal value) if outbid.
2. **Marketing process:** 2–4 weeks; limited NDA/IM process; compressed due diligence
3. **Auction:** Qualified bidders compete; highest bid wins
4. **Court approval:** Winning bid submitted to court for approval
5. **Closing:** Often within days of court approval

**Stalking horse advantage:** The stalking horse gets time to negotiate terms, secure the asset, and earn the break-up fee even if outbid.

## 5. Management Retention in Distress

### The Talent Problem

Key employees (customer relationship managers, engineers, financial staff) leave distressed companies rapidly. Once they leave, the business loses value. Acquirers must address this proactively.

**Key retention mechanisms:**
- **KERP (Key Employee Retention Plan):** Payments to retain key staff through the transaction
- **KEIP (Key Employee Incentive Plan):** Performance-based bonuses tied to successful restructuring
- **Equity rollover:** Key management receives equity in the post-restructuring company
- **Severance clarity:** Employees need certainty about what happens to their compensation and pension if they stay

## 6. Pakistan Distressed M&A

Pakistan has had several notable distressed situations:

**K-Electric (KESC):**
- Karachi's electricity distribution company was loss-making, privatized in 2005, restructured multiple times
- Abraaj Group acquired K-Electric; KES Power (a consortium) subsequently managed the business
- Sale to Shanghai Electric Group announced in 2016 ($1.77B) — still unresolved years later due to regulatory issues
- Classic distressed/complex M&A: circle debt exposure, regulatory uncertainty, labor issues

**Silk Bank:**
- Sub-scale Pakistani bank with capital adequacy concerns
- Merger with FINCA (a microfinance institution) proposed
- Regulatory pressure from SBP for consolidation

**Pakistan Steel Mills:**
- Government-owned steel company, loss-making for decades
- Multiple privatization attempts, worker resistance, creditor (banks) with significant exposure
- A textbook case of distressed government-owned enterprise M&A challenges
