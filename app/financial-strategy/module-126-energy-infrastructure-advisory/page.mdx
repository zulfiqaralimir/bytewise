# Module 126 — Energy & Infrastructure Advisory

Energy and infrastructure advisory is one of the most technically demanding and financially significant areas of IB. Energy deals can be multi-billion dollar transactions with long gestation periods, complex regulatory approvals, and specialized valuation frameworks (NAV for E&P; DCF of regulated cash flows for infrastructure). This module covers E&P M&A, power sector advisory, renewable energy, and infrastructure deals — with a Pakistan and Gulf focus.

## Learning Objectives

- Value E&P companies using NAV (2P reserve-based DCF at PV10)
- Structure an IPP advisory mandate (project finance, PPA, construction contract)
- Advise on renewable energy M&A in Pakistan and Gulf
- Execute infrastructure M&A and project financing
- Analyze Pakistan OGDCL/PPL deal context and Gulf Aramco/ADNOC strategy

## 1. E&P M&A — NAV Valuation

### Reserve-Based Valuation

E&P companies hold underground reserves — hydrocarbons that will be produced and sold over time. The value of an E&P company is essentially the NPV of its future production cash flows:

```
NAV = PV of 2P proved + probable reserves (discounted at 10%)
    + Risked exploration upside (probability × NPV if successful)
    − Net debt
    = NAV per share
```

**PV10:** Industry convention — discount reserves at 10% regardless of WACC.

**Key inputs:**
- Long-term oil price assumption (typically Brent curve)
- Production profile (decline curve based on reservoir engineering)
- Lifting costs (opex per BOE)
- Royalties and government take
- Capex for development wells

**Pakistan E&P context:**
OGDCL, PPL, and MARI are the major listed E&P companies on PSX. International companies active in Pakistan: ENI, MOL, CNOOC, BHP (historically). Reserve reports are published annually.

**PSX E&P valuation shortcuts:**
Analysts often apply EV/2P (EV per barrel of proved + probable reserves) as a quick screen:
- Pakistan E&P peers: $3–6/BOE (lower than global EM peers due to Pakistan risk premium)

### E&P M&A Structures

**Farm-out:** An E&P company sells a percentage of its working interest in a license to another party in exchange for carried cost. Common in exploration-stage assets where the license holder needs cash to fund drilling.

**Full acquisition:** Strategic buyer acquires 100% of an E&P company.

**Pakistan regulatory:** OGRA (Oil & Gas Regulatory Authority) and Petroleum Division approval required for E&P asset transfers.

## 2. IPP Advisory and Project Finance

### Independent Power Producer (IPP) Structure

An IPP is a private company that builds, owns, and operates a power plant and sells electricity under a long-term Power Purchase Agreement (PPA):

```
Fuel Supplier → IPP → WAPDA/CPPA-G (off-taker) → Grid → Consumers
```

**Key project finance documents:**
- **PPA (Power Purchase Agreement):** WAPDA/CPPA-G commits to buy all electricity produced (take-or-pay)
- **Fuel Supply Agreement (FSA):** Gas/coal supplier commits to supply fuel
- **Implementation Agreement (IA):** Government provides additional comfort (sovereign guarantee)
- **EPC Contract:** Construction company commits to build the plant on time and on budget
- **O&M Agreement:** Operating company commits to run the plant

**Project finance structure:**
80–85% debt financing (non-recourse — only project cash flows service the debt, not the sponsors' balance sheets); 15–20% equity. This high leverage amplifies equity returns.

### Pakistan IPP Landscape

Pakistan's IPP sector has been one of the most active in South Asia:
- 1994 Power Policy (Hub Power Company, KAPCO): first wave of private power
- 2002 Power Policy: second wave
- CPEC Energy Projects (2015–2020): coal and hydro plants
- Renewable Energy Policy 2019: solar and wind feed-in tariffs

**Circular debt crisis:**
Pakistan's power sector suffers from systematic circular debt — the government sets retail electricity tariffs below cost; DISCOs can't collect full receivables; IPPs aren't paid on time; they borrow to cover expenses; interest costs rise. Total circular debt has grown to PKR 2.5T+.

**IB advisory in Pakistan power:**
Advising IPPs on refinancing (replace expensive PKR debt with cheaper instruments), restructuring (when circular debt disrupts cash flows), and sale processes (Chinese or other strategic buyers for CPEC plants).

## 3. Renewable Energy M&A

### Global Renewable Energy M&A Themes

- Solar and wind M&A dominated global energy M&A 2020–2024
- Strategic buyers: major oil companies (BP, Shell, TotalEnergies) diversifying into renewables
- Infrastructure funds: Brookfield, BlackRock Infrastructure, Macquarie paying high multiples for contracted renewable assets
- Typical renewable energy M&A multiple: 10–16x EV/EBITDA for contracted projects

**Pakistan renewable energy:**
- Solar policy: competitive tariff process (auctions); recent deals include 600MW Quaid-e-Azam Solar Park
- Wind: Jhimpir, Gharo wind corridors in Sindh; ACWA Power, Zorlu Energy, Lucky Electric Wind
- Hydro: Tarbela, Mangla, Neelum-Jhelum (government-owned); new hydro projects under CPEC
- IB advisory: Renewable energy companies raising equity or project finance; foreign buyers interested in acquiring operational wind/solar projects

**Gulf renewable energy (Masdar, ACWA Power):**
- UAE's Masdar is the world's largest renewable energy company by capacity under development
- ACWA Power (Saudi) has become a leading global renewable energy developer
- Gulf SWFs investing in renewable energy globally (ADIA, Mubadala, PIF)

## 4. Infrastructure M&A

### Infrastructure Valuation

Infrastructure assets (roads, ports, airports, utilities) generate stable, long-term cash flows under regulatory frameworks or concession agreements. Valued using:
- **Regulated Asset Base (RAB) model:** Regulator allows a return on the RAB; value = RAB × allowed P/RAB multiple
- **DCF of contracted cash flows:** For PPP concessions; discount at infrastructure WACC (7–10% for rated OECD markets; 12–15% for Pakistan)
- **EV/EBITDA:** For infrastructure companies with market comparables

**Pakistan infrastructure deals:**
- Gwadar Port: China Overseas Port Holdings operates under 40-year concession
- NLC (National Logistics Cell): Government logistics; no IB advisory currently
- Lahore-Islamabad Motorway M2: NHA-operated; potential for sale-leaseback or privatization

**NEOM (Saudi Arabia):**
NEOM is the world's most ambitious infrastructure project — a $500B futuristic city and industrial zone in northwest Saudi Arabia. Financing involves:
- PIF direct equity
- Project finance for specific components (THE LINE, OXAGON, SINDALAH)
- International bank syndication
- Project bonds for specific infrastructure

## Self-Assessment

1. You are advising a PE fund on the acquisition of an operational solar IPP in Pakistan (100MW, 25-year PPA with NEPRA at PKR 12/kWh, construction complete, operating for 2 years):
   (a) Describe the three key due diligence workstreams specific to a Pakistani solar IPP (beyond standard M&A DD).
   (b) What is the primary credit risk in this investment, and how does the Implementation Agreement with the Government of Pakistan mitigate it?
   (c) Build a simplified DCF: annual revenue = PKR 12/kWh × 100MW × 8,760 hours × 25% capacity factor. Assume PKR 2/kWh operating cost, 29% tax, and 14% discount rate. Is this project worth PKR 4B?
   (d) The PPA is in PKR. The PE fund reports in USD. How does this currency mismatch affect the investment case, and what can the fund do to manage it?

2. ADNOC is planning to sell a 20% stake in its gas processing infrastructure to international infrastructure funds. The assets generate $2B annual EBITDA under 20-year gas processing agreements:
   (a) What is the appropriate valuation multiple range for contracted infrastructure assets in the UAE? Calculate the implied 20% stake value.
   (b) Why would international infrastructure funds (Brookfield, Macquarie) be interested in this asset? What makes it attractive vs. typical infrastructure investments?
   (c) ADNOC retains 80% and a majority of board seats. The infrastructure fund has minority investor protections in the shareholder agreement. List four minority protections you would negotiate.
   (d) A Malaysian fund wants to invest using an Islamic structure. How would you adapt the deal structure to be Shariah-compliant?
