# Module 90 — ESG for Investment Managers

ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) has moved from a niche ethical screen to a mainstream investment consideration — driven by regulatory pressure, institutional investor mandates, and growing evidence that ESG factors predict long-term risks that financial statements miss. For FERROQUANT, ESG matters on three levels: as a potential alpha signal (ESG data predicts stock returns), as a risk management tool (poor governance often predates financial fraud), and as a commercial necessity (Gulf sovereign wealth funds increasingly require ESG compliance from asset managers seeking mandates).

## Learning Objectives

- Explain the three pillars of ESG and why each matters financially
- Distinguish ESG screening, integration, and impact investing approaches
- Construct and evaluate quantitative ESG signals
- Navigate SFDR Article 6/8/9 classification requirements
- Understand greenwashing risk and how to mitigate it
- Apply ESG to Gulf SWF mandates and Pakistan's emerging ESG landscape

## 1. ESG Framework: What Each Pillar Captures

### Environmental (E)

**What it measures:** A company's impact on and exposure to environmental risks.

| Metric | What It Signals | Financially Relevant Because |
|---|---|---|
| Carbon emissions (Scope 1, 2, 3) | Direct/indirect greenhouse gas | Carbon pricing risk; stranded asset risk |
| Water consumption | Operational water intensity | Water scarcity risk in water-stressed regions |
| Waste generation/recycling | Operational efficiency | Regulatory fines; remediation costs |
| Renewable energy usage | Transition risk exposure | Future cost structure; regulatory exposure |
| Biodiversity impact | Land use, deforestation | Regulatory and social license risk |

**Financially material example:** A Pakistan cement company with high energy intensity (coal-powered kilns) faces rising input costs as Pakistan transitions toward RLNG and renewable energy. The company's environmental profile is directly linked to its long-term profitability.

### Social (S)

**What it measures:** A company's relationships with employees, communities, and supply chains.

| Metric | What It Signals | Financially Relevant Because |
|---|---|---|
| Worker safety (injury rates) | Operational risk | Liability, productivity, regulatory |
| Labor practices (fair wages, no child labor) | Supply chain risk | Reputational risk, market access |
| Community relations | License to operate | Project delays, protests, disruptions |
| Gender diversity | Governance quality | Research shows diverse boards outperform |
| Data privacy | Customer data protection | Regulatory fines (GDPR, PDPA) |

**Pakistan context:** PSX-listed companies in textile and manufacturing sectors face scrutiny over labor practices (child labor, minimum wage compliance, factory safety — post-Baldia fire reforms) from international buyers and investors.

### Governance (G)

**What it measures:** The quality of a company's leadership, accountability, and shareholder rights.

| Metric | What It Signals | Financially Relevant Because |
|---|---|---|
| Board independence | Management oversight quality | Fraud prevention; strategic oversight |
| CEO/Chairman separation | Power concentration | Governance risk |
| Audit quality (Big 4 vs. local) | Financial reporting reliability | Fraud risk proxy |
| Related-party transactions | Minority shareholder risk | Cash diversion risk |
| Ownership concentration | Founder/family control | Minority shareholder rights |

**Pakistan G-factor:** PSX-listed companies often have concentrated family ownership (>50% family holding). This is not inherently bad — family-controlled businesses can be better aligned with long-term value creation — but it increases related-party transaction risk and minority shareholder oppression risk.

## 2. ESG Investment Approaches

### Negative Screening (Exclusion)

**Exclude companies involved in:**
- Controversial weapons (cluster munitions, chemical/biological/nuclear)
- Tobacco
- Gambling and adult entertainment
- Alcohol (critical for Islamic finance-compliant mandates — covered in Module 91)
- Fossil fuels (for climate-focused mandates)

**Minimum viable screen for Gulf SWF mandates:**
Many GCC sovereign wealth funds require negative screens on:
- Companies deriving >10% revenue from tobacco
- Companies manufacturing controversial weapons
- Companies with major ESG controversies (e.g., Volkswagen emissions scandal, BP Deepwater Horizon)

### ESG Integration

**Not exclusion — weighting adjustment:** ESG scores modify valuations rather than eliminating companies.

**Approaches:**
1. **Best-in-class:** Hold the highest ESG-scoring companies within each sector (not just "clean" sectors). A high-ESG oil company is preferred over a low-ESG oil company — sector exposure maintained.
2. **ESG tilt:** Overweight high-ESG stocks, underweight low-ESG stocks within the portfolio. Maintain sector neutrality to avoid unintended sector bets.
3. **ESG factor integration:** Include ESG scores as an additional factor alongside momentum, value, and quality in the quantitative model.

### Thematic / Impact Investing

**Thematic:** Concentrate in themes — renewable energy, water infrastructure, digital inclusion, healthcare access. Accepts sector concentration for thematic exposure.

**Impact:** Target measurable positive outcomes (jobs created, CO2 avoided, loans to underserved communities). Requires impact measurement and reporting — not just screening.

## 3. Quantitative ESG Signals

### ESG as a Factor

**ESG score:** Aggregated score from data providers (MSCI ESG, Sustainalytics, Bloomberg ESG). Covers E, S, and G sub-scores.

**ESG momentum:** Change in ESG score over time. Improving ESG scores predict positive stock returns (rising ESG score → institutional buying). More alpha than the level of the ESG score.

**ESG controversy score:** Flag companies with active ESG controversies (environmental spills, labor lawsuits, governance scandals). Companies with recent major controversies tend to underperform.

**Governance quality proxy (G-factor):**
- Audit quality (Big 4 = 1, non-Big 4 = 0)
- Board independence ratio
- Related-party transaction intensity (related-party transactions / total revenue)
- Ownership concentration (family/founder % → higher concentration = lower G score)

**G-factor as a fraud predictor for Pakistan:**
PSX has had numerous corporate governance failures (Bank Alfalah loan book issues, TFC defaults, related-party abuse). A quantitative G-score that penalizes weak governance has served as a genuine risk-reduction signal in Pakistani equities.

### IC Analysis of ESG Signals on PSX

**Challenge:** ESG data coverage for PSX is limited. MSCI covers ~30 KSE-100 companies. Bloomberg ESG covers ~20. Many PSX companies have no formal ESG scoring.

**FERROQUANT's approach:**
1. Use available third-party ESG data for large-caps
2. Build an internal "mini-ESG" score using proxy metrics available from annual reports:
   - G-factor: board composition, audit quality, related-party transactions (from SECP filings)
   - E-proxy: energy intensity (from production data in annual reports)
   - S-proxy: workforce safety (accidents reported in annual report)
3. Back-test this internal score as a long/short signal on KSE-100

## 4. SFDR Classification (EU Sustainable Finance Disclosure Regulation)

### Article 6, 8, and 9

SFDR requires EU-regulated funds (UCITS, AIFs marketed in EU) to disclose their ESG approach in one of three categories:

**Article 6 — "No specific ESG claims":**
The fund does not integrate ESG systematically. No specific ESG disclosures required beyond a brief explanation of why ESG isn't integrated.
- Label: No ESG label
- Marketing claim: Cannot claim to be "sustainable" or "ESG"

**Article 8 — "Promotes environmental or social characteristics":**
The fund promotes E or S characteristics as part of its investment strategy, but the primary objective is financial return.
- Examples: A diversified fund that applies ESG screens + uses ESG tilt
- Required: Pre-contractual and periodic disclosure of how ESG characteristics are achieved
- Label: "Light Green"

**Article 9 — "Has sustainable investment as its objective":**
The fund's primary objective is sustainable investment (measurable positive impact).
- Examples: A climate-focused fund that invests only in renewable energy companies
- Required: Detailed impact reporting, EU taxonomy alignment
- Label: "Dark Green"

**FERROQUANT's SFDR classification:**
If FERROQUANT markets to EU investors through a DIFC-to-Europe structure, it must classify:
- Base strategy (PSX momentum + PIBs): likely **Article 6** (no ESG claims)
- ESG-tilted version: **Article 8** (promotes governance as a characteristic)
- Cannot claim Article 9 without dedicated impact measurement

### Greenwashing Risk

**Greenwashing:** Claiming ESG compliance or impact without genuine underlying practice.

**Regulatory risk:** EU regulators have investigated multiple funds for misleading Article 8/9 claims. Fines and reputational damage.

**FERROQUANT greenwashing protection:**
- Only market the fund using SFDR language that matches the actual investment process
- Document ESG integration in the investment policy statement (IPS)
- Annual review of ESG score coverage and methodology
- Never use ESG labels in marketing without SFDR-compliant underlying process

## 5. Gulf SWF ESG Mandates

### ADIA (Abu Dhabi Investment Authority)

ADIA manages ~$993B and has made ESG integration mandatory for external managers:
- Requires external managers to sign the ADIA ESG Manager Framework
- Assesses ESG capability during manager due diligence
- Reports its own portfolio carbon footprint and expects managers to provide data

**ADIA's priorities:**
- Climate risk integration (physical and transition risk)
- Board diversity and governance quality
- Human capital management

**For FERROQUANT to access ADIA mandates:** Must demonstrate ESG integration process, must be able to report portfolio ESG scores and carbon footprint.

### Mubadala Investment Company (Abu Dhabi)

**AUM:** ~$276B. Focus on technology, energy transition, and infrastructure.

**ESG requirements for external managers:**
- Signed UN PRI (Principles for Responsible Investment) commitment
- Annual ESG reporting using TCFD (Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures) framework
- Anti-bribery and corruption policy aligned with UNODC standards

### Qatar Investment Authority (QIA)

**AUM:** ~$475B. Focus on financial services, real estate, and diversified assets.

**ESG approach:** Less prescriptive than ADIA. Focuses on governance (board quality) and controversy avoidance. Growing emphasis on climate risk.

### What FERROQUANT Needs for GCC SWF Access

**Minimum ESG infrastructure for GCC SWF mandates:**
1. UN PRI signatory status (free to join, annual reporting required)
2. ESG integration policy document
3. Portfolio ESG score reporting capability
4. Board diversity and governance policy
5. Anti-bribery/corruption policy (FCPA/Bribery Act compliance)
6. Annual ESG report to investors

## 6. Pakistan's ESG Landscape

### PSX ESG Developments

**PSX and SECP ESG initiatives:**
- SECP launched **Environmental and Social Risk Management (ESRM) guidelines** in 2017
- PSX introduced the **Pakistan ESG Index** (tracking high-ESG-scoring KSE-100 companies) in 2020
- SECP sustainability reporting guidelines (voluntary initially, moving toward mandatory for listed companies)
- State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) introduced **Green Banking Policy** and **Green Taxonomy** (2021) to encourage environmental lending

**Pakistan Sustainability Reporting:**
- Large listed companies required to include sustainability disclosures in annual reports (SECP directive 2019)
- Coverage of GHG emissions, water use, labor practices, board diversity
- Enforcement is light → compliance is patchy → data quality is inconsistent

### Pakistan ESG Challenges

**Structural constraints:**
- Limited data availability: Most PSX companies lack international-standard ESG disclosures
- Governance weaknesses: Related-party transactions, family control, audit quality gaps are widespread
- Environmental: Pakistan is a major climate-vulnerable country (floods, heat stress) but its industry's environmental disclosure is underdeveloped
- Social: Labor practices in textile sector (Pakistan's largest export industry) are scrutinized by international buyers — creating ESG risk for FERROQUANT's portfolio exposure

**ESG opportunity:**
- Companies improving governance (hiring Big 4 auditors, reducing related-party transactions, adding independent directors) tend to see valuation re-rating
- FERROQUANT's G-factor-focused signal captures this re-rating momentum before it fully shows in price

## Self-Assessment

1. FERROQUANT is evaluating two PSX textile companies for inclusion in its equity book:
   
   **Company A (NML — Nishat Mills):**
   - Board: 9 members, 3 independent (33% independence)
   - Auditor: KPMG Pakistan (Big 4)
   - Related-party transactions: 8% of revenue
   - ESG controversy score: None recent
   - Environmental: Energy intensity falling 5%/year; LEED-certified factory
   
   **Company B (Mytextile Ltd):**
   - Board: 7 members, 1 independent (14% independence)
   - Auditor: Small local firm
   - Related-party transactions: 22% of revenue
   - ESG controversy score: 1 recent factory safety incident
   - Environmental: No disclosure
   
   Both companies have similar valuations (P/E 8x) and momentum signals.
   
   (a) Score each company on a simple G-factor (0–10 scale) using: board independence, audit quality, related-party transaction intensity. Which scores higher?
   (b) FERROQUANT's portfolio manager wants to include both stocks equally. FERROQUANT's risk manager disagrees, citing the G-factor difference. Make the risk manager's argument: what specific financial risks does the G-factor difference represent?
   (c) If FERROQUANT applies a "best-in-class" ESG approach to the textile sector, how should it adjust the position sizes of Company A vs. Company B? Show the weight adjustment methodology.
   (d) A Gulf SWF investor reviewing FERROQUANT's portfolio asks for the ESG score of each holding. Company B has no MSCI ESG score. How should FERROQUANT handle this gap in external ESG coverage?

2. FERROQUANT is considering registering as a UN PRI signatory and classifying its primary fund as SFDR Article 8:
   
   (a) List the five commitments a UN PRI signatory must make. For each, identify the specific action FERROQUANT must take to comply.
   (b) For SFDR Article 8 classification, FERROQUANT must "promote" a specific environmental or social characteristic. Which characteristic should FERROQUANT promote given its PSX focus? Justify your choice and explain how it would be operationalized in the investment process.
   (c) An EU investor asks: "Is this fund Article 9 — does it have sustainable investment as its primary objective?" Why would claiming Article 9 be inappropriate for FERROQUANT's current strategy? What additional changes to the strategy would be required to genuinely qualify as Article 9?
   (d) Six months after claiming Article 8, FERROQUANT's risk team identifies that 15% of the equity book is invested in companies with active ESG controversies (factory accidents, regulatory investigations). What are the legal and reputational risks? What immediate actions should FERROQUANT take?
