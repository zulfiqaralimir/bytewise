# Module 98 — Comparable Company Analysis

Comparable company analysis (trading comps) is the most widely used valuation methodology in investment banking. Its logic is simple: if similar public companies trade at certain multiples of their earnings or revenue, a private company with similar characteristics should be worth a comparable multiple. The devil is in the execution — selecting the right peers, spreading financials consistently, calculating EV correctly, and presenting the output in a way that withstands client and board scrutiny.

## Learning Objectives

- Select appropriate peer companies using four criteria
- Calculate Enterprise Value correctly from market data
- Spread trailing, LTM, and forward financial metrics consistently
- Calculate and interpret EV/EBITDA, EV/Revenue, P/E, and P/BV multiples
- Apply discounts and premiums to the comps range
- Build and present a comps output table for Pakistan and Gulf companies

---

## 1. The Logic of Trading Comps

### Why Public Market Comparables Matter

Public markets represent the collective judgment of thousands of informed investors about what a business is worth. If the market consistently values cement companies at 7–9x EBITDA, that is powerful evidence that a cement company is worth approximately 7–9x EBITDA.

**Trading comps give you:**
- A market-based reference range for valuation
- A benchmark to assess whether a private company is cheap or expensive relative to peers
- A reality check on DCF assumptions (if your DCF gives 15x EBITDA but peers trade at 8x, your assumptions need scrutiny)

**Limitations:**
- Public market sentiment affects multiples (bull markets → high multiples; bear markets → low multiples)
- No perfect comparable exists — every company is unique
- Minority discount: public market prices represent minority stakes; M&A typically requires a control premium (20–40% above the trading price)

---

## 2. Step 1 — Peer Group Selection

### The Four Selection Criteria

A peer group should consist of companies that are similar to the target on all four dimensions:

**1. Business similarity:**
- Same industry or sub-industry
- Similar products/services
- Similar business model (asset-heavy vs. asset-light, B2B vs. B2C)

**2. Geographic similarity:**
- Operate in the same country or at minimum the same economic region
- A Pakistan cement company should be compared to other Pakistan/regional cement companies — not US cement companies (different cost structures, growth rates, capital costs)

**3. Financial profile similarity:**
- Similar revenue scale (±1–2 orders of magnitude)
- Similar growth rates
- Similar margins
- Similar capital intensity (capex/revenue)

**4. Lifecycle stage:**
- High-growth startups vs. mature, dividend-paying businesses should never be in the same comp set
- A 30% revenue growth company trades at very different multiples than a 3% growth company

### Tier the Peer Group

Not all peers are equally comparable. Structure the comp table in tiers:

- **Tier 1 (Core comps):** Most directly comparable — same business, same geography, similar scale. Use this range for primary valuation.
- **Tier 2 (Secondary comps):** Similar business but different geography or scale. Use as a sanity check.
- **Tier 3 (Reference comps):** Tangentially related — different sub-sector or significantly different scale. Show in appendix for context.

## 3. Step 2 — Calculating Enterprise Value

### Enterprise Value Formula

```
Enterprise Value = Market Capitalization
                + Total Debt (gross)
                + Minority Interest
                + Preferred Stock
                − Cash and Cash Equivalents
```

**Market capitalization:**
```
Market Cap = Share Price × Diluted Shares Outstanding
```

Diluted shares include: basic shares + in-the-money options (treasury stock method) + convertible securities (if-converted method).

**Minority interest:** If the company consolidates a subsidiary it doesn't fully own, the minority's share of the subsidiary is included in EV (because the consolidated EBITDA includes the subsidiary's full earnings).

**Total debt:** Use gross debt (Module 96 definition) — all interest-bearing obligations.

**Cash:** Deduct only freely available cash. Restricted cash stays in debt-side adjustments.

### EV in Pakistan Context

**PSX-listed company EV example:**
- Engro Fertilizers share price: PKR 85
- Diluted shares: 1,400M
- Market Cap: PKR 119B
- Net debt (short-term + long-term − cash): PKR 12B
- Enterprise Value: PKR 131B

**Important:** When spreading comps for a Pakistani company, always use the same currency for all peers (USD or PKR) and specify the exchange rate and date.

## 4. Step 3 — Spreading Financials

### Time Periods

**Historical periods:**
- LTM (Last Twelve Months): The most recent 12 months. Calculated as: Last annual + YTD current year − YTD prior year.
- Prior full fiscal year (FY-1): The last complete fiscal year.
- Two years ago (FY-2): For trend analysis.

**Forward-looking periods (consensus estimates):**
- NTM (Next Twelve Months): Analyst consensus estimate for the next 12 months. Often more relevant for growth companies.
- CY+1 (Calendar Year +1): Next full calendar year estimates.
- CY+2: The year after.

**Source for estimates:** Bloomberg consensus, Refinitiv (IBES), company guidance.

### Metrics to Spread

For each comp company, spread:

```
Revenue (LTM, CY+1, CY+2)
EBITDA (LTM, CY+1, CY+2)
EBITDA margin % (LTM, CY+1, CY+2)
EBIT (LTM)
D&A (LTM)
Net Income (LTM, CY+1, CY+2)
EPS (LTM, CY+1, CY+2)
Book Value per Share (latest balance sheet)
Free Cash Flow (LTM)
```

**Consistency is critical:** Apply the same adjustments to all companies. If you exclude restructuring charges for one company, you must apply the same treatment across all peers.

## 5. Step 4 — Calculating Multiples

### EV-Based Multiples

```
EV/EBITDA = Enterprise Value / EBITDA
EV/EBIT   = Enterprise Value / EBIT
EV/Revenue = Enterprise Value / Revenue
```

**EV/EBITDA** is the most widely used M&A valuation multiple because:
- It is capital structure-neutral (uses EV, which adds back debt, rather than equity value)
- It is comparable across companies with different leverage levels
- It approximates "what multiple of annual cash earnings am I paying?"

**Typical EV/EBITDA ranges by sector:**
| Sector | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Software/SaaS (growth) | 15–40x |
| FMCG / consumer staples | 10–18x |
| Healthcare | 10–20x |
| Industrial/manufacturing | 6–10x |
| Energy (E&P) | 4–8x |
| Cement | 6–10x |
| Banking | Not applicable (P/BV used instead) |
| Telecom | 5–9x |

### Equity-Based Multiples

```
P/E = Share Price / Earnings Per Share
P/BV = Share Price / Book Value Per Share
```

**P/E:** Most intuitive — how many years of earnings does the market price imply? Volatile because EPS is affected by capital structure (interest expense) and tax.

**P/BV:** Most relevant for banks, insurance companies, and asset-heavy businesses where balance sheet value is a better anchor than earnings.

## 6. Worked Example: Pakistan Cement Sector Comps

### Peer Group

| Company | Ticker | Market Cap (PKR B) | Net Debt (PKR B) | EV (PKR B) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lucky Cement | LUCK | 185 | 8 | 193 |
| DG Khan Cement | DGKC | 62 | 15 | 77 |
| Cherat Cement | CHCC | 28 | 6 | 34 |
| Maple Leaf Cement | MLCF | 22 | 12 | 34 |
| Bestway Cement | BWCL | 38 | 4 | 42 |

### Spreading LTM Financials (PKR billions, illustrative)

| Company | Revenue | EBITDA | EBITDA Margin | Net Income |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lucky Cement | 105 | 28 | 26.7% | 18 |
| DG Khan Cement | 55 | 13 | 23.6% | 7 |
| Cherat Cement | 28 | 7 | 25.0% | 4 |
| Maple Leaf Cement | 32 | 7 | 21.9% | 3 |
| Bestway Cement | 42 | 10 | 23.8% | 6 |

### Calculating Multiples

| Company | EV/EBITDA | EV/Revenue | P/E |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lucky Cement | 6.9x | 1.84x | 10.3x |
| DG Khan Cement | 5.9x | 1.40x | 8.9x |
| Cherat Cement | 4.9x | 1.21x | 7.0x |
| Maple Leaf Cement | 4.9x | 1.06x | 7.3x |
| Bestway Cement | 4.2x | 1.00x | 6.3x |
| **Median** | **4.9x** | **1.21x** | **7.3x** |
| **Mean** | **5.4x** | **1.30x** | **8.0x** |
| **Min** | **4.2x** | **1.00x** | **6.3x** |
| **Max** | **6.9x** | **1.84x** | **10.3x** |

**Key observation:** Lucky Cement trades at a premium (6.9x vs. sector median 4.9x) — reflects its superior margins, export capability, and stronger management track record.

### Valuing a Private Target

If a private cement company has LTM EBITDA of PKR 9B:
```
At 25th percentile (4.9x): EV = PKR 44B
At median (4.9x):           EV = PKR 44B
At 75th percentile (5.9x): EV = PKR 53B
At maximum (6.9x):          EV = PKR 62B
```

The comps suggest an EV range of approximately PKR 44–62B. The midpoint (PKR 48–53B) would be the analyst's base case recommendation before applying any control premium.

## 7. Presenting Comps Output

### The Comps Table Format

Every IB comps table includes:
1. Company name, ticker, market cap, EV
2. Revenue (LTM, NTM)
3. EBITDA (LTM, NTM) with margin %
4. EPS (LTM, NTM) if P/E is relevant
5. All calculated multiples (EV/EBITDA, P/E, P/BV where relevant)
6. Summary row: Mean, Median, 25th percentile, 75th percentile

**Highlight outliers:** If one company is clearly an outlier (e.g., distressed balance sheet, recent M&A disruption), flag it and explain whether to include or exclude from the median calculation.

### Applying Benchmarks to Target Company

Apply a selected multiple range to the target's metrics:

```
Target's LTM EBITDA: PKR 9B
Selected multiple range: 5.0x – 6.0x (median to 75th percentile)

Implied EV range: PKR 45B – PKR 54B
Less: Net Debt (PKR 8B)
Implied Equity Value range: PKR 37B – PKR 46B
```

**Premium/discount adjustments:**
- Target is smaller than peers → apply 10–15% size discount
- Target has better margins than peers → apply 5–10% premium
- Private company vs. public peers → typically no separate discount (control premium in M&A offsets the private company discount)

   You identify the following potential peer companies:

   - (B) Coca-Cola Icecek (Turkey-listed, bottler, similar geography/demographics)
