# Module 106 — Buy-Side M&A Process

Every sell-side process has a counterpart on the other side of the table: the buy-side. While the sell-side advisor focuses on maximizing price and running a clean process, the buy-side advisor's job is to help the acquirer understand what they are buying, what it is worth, how to structure the offer to win the auction, and how to negotiate the SPA to protect the buyer's interests. This module covers buy-side M&A from first contact with a target through to signing.

## Learning Objectives

- Manage the buy-side due diligence workstreams
- Quantify synergies and integrate them into bid pricing
- Design a bid strategy for competitive auctions
- Negotiate key SPA financial terms from the acquirer's perspective
- Build the acquisition business case for internal approval
- Apply buy-side M&A skills to Pakistan and Gulf deal contexts

---

## 1. Initial Assessment and Go/No-Go

### When a Target Appears

A buy-side mandate can originate two ways:
1. **Proactive (banker-originated):** The IB advisor identifies a target and pitches the idea to the acquirer
2. **Reactive (process-initiated):** The acquirer receives a teaser or process letter from a sell-side advisor running an auction

In both cases, the first step is an internal go/no-go assessment:

**Strategic fit:** Does this target advance the company's strategy as articulated to shareholders and the board?

**Financial impact:** Is the acquisition likely to be accretive or dilutive to earnings? Can the company afford the deal?

**Integration feasibility:** Can we actually combine these businesses successfully?

**Regulatory clearance:** Are there antitrust, sector-specific, or foreign investment restrictions that could block or significantly delay the deal?

### Preliminary Valuation

Before investing in due diligence, the buy-side team does a preliminary valuation:
- Quick trading comps and precedent transaction screen
- Back-of-envelope DCF using public financial information
- LBO analysis (if PE) or accretion/dilution analysis (if strategic acquirer)

**Acquisition accretion/dilution:**
For listed strategic acquirers, the deal must be evaluated for impact on earnings per share:

```
Accretive deal: Combined EPS > Acquirer EPS alone (before synergies)
Dilutive deal: Combined EPS < Acquirer EPS alone (before synergies)
```

A deal may be dilutive on a standalone basis but accretive once synergies are included. The board and market will scrutinize this carefully.

## 2. Due Diligence Workstreams

### The DD Organization

Due diligence is the systematic investigation of the target company before committing to a deal. It is managed by the acquirer and its advisors:

**Financial DD (FDD):**
- Led by: big 4 accounting firm (KPMG, EY, Deloitte, PwC) or boutique FDD firm
- Scope: quality of earnings, net debt, working capital, tax exposure, off-balance-sheet items
- Output: FDD report with adjusted EBITDA, net debt definition, and flagged risks

**Legal DD:**
- Led by: external law firm (White & Case, Linklaters, Clifford Chance for large cross-border; Haidermota, Vellani & Vellani, Mohsin Tayebaly for Pakistan)
- Scope: material contracts, litigation, IP, employment, regulatory compliance, corporate structure
- Output: Legal DD report; marked-up SPA provisions

**Tax DD:**
- Led by: tax advisors (often the same big 4 firm as FDD)
- Scope: historic tax filings, open tax years, disputed assessments, deferred tax, transfer pricing
- Pakistan specific: FBR, SRB, provincial tax authority filings; withholding tax on dividends; advance tax recoverable

**Commercial DD:**
- Led by: strategy consultants (McKinsey, Bain, BCG for large deals; local consultants for mid-market) or IB team
- Scope: market size, competitive position, customer concentration, pricing power, growth sustainability
- Output: Commercial DD report validating management's revenue projections

**Technical / Operations DD:**
- Led by: industry-specific engineers or operations experts
- Scope: plant condition, capex requirements, environmental liabilities, technology risk
- Pakistan manufacturing: power infrastructure, machinery age, maintenance backlog

**HR DD:**
- Key management retention risk, pension obligations, employment claims, union agreements

### The Due Diligence Matrix

A well-managed buy-side DD tracks all workstreams in a single matrix:

| Workstream | Advisor | Status | Key Issues | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Financial DD | KPMG | In progress | EBITDA overstated by PKR 200M | Price chip risk |
| Legal DD | Haidermota | Complete | Change-of-control in 2 key customer contracts | Consent required |
| Tax DD | KPMG | In progress | PKR 500M FBR dispute open | Indemnity required |
| Commercial DD | IB team | Complete | Customer concentration (top 3 = 55% revenue) | Risk disclosed |
| Technical DD | Engineering firm | In progress | Furnace requires PKR 150M capex in Year 2 | Capex adjusted |

## 3. Synergy Quantification

### Why Synergies Matter for Bid Pricing

A strategic acquirer can pay more than a financial buyer (PE) because synergies increase the value of the combined entity. The buyer must quantify synergies to:
1. Know the maximum price they can pay while still creating value
2. Justify the acquisition premium to their board and shareholders
3. Price above PE bidders in a competitive auction

### Types of Synergies

**Revenue synergies:**
- Cross-selling: offer the target's products to the acquirer's customers, and vice versa
- Geographic expansion: use the target's distribution to sell the acquirer's products in new regions
- Pricing power: combined entity has more market power

**Cost synergies:**
- Headcount reduction: eliminate duplicate functions (finance, HR, IT, legal)
- Procurement savings: larger combined volumes → better supplier pricing
- Manufacturing: rationalize production across combined plant network
- SG&A: reduce advertising, marketing, and admin overhead
- IT systems: consolidate to single ERP, eliminate duplicate software licenses

### Synergy Modeling

**Cost synergy model structure:**

| Synergy Category | Annual Run-Rate Value (PKR M) | Realization Year | One-Time Cost to Achieve |
|---|---|---|---|
| Headcount (50 FTE × avg PKR 3M) | 150 | Year 1–2 | PKR 200M (severance) |
| Procurement savings (2% of COGS) | 180 | Year 2 | Minimal |
| SG&A consolidation | 80 | Year 2 | PKR 50M (system integration) |
| Facility rationalization | 60 | Year 3 | PKR 100M (lease exit) |
| **Total run-rate synergies** | **470** | — | **PKR 350M integration costs** |

**Synergy PV calculation:**
```
PV of Synergies = Σ [Annual Synergy / (1+WACC)^t] over realization period
Typical synergy PV multiple: 5–8x annual run-rate synergies
```

**Rule of thumb:** Buyers typically share 30–50% of synergy value with the seller through the acquisition premium. If total synergy PV is PKR 2.5B, expect PKR 0.75B–1.25B of that to flow to the seller in higher price.

## 4. Bid Strategy

### Competitive Auction Dynamics

In a competitive auction, the buyer must balance:
- **Price:** High enough to win; low enough to create value
- **Speed:** Quick enough to show commitment; thorough enough to avoid post-close surprises
- **Conditionality:** Fewer conditions = more attractive bid (even at same price)
- **Structure:** Cash is king; earn-outs and shares are discounted by sellers

**First-round strategy:**
- Submit an indicative valuation that puts you in the shortlist (top 3–4 bidders) without overpaying
- Show strategic rationale clearly — sellers want to know you will be a good owner
- Minimize conditionality — financing conditions are a major red flag in first round

**Second-round strategy:**
- Second round is won on two dimensions: price and certainty of close
- Submit the highest price your analysis can justify (synergies included)
- Mark up the SPA as cleanly as possible — limit the buyer-friendly changes you demand
- Offer a tight timeline to exclusivity and signing

### Bid Validation — The "Walk-Away" Price

Before submitting a final bid, determine the walk-away price — the maximum price beyond which the deal destroys value:

```
Walk-away price = Standalone Value of Target + PV of Synergies
                = PKR 38B + PKR 10B = PKR 48B maximum
```

If the auction clearing price exceeds PKR 48B, walk away. Discipline to walk away from overpriced deals is one of the most valuable skills in M&A.

## 5. SPA Financial Terms — Buyer's Perspective

### What the Buyer Negotiates Hard

**Working capital peg:**
The buyer wants the peg set at normalized working capital (12-month average), not the highest recent quarter (which the seller prefers). Every PKR 1B of lower working capital peg = PKR 1B less to the seller at closing.

**Net debt definition:**
The buyer wants to include everything debt-like (pension deficits, disputed tax as debt-like items, letters of credit, deferred consideration). The seller resists. This is one of the most contentious SPA negotiations.

**Representations and warranties:**
The buyer wants broad reps; the seller wants them narrow and heavily qualified. Key reps:
- Financial statements are accurate and prepared in accordance with IFRS
- No undisclosed liabilities
- No material adverse change in the business
- All material contracts are disclosed and in force
- No ongoing litigation not disclosed

**Indemnities:**
For specific identified risks (tax disputes, environmental liabilities, customer claims), buyers demand specific indemnities — dollar-for-dollar coverage if the risk materializes. Sellers resist; buyers push.

**Escrow:**
A portion of the purchase price (typically 10–15%) is held in escrow for 12–24 months to cover warranty claims. Sellers want small escrow, short period; buyers want large, long.

## Self-Assessment

1. You are advising a Gulf FMCG company on its acquisition of a Pakistani packaged food manufacturer through a competitive auction. The target's LTM EBITDA is PKR 3.5B. The auction currently has 6 bidders.

   (a) Set up the DD workstream matrix for this acquisition. Name the advisor for each workstream (you can use generic firm names) and describe the top two issues you would expect each workstream to uncover.
   (b) Your financial DD reveals: (i) PKR 200M of owner-related non-recurring costs that should be added back, and (ii) PKR 150M revenue from a one-time government contract that won't recur. Calculate the adjusted EBITDA, and explain the impact on your bid price vs. the seller's expectation.
   (c) Quantify synergies. The acquirer estimates: 40 FTE eliminated at PKR 3.5M average cost, 1.5% procurement savings on PKR 8B COGS, and PKR 60M SG&A savings. What is the total annual run-rate synergy? If synergies are worth 6x run-rate, what is the total synergy PV?
   (d) The auction's first-round clearing price appears to be PKR 28–30B (EV). Your standalone valuation of the target (at adjusted EBITDA PKR 3.7B × 7.5x) is PKR 27.8B. Should you bid above standalone? What is your maximum bid including synergies (assume you pass through 40% of synergy PV to the seller in price)?

2. In SPA negotiations on a Pakistani industrial acquisition, the following issues remain open:

   | Issue | Seller's Position | Buyer's Position |
   |---|---|---|
   | Working capital peg | PKR 2.8B (peak quarter) | PKR 2.2B (12-month average) |
   | Net debt | Exclude advance income tax recoverable PKR 400M | Include as debt-like item |
   | W&R escrow | 5% of deal value, 12 months | 15% of deal value, 24 months |
   | Tax indemnity | Cap at PKR 200M (50% of FBR dispute) | Full cover for all FBR disputes |

   (a) On the working capital peg: which party's position is commercially more defensible? Propose a compromise.
   (b) On the advance income tax: what is the commercial argument for treating it as debt-like? Is the buyer or seller correct?
   (c) On the escrow: what is the market standard range? Propose a compromise that could close the gap.
   (d) On the tax indemnity: why does the buyer want full cover? What information would help assess the realistic exposure, and how might the parties split the risk?
