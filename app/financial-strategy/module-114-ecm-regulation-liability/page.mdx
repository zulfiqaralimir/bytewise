# Module 114 — ECM Regulation & Prospectus Liability

Every equity capital markets transaction involves legal exposure. The prospectus is a legal document — and if it contains a material misstatement or omission that causes investors to lose money, both the issuing company and the underwriting banks face liability. Understanding the regulatory framework, liability standards, and the defenses available to banks is essential for any ECM professional.

## Learning Objectives

- Explain the prospectus liability standard (material misstatement/omission)
- Understand the due diligence defense available to underwriting banks
- Explain the legal mechanics of the greenshoe option
- Apply SECP prospectus liability framework for Pakistani IPOs
- Understand UAE SCA and Saudi CMA prospectus requirements

---

## 1. Prospectus Liability

### The Material Misstatement Standard

A prospectus contains a material misstatement if it includes a false statement (or omits information) that would affect a reasonable investor's decision to invest. The standard in most jurisdictions:

- The misstatement or omission must be **material** — significant enough to matter to an investor
- The investor must have **relied** on the prospectus
- The investor must have suffered a **loss** as a result

**Who is liable:**
- The issuing company and its directors (primary liability)
- The underwriting banks (secondary liability — for statements they verified or should have verified)
- The auditors (for financial statements included in the prospectus)
- The lawyers (if they drafted the document negligently)

### The Due Diligence Defense

Underwriting banks can defend against liability by demonstrating they conducted reasonable due diligence — that they made reasonable inquiries and had reasonable grounds to believe the prospectus was accurate.

**What due diligence defense requires:**
- Verification of all factual statements in the prospectus
- Management interviews
- Review of material contracts
- Auditor comfort letters (confirming financials are correct)
- Legal opinions on material legal matters
- Expert opinions on technical matters (reserves, environmental)

**Comfort letter process:**
Auditors provide "comfort letters" to underwriting banks confirming:
- Financial statements in the prospectus are consistent with the audited accounts
- No material adverse changes in the financial position since the last audited date
- Specific financial figures are mathematically accurate

### Pakistan Prospectus Liability

Under the Securities Act 2015 (Pakistan):
- The issuer, directors signing the prospectus, and underwriters are jointly liable for material misstatements
- SECP may impose administrative sanctions; criminal liability exists for willful fraud
- Civil liability: investors may sue for losses caused by materially misleading prospectuses

## 2. Greenshoe Option — Legal Mechanics

The greenshoe (over-allotment option) is a contractual right granted by the issuer to the underwriting banks allowing them to sell up to 15% more shares than the IPO size and then purchase additional shares (from the issuer or in the market) to cover this short position.

**Legal structure:**
1. Underwriters sell 115% of IPO shares at offer price
2. Stabilization period begins (30 days)
3. If price falls below offer price: underwriters buy shares in the market → supports price → covers their short
4. If price rises: underwriters exercise the greenshoe (buy new shares from issuer at offer price) → permanently increases IPO size by 15%

**Stabilization disclosure:** All stabilization activity must be disclosed to the exchange. Undisclosed stabilization is market manipulation.

## 3. Gulf ECM Regulation

**UAE — Securities and Commodities Authority (SCA):**
- Regulates IPOs on DFM and ADX
- Prospectus approval: typically 4–6 weeks
- Minimum free float for DFM: 25% for AED 200M+ company

**Saudi Arabia — Capital Markets Authority (CMA):**
- Tadawul IPO prospectus reviewed by CMA
- Strict disclosure requirements; one of the most demanding in the region
- NOMU (parallel market): lighter touch for smaller companies
