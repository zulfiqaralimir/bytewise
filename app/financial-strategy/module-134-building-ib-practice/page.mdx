# Module 134 — Building an IB Practice in Pakistan & Gulf

Most of this course has been about being an IB professional — advising clients, structuring deals, executing transactions. This module addresses a different question: what does it take to build an IB firm? Whether you are thinking about starting a boutique advisory practice in Karachi, setting up a DIFC-licensed firm in Dubai, or joining a regional bank in an IB build-out role, understanding the business of investment banking — licensing, team economics, client development, and competitive positioning — is essential.

## Learning Objectives

- Understand SECP NBFC-IB and DFSA/FSRA licensing requirements
- Structure an IB team and model deal economics from a business perspective
- Build a client development strategy for a Pakistan or Gulf IB boutique
- Compare DIFC vs. ADGM as IB operating jurisdictions
- Develop a competitive positioning strategy for a regional boutique

---

## 1. Regulatory Licensing

### Pakistan — SECP NBFC-IB License

To operate as an Investment Finance Service (IFS) or Investment Banking Company in Pakistan, a firm must be licensed by SECP as a Non-Banking Finance Company (NBFC):

**License categories relevant to IB:**
- **Investment Finance Services (IFS):** Full-service IB including advisory, ECM, DCM
- **Broker-Dealer License:** Required for placing shares (ECM) and bond underwriting
- **Research Analyst License:** Required for publishing equity research

**Minimum paid-up capital requirements (SECP NBFC Regulations 2008, as amended):**
- Investment Finance Services: PKR 300–500M minimum paid-up capital
- This is the primary barrier to entry for Pakistan IB boutiques

**Ongoing regulatory requirements:**
- SECP annual returns and financial statements
- Net Capital Adequacy maintenance
- Fit-and-proper criteria for key executives
- Compliance officer appointment mandatory

**Practical reality:** Most Pakistan IB boutiques are licensed as advisory firms (not requiring SECP NBFC capital requirements) and partner with licensed broker-dealers for the execution/placement function.

### Gulf — DFSA Licensing (DIFC)

The DFSA licenses financial services in the DIFC under the Financial Services and Markets Rulebook (FSM).

**Relevant DFSA license categories for IB:**
- **Category 3A (Arranging):** Arranging deals/transactions; most IB advisory firms use this
- **Category 3B (Arranging — Bringing Issues to Market):** Covers ECM/DCM deal origination
- **Category 4 (Advising):** Corporate finance advisory; M&A and strategic advisory

**DFSA requirements for Category 3A IB license:**
- Base Capital: $500,000 (Category 3A)
- Approved Persons: Principals and senior managers must be individually approved by DFSA
- Compliance and Risk Officer: Required roles
- Audited financial statements annually
- Office in DIFC (can be a small, serviced office)

**Practical advantage of DFSA:** International recognition; counterparties and clients globally recognize DFSA-regulated entities; enables access to DIFC's common law courts for dispute resolution.

### ADGM — FSRA Licensing

Similar to DFSA but Abu Dhabi-based:
- Category 3A equivalent (Arranging)
- FSRA-approved persons requirements
- Base capital: $140,000 for Category 3A (lower than DFSA)
- Abu Dhabi entities: PIF, Mubadala, ADIA more likely to engage ADGM-licensed firms
- Growing competitiveness vs. DIFC for FinTech and asset management

## 2. IB Team Structure and Economics

### Minimum Viable IB Team

A boutique IB practice can operate with a small but high-quality team:

**5-person boutique (year 1–2):**
| Role | Responsibility | Seniority |
|---|---|---|
| Managing Partner / MD | Deal origination, client relationships, deal leadership | Senior (10–15 years) |
| Senior Advisor / VP | Deal execution, modeling, client management | Mid (5–8 years) |
| Associate | Modeling, pitch books, due diligence | Junior (2–4 years) |
| Analyst | Data, research, formatting, admin | Entry (1–2 years) |
| Operations/Compliance | Regulatory filings, admin, compliance | Specialist |

**Scaling to 15-person firm (year 3–5):**
Add a second MD (revenue-generating) and a second VP/Associate team.

### Deal Economics

**Revenue model for a Pakistan M&A boutique:**
- Typical M&A fee: 1.5–2.5% of deal value for mid-market deals (PKR 1–10B)
- Retainer: PKR 500K–2M/month (credited against completion fee)
- Minimum fee: PKR 10–20M per deal

**Revenue example — 3 deals/year:**
- Deal 1: PKR 3B sell-side → 2% fee = PKR 60M
- Deal 2: PKR 5B buy-side → 1.5% fee = PKR 75M
- Deal 3: PKR 1.5B ECM (rights issue) → 1.5% = PKR 22.5M
- **Total revenue: PKR 157.5M**

**Cost structure (5-person team):**
- Salaries + bonuses: PKR 60–80M/year
- Office and technology: PKR 5–8M/year
- SECP/legal/compliance: PKR 5M/year
- **Total costs: ~PKR 70–93M**
- **EBITDA: PKR 64–87M (40–55% margin)**

**Gulf boutique economics are similar but in USD:**
3 deals at $2–5M fees each = $6–15M revenue; team costs $3–6M.

## 3. Client Development Strategy

### Building a Pakistan IB Client Pipeline

**Tier 1: Existing relationships**
Your first deals come from your personal network. Every IB professional building a boutique must identify:
- CFOs and CEOs at listed companies from previous advisory work
- Private equity firms that need local execution support
- Family business groups considering transactions

**Tier 2: Systematic outreach**
- Monthly market intelligence notes to 50 target CFOs (market updates, relevant deal news)
- ICAP events attendance and speaking slots
- PSX publications and commentary (builds credibility)

**Tier 3: Cross-border relationships**
- Partner with regional banks (Arab Bank, Al Rajhi, Standard Chartered Pakistan) for cross-referral
- Relationships with Dubai-based advisors for Pakistan inbound deal flow
- Connect with KPMG/PWC TAS teams for Big 4 referrals

### Gulf Client Development

**In the Gulf, reputation-building is different:**
- DIFC events and Gulf Capital Markets Association participation
- Hosting family offices and HNWI roundtables on investment themes
- Track record publication (press releases for completed deals)
- Sovereign fund relationships require multiple years of cultivation before mandates

## 4. DIFC vs. ADGM: Choosing Your Hub

| Factor | DIFC | ADGM |
|---|---|---|
| Jurisdiction | Dubai (Emirate of Dubai) | Abu Dhabi |
| Regulator | DFSA | FSRA |
| Base Capital (Cat 3A) | $500,000 | $140,000 |
| Client proximity | Dubai-based family offices, PE, hedge funds | ADIA, Mubadala, Abu Dhabi SWFs |
| Deal flow access | Consumer, real estate, logistics, tech | Energy, sovereign, infrastructure |
| International recognition | Higher (longer-established) | Growing |
| Office space costs | Higher (DIFC premium) | Lower |

**Recommendation for a Pakistan-focused boutique:** DIFC is the better first choice — larger deal volume, stronger international recognition, and the largest Pakistani finance community in the UAE is Dubai-based.

## 5. Competitive Positioning

### How to Compete Against Bulge Brackets

A 5-person boutique cannot win a $5B M&A mandate against Goldman Sachs. But it can dominate the mid-market advisory space by:

**Specialization:**
- "The Pakistan FMCG M&A specialist" — deep sector expertise and relationships the bulge brackets lack
- "The Pakistan-Gulf cross-border specialist" — bilateral deal flow both directions

**Speed and attention:**
- Boutiques can deliver faster, more tailored advice
- Senior banker attention on every deal (vs. junior-heavy at bulge brackets)

**Relationships > brand:**
- In Pakistan, CFOs often choose advisors they trust personally over brand names
- Build the relationship before the deal arrives

**Positioning statement example:**
> "We are a Karachi-based advisory boutique focused exclusively on mid-market M&A, ECM, and DCM transactions for Pakistani companies and Gulf investors looking at Pakistan. We bring senior advisor attention to every mandate, deep local market knowledge, and Pakistan-Gulf cross-border execution capability."
