# Module 105 — Sell-Side M&A Process

A sell-side M&A process is one of the most complex, high-stakes projects in investment banking. Run properly, it creates competitive tension among bidders, maximizes the price received by the seller, and manages the transaction to a clean close. Run poorly, it leaks confidential information, frightens employees and customers, and destroys the very value it was meant to realize. This module covers the full sell-side process from mandate to closing.

## Learning Objectives

- Design a sell-side M&A process (broad auction vs. targeted)
- Execute the preparation phase (management presentation, data room, IM)
- Run a two-round bid process with NDAs, process letters, and management meetings
- Navigate exclusivity, SPA negotiation, and closing conditions
- Understand Pakistan SECP/Competition Commission regulatory approvals
- Manage the sell-side process for Gulf M&A transactions

---

## 1. Process Design Decisions

### Broad Auction vs. Targeted Process

The first decision is how widely to run the process:

**Broad auction (20–40+ bidders contacted):**
- Maximum competitive tension → highest price
- High information leakage risk — many parties aware of the sale
- Management distraction (many management presentations)
- Best for: large transactions, motivated seller wanting maximum value, low confidentiality sensitivity

**Targeted process (5–10 bidders):**
- More confidential — fewer parties in the process
- Preserves key relationships (employees, customers, suppliers don't find out)
- Less competitive tension
- Best for: strategic transactions, seller with specific buyer preferences, sensitive business

**Bilateral negotiation (single buyer approach):**
- No competitive tension; price risk for seller
- Maximum confidentiality; fastest execution
- Best for: exclusive relationships, government-directed transactions, highly sensitive businesses, situations where seller already has a preferred buyer

**Pakistan M&A process reality:**
Pakistan's M&A market is smaller and less institutionalized than Gulf or Western markets. "Broad auctions" in Pakistan typically contact 10–15 parties, not 40+. Many deals are effectively bilateral or targeted because the buyer universe is thin.

## 2. Preparation Phase (Weeks 1–8)

### Preparing the Company for Sale

Before contacting any buyers, the banker and client prepare:

**Financial preparation:**
- Audited financial statements for last 3 years (convert to IFRS if not already)
- Management accounts (monthly data, current year)
- QoE analysis (Module 96) — identify and normalize EBITDA adjustments before buyers do
- 5-year management projections (with assumptions clearly stated)
- Net debt calculation and supporting schedule

**Legal preparation:**
- Material contracts review (customer, supplier, employment, lease)
- Identify change-of-control provisions in key contracts (clauses that trigger renegotiation or termination on acquisition)
- Litigation and contingency review
- IP and technology ownership verification

**Data room preparation (VDR — Virtual Data Room):**
The VDR is an online secure document repository where all deal documents are stored. Leading providers: Datasite (formerly Merrill), Intralinks, Ansarada, ShareVault.

VDR organization by section:
1. Corporate (incorporation documents, cap table)
2. Financial (audited accounts, management accounts, projections)
3. Legal (material contracts, litigation)
4. Operations (facilities, capex schedule, production data)
5. HR (org chart, key employee contracts, pension)
6. IP / technology
7. Regulatory / compliance
8. Real estate

**Pakistan VDR reality:** Pakistani companies often have poor document organization. A significant portion of prep time is document collection and digitization. Tax documentation in particular is often scattered across multiple FBR and provincial tax authority filings.

### The Information Memorandum (IM)

The IM (also called the Confidential Information Memorandum or CIM) is the primary marketing document sent to prospective buyers after they sign an NDA. It is typically 60–120 pages:

**IM Structure:**
1. Executive summary (investment highlights, deal overview)
2. Business overview (history, products/services, customers, operations)
3. Market overview (industry size, growth, competitive landscape)
4. Financial overview (historical financials, key metrics, LTM, projections)
5. Investment highlights and value creation opportunities
6. Transaction overview (process timeline, contact information)

**Teaser:** A 2–4 page anonymous summary distributed before NDA signing to gauge interest. Does not name the company; describes it generically ("a leading Pakistani FMCG company with PKR X revenue and Y% margins").

## 3. Phase 1: First Round Bids (Weeks 9–16)

### NDA and Process Letter

Interested parties sign an NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement) and receive the IM and a process letter.

**Process letter specifies:**
- First round bid deadline
- Required bid format (indicative valuation, deal structure, preliminary due diligence questions, financing sources)
- Management presentation schedule
- What happens next (second round vs. exclusivity)

**Management presentations:** For serious first-round bidders, a 2–3 hour presentation by management (CEO, CFO, COO) covering the business. Held at a neutral location (the IB firm's offices). Q&A session follows.

### Evaluating First Round Bids

After first round bids are received, the seller's advisor evaluates:

| Criterion | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| Valuation | Headline number; EV vs. equity value distinction |
| Conditionality | How many conditions? Financing condition? MAC condition? |
| Structure | Cash vs. stock vs. earn-out |
| Due diligence requirements | Extensive DD = more risk of price chip at second round |
| Counterparty quality | Can this buyer actually close? (Financial capacity, regulatory) |
| Timeline | How quickly can they move to close? |

**Shortlisting:** Typically 3–6 bidders are invited to second round from first round submissions.

## 4. Phase 2: Second Round Bids (Weeks 17–22)

### Enhanced Due Diligence

Second-round participants receive:
- Full VDR access
- Management Q&A sessions (written and verbal)
- Site visits (facilities, operations)
- Expert sessions (auditors, legal counsel for buyer questions)

**Q&A process:** All buyer questions are submitted in writing through the VDR. Answers are provided to all second-round participants simultaneously (to prevent information asymmetry between bidders).

### The Bid Process Letter (Second Round)

Second round process letter specifies:
- Binding bid deadline (unlike first round which is indicative)
- Required submissions: final valuation, marked-up SPA (the buyer marks up the seller's draft SPA with their proposed changes), financing commitment letter (for PE buyers), regulatory approvals plan
- Exclusivity will be granted to the best bidder

### Evaluating Final Bids

**Price:** Obviously critical, but not the only factor.

**SPA mark-up:** A buyer who marks up the draft SPA extensively (proposes many favorable changes) creates execution risk. Compare mark-ups across bidders — the "cleanest" SPA (fewest buyer-friendly changes) is valuable.

**Conditionality:** A bid with fewer conditions (no financing condition, limited MAC definition) is more certain to close.

**Regulatory:** A buyer who requires many regulatory approvals introduces timeline and completion risk.

## 5. Exclusivity and SPA Negotiation (Weeks 22–28)

### Granting Exclusivity

The seller grants exclusivity to one bidder for a specified period (typically 4–8 weeks) to negotiate the final SPA. During exclusivity:
- No other buyers may be contacted
- The buyer completes final due diligence
- SPA is negotiated and finalized

**Exclusivity risk:** If the buyer discovers material issues during final DD, they may demand a price reduction (chip) or walk away. The seller's leverage diminishes once exclusive.

**Pakistan exclusivity dynamics:** Pakistani sellers often resist long exclusivity periods (4 weeks maximum is common). However, buyers need exclusivity to justify final DD investment (legal fees, accounting fees, management time).

### SPA Key Terms

The Share Purchase Agreement is the definitive legal document. Key financial provisions:

**Purchase price mechanics:**
- Fixed price vs. completion accounts (final price adjusted for actual net debt and working capital at closing)
- Locked box mechanism (price fixed at a historical balance sheet date; economic risk transfers from that date)

**Conditions precedent (CPs):**
- Regulatory approvals (Competition Commission of Pakistan approval, SECP, SBP for financial sector)
- Third-party consents (change-of-control clauses in major contracts)
- No material adverse change (MAC) — buyer can walk if business deteriorates materially before close

**Representations and warranties:**
Seller makes detailed factual statements about the business. If false → buyer has a claim.

**Indemnities:**
Specific protections for known risks (tax disputes, litigation) where seller provides specific financial coverage.

**W&I Insurance:** In larger transactions (typically $50M+), Warranty & Indemnity insurance is purchased to cover rep & warranty claims. Becoming more common in Gulf transactions; rare but growing in Pakistan.

## 6. Regulatory Approvals — Pakistan

### Competition Commission of Pakistan (CCP)

Mergers, acquisitions, and amalgamations that meet the threshold must be notified to the CCP:
- Threshold: combined turnover of PKR 1B or more (as of recent rules)
- Timeline: 30 days for Phase 1 review; Phase 2 up to 90 additional days
- Most transactions clear in Phase 1 if there is no significant market concentration concern

### SECP

For acquisitions of PSX-listed companies:
- SECP Takeover Regulations 2017: acquisition of 25%+ requires a mandatory public offer (MTO) to all shareholders
- Price for MTO: the higher of (a) deal price and (b) 6-month VWAP of the target's shares
- SECP must approve the offer document

### SBP Approval

Required for acquisitions of banks, leasing companies, and other financial institutions. SBP's fit-and-proper criteria apply to major shareholders.

### Foreign Investment Approvals

- Board of Investment (BOI): facilitates foreign investment approvals
- Sector-specific restrictions: foreign ownership limits in media, banking
- Repatriation: capital gains and dividends are repatriable under Investment Policy 2013

## Self-Assessment

1. You are advising the sale of a Pakistani pharmaceutical company (PKR 35B enterprise value). Design the sell-side process:

   (a) Should you run a broad auction, targeted process, or bilateral negotiation? Justify your recommendation based on the specific characteristics of the pharmaceutical sector and Pakistan's M&A market.
   (b) Draft the one-page executive summary for the teaser document. Include: anonymous company description, financial highlights (PKR 20B revenue, PKR 4.8B EBITDA, 24% margin, 15% 3-year CAGR), and process description. Do NOT name the company or its specific products.
   (c) You receive 8 first-round bids. Three bidders offered PKR 45–48B, two offered PKR 38–42B, and three offered PKR 30–35B. How would you structure the shortlist for second round, and what criteria would you use beyond price?
   (d) A first-round bidder (a Gulf strategic acquirer) says: "We don't need Phase 2 — we want to go directly to exclusivity based on our PKR 47B first-round bid." Advise the client on whether to accept this. What does the seller gain and lose by going to exclusivity early?

2. A Pakistani industrial company's SPA negotiation has reached an impasse on two issues:

   **Issue 1:** The buyer wants a broad MAC (Material Adverse Change) clause that allows them to walk away if the company's EBITDA falls more than 10% before closing. The seller wants to limit the MAC to events outside the ordinary course of business.

   **Issue 2:** The buyer discovered during final DD that the company has an outstanding FBR tax dispute of PKR 500M (not fully disclosed in the IM). The buyer is demanding PKR 500M be escrowed from the purchase price until the dispute is resolved.

   (a) On Issue 1: What is the commercial logic of each party's position on the MAC clause? What is the market standard for MAC provisions in M&A transactions?
   (b) On Issue 2: Was the seller obligated to disclose the FBR dispute in the IM? Under what circumstances might the non-disclosure constitute a warranty breach?
   (c) Propose a compromise solution to each issue that could break the impasse and allow the deal to proceed.
   (d) The buyer's lawyer says: "If we don't resolve these in 3 days, we will withdraw." How do you respond as the seller's IB advisor?
