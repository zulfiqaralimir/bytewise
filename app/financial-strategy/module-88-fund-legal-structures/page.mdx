# Module 88 — Fund Legal Structures

The legal structure of a fund is not just paperwork — it determines who can invest, how profits are distributed, what liabilities arise, and how the fund is regulated. A poorly structured fund can cost millions in unnecessary tax, restrict the investor base to local money, or expose the manager to personal liability for fund losses. FERROQUANT's legal architecture must serve three masters simultaneously: investor protection, operational efficiency, and regulatory compliance across Pakistan, the UAE/Gulf, and international jurisdictions.

## Learning Objectives

- Explain the key characteristics of Cayman Islands limited partnerships and their dominance in hedge fund structuring
- Understand BVI funds, DIFC structures, and how Gulf-domiciled fund structures work
- Navigate Pakistan's NBFC and AMC frameworks under SECP regulation
- Explain master-feeder structures and why they exist
- Understand the core provisions of Limited Partnership Agreements (LPAs) and other governing documents
- Analyze GP/LP economics: management fees, performance fees, hurdle rates, and high-water marks
- Apply fund structure selection logic to FERROQUANT's investor base and strategy

## 1. Why Fund Structure Matters

### Legal Structure Determines Everything

**Investor access:** A Cayman fund can accept investors from 50+ countries; a Pakistan NBFC is restricted to Pakistani residents. The wrong structure limits the capital pool.

**Tax efficiency:** A Cayman limited partnership is tax-transparent — investors are taxed in their home jurisdictions, not twice (once in Cayman and once at home). An improperly structured fund can trigger double taxation.

**Liability protection:** In a limited partnership, limited partners (investors) have liability limited to their invested capital. The general partner (fund manager) bears unlimited liability — but this is typically held through a corporate GP entity to limit personal exposure.

**Regulatory access:** Institutional investors (pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, endowments) often have mandates that restrict investment to regulated fund structures (UCITS, SEC-registered, AIFMD-compliant).

## 2. Cayman Islands Limited Partnership

### Why Cayman Dominates Hedge Fund Structuring

The Cayman Islands is the default domicile for international hedge funds. ~75% of global hedge fund AUM is structured through Cayman vehicles. Reasons:

1. **Tax neutrality:** No corporate, capital gains, or withholding taxes in Cayman (investors pay tax in their home countries based on their tax status)
2. **Legal flexibility:** Cayman law allows flexible economic arrangements between GP and LPs — custom fee structures, side pockets, series structures
3. **Speed of formation:** A Cayman exempted limited partnership (ELP) can be formed in 2–3 weeks
4. **International recognition:** Most institutional investors' legal teams and compliance departments approve Cayman structures without additional due diligence friction

### Cayman Fund Structures

**Exempted Limited Partnership (ELP):**
The most common structure. Partners are "exempted" from economic substance rules because the fund's clients are offshore.

```
General Partner (GP)
  [Manager entity — typically a Cayman or Delaware LLC]
        ↓
Cayman ELP (the Fund)
  ↑ Limited Partners (LPs)
  [Investors — no management control, limited liability]
```

**Segregated Portfolio Company (SPC):**
A Cayman corporate structure where different "portfolios" (similar to sub-funds) have legally segregated assets and liabilities. One portfolio's losses cannot reach another. Used for multi-strategy funds with different risk profiles.

**Unit Trust:**
Alternative to LP structure. Investors hold units in a trust rather than LP interests. Common in some Asia-Pacific jurisdictions. Less common for hedge funds.

### Formation Documents

**Limited Partnership Agreement (LPA):**
The core governing document. Covers:
- Capital contributions and withdrawal rights
- Management fee and performance fee calculation
- GP/LP governance rights
- Investment strategy and restrictions
- Transfer restrictions on LP interests
- Dissolution and winding-up provisions

**Subscription Agreement:**
Investor-specific agreement: representations about investor eligibility (sophisticated investor, not US person if offshore fund, AML/KYC documentation), investment amount, and acknowledgment of risk.

**Private Placement Memorandum (PPM) / Offering Memorandum (OM):**
Disclosure document for potential investors. Describes the strategy, risks, fees, team, and legal structure. Not a marketing document — it is a legal document designed to ensure investors understand what they're getting into.

## 3. BVI Funds

### BVI Investment Business

The British Virgin Islands (BVI) offers a faster, cheaper fund structuring option than Cayman:
- **BVI Private Fund:** Less than 50 investors, incubator/emerging manager funds; minimal regulatory requirements
- **BVI Professional Fund:** Minimum $100,000 per investor; slightly more regulated than private fund
- **Cost:** BVI formation is cheaper than Cayman (~$5K vs. $20K+); annual maintenance is also lower

**BVI vs. Cayman:**
| Feature | BVI | Cayman |
|---|---|---|
| Formation cost | ~$5K–10K | ~$20K–40K |
| Annual maintenance | ~$3K | ~$8K–15K |
| Investor acceptance | Good | Excellent |
| Legal flexibility | Good | Excellent |
| Institutional preference | Growing | Strong |

BVI is preferred for smaller funds, incubators, and early-stage managers. As AUM grows, many funds "re-domicile" to Cayman to access larger institutional capital.

## 4. DIFC Fund Structures (Dubai International Financial Centre)

### DIFC as a Fund Domicile

The Dubai International Financial Centre (DIFC) is a financial free zone with its own legal system (English common law), regulator (DFSA — Dubai Financial Services Authority), and courts.

**Why DIFC for a Pakistan/Gulf-focused fund:**
- Proximity to Gulf investors (ADIA, Mubadala, QIA, family offices)
- DIFC funds can be marketed to GCC investors without FATCA/CRS complications that US structures create
- English common law framework — familiar to international investors
- DFSA is respected internationally; DIFC regulatory approval increases investor confidence

**DIFC Fund Types:**

**Exempt Fund:**
- Available to Professional Clients only (minimum $500K net assets or professional experience)
- No minimum capital requirement for investors
- Regulated by DFSA, registered with DIFC Registrar of Companies
- Fast setup: 4–6 weeks

**Qualified Investor Fund (QIF):**
- Available only to Qualified Investors ($1M+ investable assets or institutional)
- Highest flexibility — minimal regulatory constraints
- Most hedge funds at DIFC are structured as QIFs
- Can be structured as an LP, company, or unit trust

**Public Fund:**
- Retail investors permitted
- Much heavier regulation (UCITS-equivalent disclosure requirements)
- Not appropriate for hedge funds; typically mutual funds

**ADGM (Abu Dhabi Global Market):**
Similar to DIFC but located in Abu Dhabi. ADGM hosts many larger sovereign wealth fund-focused structures. Abu Dhabi's proximity to ADIA/Mubadala makes ADGM preferred for SWF-backed funds.

## 5. Pakistan Fund Structures: SECP Framework

### Asset Management Company (AMC) Framework

**For managing local mutual funds (open-end and closed-end):**
- Requires an AMC License from SECP (Non-Banking Finance Company Rules 2003)
- Minimum paid-up capital: PKR 200M (approximately USD 700K)
- Separate trustee (a scheduled bank) holds fund assets — the AMC manages but cannot hold assets
- Permitted fund types:
  - **Open-end mutual fund (Collective Investment Scheme):** Daily NAV, investor can redeem at any time
  - **Closed-end fund (Listed):** Fixed capital, listed on PSX, investors trade shares in the secondary market
  - **Voluntary Pension System (VPS) fund:** Retirement savings, tax-advantaged

**NBFC-IM (Investment Manager) License:**
For managing a wider range of funds including discretionary mandates and offshore structures:
- Minimum capital: PKR 30M for basic IM license; higher for AUM thresholds
- Allows managing separate managed accounts (SMAs) for institutional and HNWI clients
- Allows managing closed-end funds and private equity funds with SECP approval

**Pakistan Alternative Investment Fund (AIF):**
- SECP introduced AIF framework in 2018 to allow hedge fund-type structures
- Private Placement → minimum PKR 10M per investor
- Eligible investments: equities, fixed income, derivatives, real estate
- Leverage allowed: up to 100% of NAV (lower than international norm)
- AIF managers require NBFC-IM license with additional AIF endorsement

### Trustee Requirement

Pakistan's fund framework requires a **registered trustee** (a scheduled bank) to hold fund assets separately from the AMC:
- HBL, UBL, MCB, Standard Chartered Pakistan act as trustees for major funds
- Trustee ensures assets are legally owned by the fund (not the manager), protecting investors from manager fraud
- Trustee fee: 0.1–0.25% of AUM per annum

## 6. Master-Feeder Structures

### Why Master-Feeder?

Large international funds often have two categories of investors with incompatible requirements:
- **US taxable investors:** Want a US-structured vehicle (Delaware LP) for US tax compliance
- **US tax-exempt and non-US investors:** Want an offshore vehicle (Cayman) to avoid unnecessary US taxes and reporting

**The problem:** Operating two separate funds (US and Cayman) doubles operational costs — two sets of books, two administrators, two auditors.

**The solution — Master-Feeder:**
```
US Feeder (Delaware LP)         Offshore Feeder (Cayman ELP)
  [US taxable investors]          [US tax-exempt + non-US investors]
          ↓                               ↓
          ↓                               ↓
          └───────────────────────────────┘
                              ↓
               Master Fund (Cayman ELP)
               [All trading happens here]
                              ↓
                  Prime Broker / Markets
```

**How it works:**
1. Both feeders invest 100% of their capital into the master fund
2. All trading, risk management, and P&L generation happens at the master level
3. Profits and losses flow back to each feeder proportionally to their share of the master
4. Each feeder handles its own investor-specific tax and regulatory requirements

**FERROQUANT structure:**
```
FERROQUANT Pakistan Feeder (SECP AIF)
  [Pakistani domestic investors — HNWI, institutions]
          ↓
          ↓
FERROQUANT DIFC Feeder (QIF)
  [Gulf/GCC investors — family offices, SWFs]
          ↓
          ↓
          └──────────────────────────────────┘
                          ↓
             FERROQUANT Master (Cayman ELP)
             [All trading and risk management]
```

This structure lets FERROQUANT serve both Pakistani domestic investors (through SECP compliance) and Gulf investors (through DIFC QIF structure) while managing one portfolio centrally.

## 7. GP/LP Economics

### Management Fee

**Standard:** "2 and 20" — 2% management fee and 20% performance fee.

**Current reality:** Fee compression has pushed the industry toward ~1.5% management fee and 15–20% performance fee for most new funds. Top-performing funds with strong track records can still command 2 and 20 or higher.

**Management fee calculation:**
```
Annual management fee = 1.5% × NAV (charged monthly as 1/12 of annual rate)
```

Management fee is charged regardless of performance. It covers the fund's operating costs (team salaries, technology, office) and provides the manager's base income.

### Performance Fee (Carried Interest)

**Standard structure:**
```
Performance fee = 20% × Profit above high-water mark (and above hurdle rate if applicable)
```

**High-water mark (HWM):** The manager only earns performance fees on new profits. If the fund falls from $100M to $80M, the manager earns no performance fee until the fund recovers above $100M.

**Purpose of HWM:** Aligns interests — the manager is incentivized to recover losses before earning additional performance fees. Without a HWM, a manager could simply shut a losing fund and start a new one to reset the fee clock.

**Hurdle rate:** Some funds add a hurdle — the manager only earns performance fees on returns above a minimum threshold (e.g., 8% per year or KIBOR).

**Example:**
- Fund NAV at start of year: PKR 2B
- Hurdle rate: 10%
- High-water mark: PKR 2B (no outstanding losses)
- End of year NAV (before fees): PKR 2.5B (+25% gross)
- Gross profit: PKR 500M
- Profit above hurdle (10% × PKR 2B = PKR 200M): PKR 300M
- Performance fee = 20% × PKR 300M = PKR 60M
- Management fee = 1.5% × PKR 2.25B (average NAV) = PKR 33.75M
- Net profit to investors = PKR 500M − PKR 60M − PKR 33.75M = PKR 406.25M
- Net return to investors: 406.25/2000 = +20.3% (vs. 25% gross)

### Side Pockets

A **side pocket** is a separate accounting unit within a fund for illiquid investments.

**Problem:** A fund with mostly liquid assets (equities, bonds) may also hold some illiquid assets (private equity stakes, distressed debt, real estate). If investors redeem, the fund must sell liquid assets to meet redemptions, leaving remaining investors stuck with a disproportionate share of the illiquid assets — the "liquidity mismatch" problem.

**Solution:** Place the illiquid asset into a side pocket:
- New investors do not participate in the side pocket
- Redeeming investors leave their side pocket allocation behind until the asset is liquidated
- When the illiquid asset is sold, proceeds flow to the side pocket participants

**Risk of abuse:** Side pockets can be misused — managers may place underperforming assets in side pockets to avoid crystallizing losses in the main fund. Governance requirements: independent valuation, LP approval, and disclosure.
