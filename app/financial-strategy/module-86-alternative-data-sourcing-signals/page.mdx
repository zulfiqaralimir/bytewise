# Module 86 — Alternative Data Sourcing & Signals

Alternative data is any data that isn't the traditional financial data inputs — price, volume, earnings, and balance sheets. Hedge funds that exploit alternative data gain an informational edge over those relying only on conventional sources. The arms race for alpha has pushed quant funds toward satellite imagery, credit card transactions, web traffic, social media sentiment, and hundreds of other non-traditional data sources. FERROQUANT's ability to generate alpha in Pakistan's markets will depend partly on finding and extracting signal from data that its competitors aren't using yet.

## Learning Objectives

- Classify alternative data by type, provider, and investment use case
- Evaluate data vendors using a systematic due diligence framework
- Build a data cleaning and signal extraction pipeline
- Understand the legal and compliance risks of alternative data use
- Apply alternative data to Pakistan and Gulf equity markets
- Construct and backtest an alternative data signal without look-ahead bias

## 1. Alternative Data Taxonomy

### The Four Major Categories

**Category 1: Individuals Data**
Generated by people going about their lives — spending, moving, communicating.

| Data Type | Source | Investment Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Credit/debit card transactions | Aggregated by banks (Yodlee, Second Measure) | Retail sales prediction before earnings |
| Mobile app usage | App stores, mobile analytics (Apptopia, Sensor Tower) | Adoption trends for tech companies |
| Geolocation/foot traffic | Mobile phone pings (Placer.ai, SafeGraph) | Store traffic, restaurant visits |
| Social media sentiment | Twitter/X, Reddit, news (StockTwits, Bloomberg NLP) | Earnings surprise prediction, meme stock dynamics |
| Job postings | LinkedIn, Indeed, Glassdoor | Hiring trends as leading growth indicator |

**Category 2: Business Processes Data**
Generated by companies as they conduct business operations.

| Data Type | Source | Investment Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Supply chain shipping | Bill of lading data (ImportGenius, Panjiva) | Trade volume, supply chain disruptions |
| Corporate credit card spend | Commercial card processors | Corporate capex, hiring |
| Web scraping (product pricing) | Self-scraped e-commerce sites | Inflation indicators, retailer margin data |
| Patent filings | USPTO, EPO, Pakistani IP Office | Innovation pipeline, R&D investment |
| App store reviews | Apple/Google Play | Product quality, customer satisfaction |

**Category 3: Sensors / Imagery Data**
Collected by physical sensors, satellites, or IoT devices.

| Data Type | Source | Investment Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Satellite imagery (optical) | Planet Labs, Maxar, Airbus | Oil tank inventory, crop yield, construction activity |
| AIS ship tracking | MarineTraffic, ExactEarth | Commodity shipment volumes (oil tankers, LNG) |
| Night light imagery | NASA/NOAA | Economic activity proxy for regions without GDP data |
| Weather data | NOAA, DTN, Pakistan Met | Agricultural yield forecast |
| Air quality/pollution | OpenAQ, satellite sensors | Industrial activity proxy |

**Category 4: Alternative Web Data**
Collected from public online sources.

| Data Type | Source | Investment Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Web traffic | SimilarWeb, Alexa | Revenue trends for internet companies |
| Google Search trends | Google Trends | Retail demand, economic sentiment |
| Online job listings | IndeedAPI, LinkedIn | Sector hiring trends |
| Product reviews/ratings | Amazon, Yelp, Trustpilot | Consumer satisfaction leading indicator |
| SEC/regulatory filings | EDGAR, Bloomberg | Regulatory risk, material disclosures |

## 2. Data Vendor Evaluation Framework

### The Five-Dimension Due Diligence

Before paying for alternative data, evaluate rigorously on five dimensions:

**1. Data Quality**
- **Coverage:** What % of the universe (companies, geographies, time periods) is covered? Survivorship bias if only currently active entities are in the data?
- **Freshness:** How quickly after events is the data available? A 30-day delay makes many signals useless.
- **Accuracy:** Has the provider done any verification? What is the error rate?
- **Consistency:** Are historical data points reliable, or have definitions changed?

**2. Signal Strength**
- **Historical test:** Does the data actually correlate with the target variable (future returns, earnings surprises)?
- **Predictive horizon:** Is the signal useful 1 day, 1 week, or 1 month in advance?
- **Decay rate:** How quickly does the signal degrade as more firms start using it?

**3. Legal/Compliance Risk**
- **Data sourcing:** Is the data legally obtained? (Critical — see Section 4)
- **MNPI (Material Non-Public Information):** Could this data constitute insider information?
- **GDPR/privacy compliance:** Is personal data properly anonymized and consented?
- **Terms of service:** Does scraping or aggregating this data violate website ToS?

**4. Cost vs. Alpha**
- Annual data cost: $10K–$5M+ depending on the data type
- Estimated signal alpha: How much additional return does this data generate?
- Payback period: At what AUM does this data become cost-effective?
- Exclusivity: Is this data available to all hedge funds or exclusively licensed?

**5. Operational Feasibility**
- **Data format:** API, SFTP, CSV? Can it integrate with your data pipeline?
- **Update frequency:** Daily, hourly, real-time?
- **Data volume:** Terabytes of satellite imagery vs. a spreadsheet — very different infrastructure requirements
- **Vendor reliability:** Is the provider financially stable? What is the contract/data continuity risk?

## 3. Data Cleaning and Signal Construction Pipeline

### The Alternative Data Pipeline

```
Raw Data Ingestion
      ↓
Data Quality Checks (completeness, anomaly detection)
      ↓
Normalization and Standardization
      ↓
Universe Alignment (match to investment universe)
      ↓
Signal Construction (statistical transformation)
      ↓
Signal Evaluation (IC, decay, correlation with other signals)
      ↓
Risk-Adjusted Signal Combination
      ↓
Backtesting (survivorship-bias-free universe)
      ↓
Live Trading with monitoring
```

### Key Data Cleaning Steps

**1. Handle missing data:**
- Forward-fill (use last known value) for slow-moving signals (satellite data, monthly surveys)
- Mark as missing and exclude for time-sensitive signals (daily card data, hourly web traffic)
- Never backward-fill (fills forward from future data — creates look-ahead bias)

**2. Remove outliers:**
- Winsorize at 1st and 99th percentile (clip extreme values without dropping the observation)
- Flag and investigate anomalies — could be data error OR genuine extreme event

**3. Normalize for company/sector size:**
- Raw signal: "Store ABC had 10,000 visitors this week"
- Normalized signal: "Store ABC's visitors are +15% vs. 4-week average" — removes level effects
- Further normalize: Z-score relative to sector peers

**4. Adjust for reporting lag:**
- If company data is published 30 days after month-end, ensure the backtest only uses data available at each point in time (point-in-time data)
- Failing to account for reporting lags is a common source of "phantom alpha" in backtests

### Information Coefficient (IC)

**IC:** The correlation between the signal and future returns.
```
IC = Pearson correlation(Signal_t, Return_{t+h})

Where h = the predictive horizon (1 day, 1 week, 1 month)
```

**IC interpretation:**
- IC = 0.05 → 5% correlation with future returns → considered a good signal for quant trading
- IC = 0.10 → strong signal
- IC = 0.15+ → exceptional signal (usually decays quickly as others find it)

**ICIR (IC Information Ratio):**
```
ICIR = Mean(IC) / Std(IC)
```

ICIR measures the consistency of the signal. A signal with high average IC but high IC volatility (predictive sometimes, wrong other times) is less useful than a stable, lower IC signal.

## 4. Legal and Compliance Risks of Alternative Data

### Material Non-Public Information (MNPI)

**MNPI risk:** If alternative data provides access to information about a company's financial results before that company releases it publicly, using that data to trade may constitute insider trading under securities law.

**Key test:** Is the information:
1. Material (would a reasonable investor find it important)?
2. Non-public (not yet disclosed by the company)?

**Gray areas:**
- **Satellite imagery of a retailer's parking lots:** Counting cars before earnings → material? Courts have not definitively resolved this, but U.S. SEC has investigated.
- **Aggregated credit card data:** If the aggregator tracks 5% of a retailer's transactions, is this company-specific MNPI? Depends on specificity and whether the company itself disclosed the information.
- **App scraping:** If a company's app reveals unannounced price changes, is this MNPI?

**Best practice:** Maintain a formal **Alternative Data Policy** with:
- Pre-approval process for new data sources reviewed by compliance
- MNPI screening for each new dataset
- Legal opinions for ambiguous data sources
- Employee trading restrictions during data reception periods

### Data Privacy Regulations

**GDPR (EU):** Individual-level data about EU residents requires consent. Anonymized aggregate data (e.g., foot traffic counts without PII) is generally permissible.

**PDPA (Pakistan Personal Data Protection Act 2023):** Pakistan enacted data protection law in 2023. Similar GDPR-style consent and data minimization requirements. Relevant for any Pakistan-sourced consumer data.

**Terms of Service violations:** Web scraping that violates a website's ToS may create legal risk under computer fraud laws (CFAA in the US). Consult legal before large-scale scraping operations.

### PSX-Applicable Alternative Data

**1. Karachi Port trust vessel data:**
- Pakistan's imports/exports move through KICT (Karachi International Container Terminal) and Port Qasim
- AIS vessel tracking data shows vessel arrivals/departures
- Signals: import volumes → retail/manufacturing activity → macro consumption data

**2. Pakistan Bureau of Statistics (PBS) web scraping:**
- PBS releases monthly CPI, trade, production data
- Release calendar is irregular → monitor PBS website for early release
- Signal: anticipate market reaction to inflation data before it moves FX/rates

**3. SECP filings:**
- PSX-listed company filings: quarterly accounts, dividends, related-party transactions
- Material information system (company announcements on PSX website)
- NLP on company announcements for earnings surprise signals

**4. Pakistan oil import data:**
- PSO, PARCO monthly import volumes (published by OCAC — Oil Companies Advisory Council)
- Signal: fuel import volumes → economic activity proxy → KSE-100 energy sector

**5. Pakistan Telecom Authority (PTA) data:**
- Cellular subscriber growth → telecom sector revenue proxy
- PTCL, Jazz, Telenor, Zong subscriber metrics → sector equity signals

**6. Agriculture sector data:**
- MNFSR (Ministry of National Food Security) crop assessment reports
- Kharif/Rabi crop sowing data → fertilizer demand → Engro/FFC earnings prediction
- Pakistan Cotton Ginners Association (PCGA) cotton arrivals data → textile sector proxy

**7. UAE/Gulf alternative data for Pakistani Diaspora:**
- UAE real estate transactions (Dubai Land Department) → sentiment indicator for Pakistani diaspora capital repatriation
- Gulf employment data → impact on Pakistan remittances → PKR/USD exchange rate signal

### Pakistan NLP Opportunities

**Urdu financial NLP:** Most Pakistan financial news is in English (Dawn, The News, ARY Business) but some commentary and social media is in Urdu. Urdu NLP for financial sentiment is a relatively undeveloped area — potential alpha.

**PSX bulletin board and investor forums:** Pakistan has active online investor communities (Pakstocks.com, Investa.pk) — sentiment and discussion volume can be signals for small-cap PSX stocks.

## 6. Signal Backtesting: Avoiding Look-Ahead Bias

### The Deadly Sins of Backtesting

**1. Look-ahead bias:** Using data in the backtest that wasn't available at the decision point.
- Example: Using full-year 2022 earnings to construct a signal "as of" January 2022
- Fix: Always use point-in-time databases; track the actual release date of each data point

**2. Survivorship bias:** Only including companies still alive in the current universe.
- Example: Backtesting on PSX-50 companies but excluding companies that were delisted during the test period
- Fix: Use a historical universe that includes all companies that existed at each point in time, including delistings

**3. Overfitting:** Tuning signal parameters to fit historical data.
- Example: Testing 100 parameter combinations and picking the best — it fits history but won't work out-of-sample
- Fix: Use walk-forward testing (train on first 60%, validate on next 20%, test on final 20% — never look at the test set during development)

**4. Transaction cost neglect:** Ignoring bid-ask spreads and market impact.
- Alternative data signals often suggest high-turnover portfolios
- In illiquid markets like PSX, transaction costs can eliminate most paper alpha
- Fix: Model realistic round-trip costs (PSX average bid-ask is 0.3–0.8% for mid/small-caps)

**5. Regime dependence:** Signal that works only in one macro regime.
- Example: Satellite oil inventory data worked well from 2015–2019 when oil markets were well-supplied; less predictive during OPEC+ managed supply from 2020+
- Fix: Test signal across multiple macro regimes; require consistent IC across regimes

### Walk-Forward Backtest Framework

```
Training period: First 60% of data (signal development and parameter tuning)
Validation period: Next 20% (out-of-sample validation, final parameter selection)
Test period: Last 20% (untouched holdout — final honest performance evaluation)

After production: Monthly re-calibration with expanding training window
```

**IC decay analysis:**
Plot IC as a function of time horizon (1 day, 1 week, 2 weeks, 1 month, 3 months). If IC decays rapidly, the signal is a short-term predictor (requires frequent rebalancing, high turnover costs). If IC is persistent, it's a longer-term factor.

1. FERROQUANT is evaluating two alternative data vendors:
   
   **Vendor A:** Aggregated credit card transactions for 200 PSX-listed companies. Coverage: 80% of KSE-100 by market cap. Monthly data, 25-day lag after month-end. Cost: $80,000/year. Historical IC (1-month forward return): 0.06, ICIR: 0.8.
   
   **Vendor B:** Satellite imagery of oil tanker arrivals at Karachi port (AIS signal). Daily updates, 1-day lag. Coverage: all crude oil imports into Pakistan (indirect signal). Cost: $45,000/year. Historical IC (1-week return of PSO, OGDCL, PPL): 0.09, ICIR: 0.5.
   
   (a) Calculate the Sharpe-equivalent "signal efficiency" for each vendor: use ICIR as the proxy. Which vendor has a more consistent signal?
   (b) Evaluate each vendor on the five due diligence dimensions. Which two dimensions are most important for FERROQUANT's specific situation in Pakistan?
   (c) Vendor A's data has a 25-day lag. For a fund rebalancing monthly, explain why this lag is actually acceptable — and under what condition would it become a fatal flaw?
   (d) FERROQUANT's compliance officer raises a concern about Vendor A: "Are we accessing MNPI about companies' revenues?" How would you analyze this concern and what safeguards would you put in place?

2. FERROQUANT wants to build a satellite imagery signal for Pakistan's cotton industry:
   
   - Data: Monthly satellite imagery from Planet Labs covering cotton-growing areas in Punjab/Sindh (NDVI crop health index)
   - Target: Predict Pakistan Cotton Ginners Association (PCGA) monthly arrivals data, published with 2-week lag
   - Investment implication: Cotton arrivals signal → textile industry earnings → NML, Nishat Mills, Interloop stock returns
   
   (a) Describe the full data pipeline: from raw NDVI index to a tradeable signal for NML's stock. Include at least 4 steps and identify the potential failure point at each step.
