# Module 127 — TMT & FinTech Advisory

Technology, Media & Telecom (TMT) advisory is the fastest-evolving area of investment banking because the underlying businesses change faster than any other sector. Advisors must understand not just finance but also product, technology infrastructure, network effects, and regulatory positioning. FinTech advisory — where technology meets financial services — combines TMT knowledge with FIG expertise. This module covers the unique aspects of TMT and FinTech advisory.

## Learning Objectives

- Apply revenue-based and ARR-based valuation for technology companies
- Understand the Rule of 40 and SaaS metrics that drive TMT M&A pricing
- Identify FinTech advisory deal types (digital banking M&A, payment company acquisitions, licensing)
- Analyze Pakistan's tech ecosystem and key M&A transactions
- Understand Gulf tech consolidation dynamics and Vision 2030 digital transformation

## 1. TMT M&A Valuation

### Software and SaaS Companies

SaaS (Software-as-a-Service) companies are valued on revenue multiples rather than EBITDA multiples, because:
- High-growth SaaS companies intentionally spend aggressively → low or negative EBITDA
- Recurring revenue (ARR — Annual Recurring Revenue) is more valuable and predictable than one-time revenue
- The "land and expand" model means high customer acquisition costs today generate recurring revenue for years

**Key SaaS metrics:**

| Metric | Formula | Benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| ARR growth | (ARR_t / ARR_{t-1}) - 1 | 30%+ = strong; 50%+ = excellent |
| NRR (Net Revenue Retention) | (Beginning ARR + expansion - churn) / Beginning ARR | 110%+ = very strong (customers expand spend) |
| Gross margin | (ARR - COGS) / ARR | 70%+ for software |
| CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) | Sales + Marketing spend / New customers added | Compare to LTV |
| LTV:CAC ratio | LTV / CAC | 3x+ = healthy unit economics |
| Rule of 40 | Revenue growth % + EBITDA margin % | 40%+ = healthy |

**EV/ARR multiples:**
- Rule of 40 score 40–60%: 5–8x ARR
- Rule of 40 score 60%+: 8–15x ARR
- Exceptional growth (50%+ revenue growth) + high NRR: 15–25x ARR

### Internet and Marketplace Companies

Marketplace businesses (e.g., ride-hailing, e-commerce, food delivery) are valued on:
- GMV (Gross Merchandise Value): total transaction value through the platform
- Take rate: % of GMV retained as revenue
- EV/Revenue: for businesses with high growth but low margins

## 2. FinTech Advisory

### FinTech Deal Types

**Digital banking licenses:**
In Pakistan, SBP issued 5 digital banking licenses in 2023 (to Easypaisa/Telenor-MCB, Nayapay, Raqami, Hugo Save, KT Bank). These licenses are themselves valuable assets — regulatory licensing as deal driver.

**Payment company acquisitions:**
Payment companies (Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, Adyen) have been among the highest-multiple M&A targets globally. Buyers pay for: payment rails, merchant relationships, and regulatory approvals.

**Embedded finance acquisitions:**
Non-financial companies acquiring fintech capabilities:
- Telecom acquiring digital wallet (Jazz + Cashwire = JazzCash)
- E-commerce acquiring BNPL (Buy Now Pay Later)

**Banking-FinTech partnerships/acquisitions:**
Traditional banks acquiring FinTechs for technology capabilities:
- MCB Bank partnership with Telenor Pakistan (Easypaisa)
- HBL investment in digital payments infrastructure

### FinTech Valuation Challenges

FinTech companies combine:
- Technology company growth dynamics (revenue multiples)
- Financial company regulatory constraints (capital adequacy, licensing)
- Network effect moats (payments platforms are winner-take-all or duopoly)

**A digital wallet with 10M users is valued more like a tech company than a bank** — the user base and engagement metrics matter as much as financials.

## 3. Pakistan Tech Ecosystem

### Key Players and Transactions

**Careem (2019) — Pakistan's most famous tech exit:**
- Dubai-based ride-hailing super-app with major Pakistan operations
- Acquired by Uber for $3.1B in 2019
- Pakistani engineers and early employees benefited significantly
- Transaction: Uber bought the entire Careem entity (including Pakistan)

**Airlift (2022 shutdown):**
- Pakistan's logistics/quick commerce startup; raised $85M Series B in 2021
- Shut down in 2022 due to funding environment and unit economics
- Important lesson: high burn rate + rising interest rates + remote market = risk

**Bazaar Technologies:**
- B2B commerce platform for Pakistani retailers
- Raised Series A/B funding from international VCs
- Potential M&A target as the market consolidates

**Systems Limited:**
- PSX-listed IT services company; Pakistan's largest listed tech company
- Provides enterprise software to Gulf, Middle East, North America
- At PKR 600+ market cap, a rare large-cap tech stock on PSX

**Pakistan digital banking licenses (2023):**
5 digital banking licenses issued — Easypaisa, Nayapay, Raqami, Hugo Save, KT Bank (a Korean fintech). These licensees will require capital, technology partners, and potentially M&A to scale.

## 4. Gulf Tech Consolidation

### Vision 2030 Digital Transformation

Saudi Vision 2030 has set ambitious digital transformation targets:
- 70% digital payments penetration (from near-zero cash economy)
- Saudi National Digital Infrastructure (SNDI)
- Support for tech startups through PIF's Sanabil Investments

**Key Gulf tech deals:**
- **Careem's Everyday App:** Post-Uber acquisition, Careem relaunched as a super-app in UAE/Pakistan
- **Noon.com:** Amazon equivalent in Gulf; backed by Mohamed Alabbar (Emaar founder)
- **SOUQ (acquired by Amazon 2017):** $650M — Gulf's largest tech M&A at the time
- **Jahez (Tadawul-listed food delivery):** First Gulf tech unicorn to IPO in Saudi Arabia
- **Tamara (Saudi BNPL):** $340M Series C — Saudi Arabia's largest fintech funding round

### Gulf FinTech Advisory

DIFC Innovation Hub and Abu Dhabi Global Market (ADGM) are the primary fintech regulatory sandboxes in the Gulf. Both offer:
- Regulatory sandbox for testing fintech products
- Pathways to full financial service licenses
- Proximity to Gulf family offices and SWF co-investors

## Self-Assessment

1. A Pakistani HR software company has PKR 450M ARR, 35% revenue growth, −5% EBITDA margin, and 120% NRR. Rule of 40 score: 30%. A Gulf strategic buyer offers 5x ARR.
   (a) Is the Rule of 40 score strong enough to justify 5x ARR? What would a higher Rule of 40 score (50%+) typically command?
   (b) NRR of 120% means customers are expanding their spending. Why is this more valuable than 100% NRR (zero churn, no expansion)? How does this affect the ARR multiple?
   (c) The buyer wants an earn-out: 2x ARR upfront (PKR 900M), and an additional 3x ARR (PKR 1.35B) if ARR exceeds PKR 700M in 2 years. Evaluate this structure from the seller's perspective.
   (d) The company has SBP's digital payment subsidiary approval as part of its product. The buyer is a non-financial Gulf company. What regulatory hurdles must they navigate to acquire a company with a Pakistani financial regulatory license?

2. ADNOC is planning to spin out its digital data analytics subsidiary (built to optimize oil production) as a standalone B2B SaaS company. The subsidiary has $120M ARR, 40% growth, 75% gross margins, and a Rule of 40 score of 55%.
   (a) Value this subsidiary using EV/ARR methodology. What is the implied valuation range?
   (b) Why would ADNOC spin this out rather than keep it inside ADNOC? What strategic rationale drives the separation?
   (c) The subsidiary has contracts with ADNOC worth $80M of its $120M ARR — 67% customer concentration. How does this affect the valuation and what would a buyer require to mitigate this risk?
   (d) If the subsidiary lists on Nasdaq (US) rather than ADX (UAE), what are the regulatory and market access considerations?
