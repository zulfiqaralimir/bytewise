# Module 124 — Sovereign & Government Advisory

Sovereign and government advisory is one of the most prestigious and complex areas of investment banking. Governments are uniquely powerful clients — they set the rules, they have sovereign immunity, and the transactions they undertake can be transformational for entire economies. This module covers the main transaction types: privatizations, sovereign debt issuance, PPP (Public-Private Partnership) financing, and SWF advisory.

## Learning Objectives

- Advise on government privatization transactions (Pakistan Privatization Commission process)
- Structure sovereign debt issuance (Eurobonds, sukuk) and navigate investor relations
- Design and execute PPP financing for infrastructure projects
- Understand Sovereign Wealth Fund (SWF) advisory and portfolio management
- Apply sovereign advisory knowledge to Pakistan government transactions and Gulf SWFs

---

## 1. Privatization Advisory

### The Privatization Rationale

Governments privatize state-owned enterprises (SOEs) to:
- Raise capital for budget support or debt reduction
- Improve SOE efficiency (private management vs. bureaucratic management)
- Reduce fiscal risk (SOE losses are on the government's balance sheet)
- Develop capital markets (creating new listed companies for investors)
- Political reform (reducing state footprint in the economy)

**The IB advisor's role in privatization:**
- Conduct strategic review: is the SOE ready for privatization?
- Prepare the company for sale (financial reporting, corporate governance, management team)
- Structure the sale (full privatization, partial IPO, strategic sale, management buyout)
- Value the business (football field, fairness opinion)
- Run the transaction (auction, IPO, or bilateral sale)

### Pakistan Privatization Commission

The Privatization Commission of Pakistan (PC) is the government body responsible for managing Pakistan's privatization program. Key features:

**Mandate:** Privatize selected SOEs to improve efficiency and raise revenue.

**Notable past privatizations:**
- Muslim Commercial Bank (MCB): privatized in 1991 — often cited as Pakistan's most successful privatization
- United Bank Limited (UBL): privatized in 2002 to Abu Dhabi Group
- Habib Bank Limited (HBL): partial privatization (51% sold to Aga Khan Fund for Economic Development in 2004)
- Karachi Electric Supply Corporation (KESC): privatized in 2005 (now K-Electric)
- Pakistan Telecommunications Company Limited (PTCL): 26% sold to Etisalat in 2006

**Pending/stalled privatizations:**
- Pakistan Steel Mills: largest loss-making SOE; multiple privatization attempts failed due to worker resistance
- State Life Insurance Corporation: strategic sale repeatedly postponed
- Pakistan International Airlines (PIA): privatization plans perennially delayed
- DISCOs (Distribution Companies): power sector privatization linked to circular debt resolution

**PC process for a strategic sale:**
1. Cabinet approval to privatize
2. Financial advisor appointed (IB firm)
3. Pre-privatization restructuring (resolve liabilities, clean up accounts)
4. Transaction structure recommendation
5. Expression of Interest from buyers
6. Request for Proposals → binding bids
7. Negotiation and signing
8. Cabinet/CCOP (Cabinet Committee on Privatization) final approval
9. Closing

## 2. Sovereign Debt Advisory

### Eurobond Issuance

Pakistan has been an active Eurobond issuer:

**Recent Pakistan Eurobond issuances:**
- 2014: $2B (5-year, 7.25%)
- 2015: $1B (10-year, 8.25%)
- 2017: $2.5B (multi-tranche: 5-year, 10-year, 30-year)
- 2019: $2.5B (multi-tranche)
- 2021: $2.5B (sukuk + Eurobond combination)

**Eurobond issuance process:**
1. Mandate banks (typically 2–3 bookrunners: HSBC, Standard Chartered, Citi + regional bank)
2. Investor roadshow (London, New York, UAE, Singapore): ~5 days
3. Announce pricing and bookbuild overnight
4. Price and close the book
5. Settlement in 3–5 business days

**IB advisory role:** Advise Finance Ministry on timing, structure (maturity, format — conventional vs. sukuk), pricing expectations, investor targeting, and market conditions.

### Sovereign Debt Restructuring

When a sovereign cannot service its debt, it restructures:
- **Paris Club:** Official bilateral creditors (governments) negotiate restructuring collectively
- **London Club:** Commercial bank creditors
- **Bondholder restructuring:** Requires majority action via CACs (Collective Action Clauses) in the bonds

**Pakistan's IMF programs:** Pakistan has entered multiple IMF programs (most recently 2019, 2023) which involve debt sustainability analysis and sometimes rescheduling of bilateral debt.

## 3. PPP Financial Advisory

A Public-Private Partnership (PPP) brings private sector capital and expertise into infrastructure projects traditionally funded by government.

**IB advisor role in PPP:**
- **Project structuring:** Design the concession agreement, risk allocation between public and private sectors
- **Financial modelling:** Build the project finance model (Module 126)
- **Bankability assessment:** Is the project structure acceptable to debt providers?
- **Procurement process:** Competitive tender to select the private partner

**Pakistan PPP context:**
- National Highway Authority (NHA) toll road PPPs
- CPEC projects (bilateral deals with China, less competitive tender)
- Lahore-Islamabad Motorway (M2): one of Pakistan's early PPP successes
- Orange Line Metro (Lahore): government-funded; not true PPP

## 4. SWF Advisory

Sovereign Wealth Funds (SWFs) are government-owned investment vehicles managing national savings. The Gulf SWFs are the world's largest:

| SWF | Country | AUM (approx.) |
|---|---|---|
| Abu Dhabi Investment Authority (ADIA) | UAE | $850B |
| Saudi Arabia PIF | Saudi Arabia | $700B |
| Kuwait Investment Authority (KIA) | Kuwait | $750B |
| Qatar Investment Authority (QIA) | Qatar | $450B |
| Mubadala Investment Company | UAE | $280B |

**IB advisory to SWFs:**
- Co-investment advisory: banks pitch deals to SWFs as co-investors
- Portfolio construction: asset allocation advice
- Direct investment sourcing: find deals for SWFs to invest in
- Strategic advisory: restructuring or disposal of portfolio assets

**Pakistan SWF:**
Pakistan does not have a well-capitalized SWF. The Pakistan Sovereign Wealth Fund Act was passed in 2023 establishing the Pakistan Sovereign Wealth Fund (SWF), but it is nascent.

## Self-Assessment

1. Pakistan's Privatization Commission has appointed you as financial advisor for the strategic sale of a 51% stake in State Life Insurance Corporation (Pakistan's largest insurer, PKR 450B assets, government-owned):
   (a) Identify the five most important pre-privatization preparations needed before approaching buyers. What specifically makes State Life difficult to privatize?
   (b) Who are the likely buyer categories? For each category, describe one specific attraction and one specific concern about acquiring State Life.
   (c) What valuation methodology is appropriate for an insurance company? Why are EBITDA-based methods not used?
   (d) Pakistan SECP requires an insurance company change-of-control approval. How does this regulatory condition affect the deal timeline, and what conditions might SECP attach to its approval?

2. ADIA (Abu Dhabi Investment Authority) is considering a $500M direct investment in a Pakistani infrastructure asset (a toll motorway concession). As the IB advisor to ADIA: (a) What due diligence workstreams are essential for a Pakistan infrastructure investment that might not be needed for a UAE domestic deal? (b) How would you structure the investment to protect ADIA's capital repatriation rights given Pakistan's historical foreign exchange restrictions? (c) The toll revenues are PKR-denominated. ADIA reports in USD. Describe the currency risk and potential hedging strategies. (d) What political risk insurance products exist (MIGA, OPIC/DFC) and how would you incorporate them into the investment structure?
