# Module 117 — Sukuk: Islamic Bond Market

Sukuk (plural of sakk, meaning certificate) are Shariah-compliant financial certificates that represent ownership in tangible assets, usufruct, or a project — rather than a debt obligation. The prohibition on riba (interest) in Islamic finance means that conventional bonds (which pay predetermined interest) are not permissible. Sukuk are structured to provide economic returns equivalent to bonds but through profit-sharing, leasing, or asset ownership mechanisms. The global sukuk market is approximately $750B outstanding and growing — with Pakistan and the Gulf being two of the most important markets.

## Learning Objectives

- Explain why sukuk are structured differently from bonds (riba prohibition)
- Understand the four main sukuk structures: Ijarah, Murabaha, Musharaka, Wakala
- Analyze Pakistan's GoP Ijarah Sukuk and corporate sukuk market
- Apply Gulf sukuk market knowledge (Saudi Aramco, Dubai, Malaysia)
- Price sukuk relative to conventional bonds (spread and premium/discount analysis)

## 1. Sukuk vs. Conventional Bond

### The Riba Prohibition

**Riba** means an increase or excess — specifically, any predetermined return on money. In Islamic finance, money has no inherent time value; returns must come from real economic activity.

**Conventional bond (prohibited):**
- Issuer borrows money
- Pays predetermined interest (riba) over time
- Repays principal at maturity
- Investors own a debt claim; there is no underlying asset

**Sukuk (permissible):**
- Investors own an interest in an underlying asset, project, or business activity
- Returns come from the income generated by that asset (rent, profit share)
- At maturity, the asset is sold back or the certificate is redeemed at face value
- The economic outcome approximates a bond — but the legal structure is asset-based

**AAOIFI Shariah Standard 17:** Defines permissible sukuk structures. Sukuk must represent ownership in assets; they cannot simply be debt instruments with Islamic labels.

## 2. Main Sukuk Structures

### Ijarah Sukuk (Lease-Based)

**Most common structure globally. Pakistan's GoP sukuk is Ijarah-based.**

**Mechanics:**
1. The obligor (e.g., government of Pakistan) owns an asset (land, building, infrastructure)
2. The obligor sells the asset to a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV)
3. The SPV issues sukuk certificates to investors; proceeds fund the asset purchase
4. The SPV leases the asset back to the obligor (sale-and-leaseback)
5. The obligor pays rent to the SPV; SPV distributes rent to sukuk holders as "profit"
6. At maturity, the obligor repurchases the asset from the SPV at the original price; SPV uses proceeds to redeem sukuk

**Why it works:** Investors own a share of the SPV (which owns the asset). Their return is rent (not interest). The asset is the legal foundation of the instrument.

**Pakistan GoP Ijarah Sukuk:**
Pakistan's government issues domestic PKR-denominated Ijarah Sukuk backed by government-owned properties (roads, buildings). These are used by Islamic banks and fund managers who cannot hold conventional T-bills/PIBs.

**Pricing:** GoP Ijarah Sukuk are priced to yield approximately the same as conventional PIBs of similar maturity — but the mechanism is rental income rather than interest.

### Murabaha Sukuk (Cost-Plus Sale)

**Structure:**
1. SPV buys a commodity on behalf of the obligor
2. SPV sells the commodity to the obligor at cost plus a predetermined profit margin, on deferred payment terms
3. Sukuk holders own a share of the receivable from the obligor

**Limitation:** AAOIFI restricts Murabaha sukuk from being tradeable in the secondary market (since trading a debt receivable at a discount is not permissible). Therefore, Murabaha sukuk are largely held to maturity by Islamic banks.

### Musharaka Sukuk (Partnership)

**Structure:**
Investors and the obligor form a partnership (musharaka) to own and manage an asset or project. Sukuk represent proportional ownership in the partnership. Profit and loss are shared according to agreed ratios.

**Use case:** Infrastructure projects, real estate development. Returns depend on actual project performance (closer to equity economics).

### Wakala Sukuk (Agency)

**Structure:**
1. SPV pools investor funds
2. Appoints the obligor as wakeel (agent) to invest the funds in a portfolio of Shariah-compliant assets
3. Returns distributed to investors as the portfolio generates income
4. Commonly used by sovereigns and banks for flexible balance sheet management

**Gulf preference:** Wakala sukuk are popular in the UAE and GCC because they allow the issuer to use proceeds flexibly (no specific asset must be identified upfront).

## 3. Pakistan Sukuk Market

### GoP Ijarah Sukuk

Pakistan's government has been issuing domestic Ijarah Sukuk since 2008. These are:
- PKR-denominated
- Backed by government-owned real estate and infrastructure
- Issued via SBP's OMO/auction process
- Benchmark for Islamic banking institutions (alternative to PIBs)
- Profit rate linked to PIB yields of comparable maturity

**Islamic banking system need:** Pakistan's Islamic banking sector (~30% of banking system) cannot hold conventional interest-bearing PIBs. GoP Ijarah Sukuk are the primary government securities for Islamic banks.

### Pakistan Corporate Sukuk

Several Pakistani companies have issued sukuk under SECP's TFC / Sukuk regulatory framework:
- Meezan Bank Tier 2 Sukuk
- K-Electric sukuk
- WAPDA Sukuk (backed by hydropower assets)
- Dubai Islamic Bank Pakistan Sukuk

**Pakistan Eurobond sukuk:**
Pakistan has issued sovereign sukuk in the international market:
- 2005: Pakistan's first international sukuk ($600M Ijarah)
- 2014, 2016, 2017, 2019, 2022: Multiple international Ijarah sukuk ($1B each, typically)
- These are USD-denominated, internationally listed, and rated by S&P/Moody's

## 4. Gulf Sukuk Market

### Saudi Arabia

Saudi Arabia is the world's largest sukuk issuer:
- Saudi Aramco sukuk: Multiple issuances (2019: $8B conventional + $4B sukuk)
- Saudi government: Regular domestic and international sukuk issuances
- Vision 2030 infrastructure projects being financed partly through sukuk
- Tadawul has an active listed sukuk market

### UAE / Dubai

Dubai has positioned itself as the global hub for Islamic finance:
- DIFC: home to major Islamic finance institutions
- Nasdaq Dubai: listed sukuk exchange (many Pakistani and Gulf sukuk are listed here for international investors)
- Dubai government sukuk: Regular $1–2B issuances
- Real estate-backed sukuk: Emaar, DAMAC, Nakheel (post-restructuring)

### Pricing Sukuk vs. Bonds

Sukuk typically price at a slight premium (higher yield) vs. comparable conventional bonds because:
- Smaller investor universe (only Shariah-compliant investors can hold)
- Less liquid secondary market
- More complex legal structure increases execution risk

**However**, for issuers with Muslim-majority investor bases (Pakistan, GCC), a sukuk structure often achieves comparable pricing to conventional bonds due to the large captive Islamic investor base.
