# Module 121 — Restructuring Advisory Process

Restructuring advisory is investment banking on the other side of a crisis. When a company cannot service its debt, is facing insolvency, or needs to fundamentally reset its capital structure, restructuring advisors are brought in to navigate the process — either advising the distressed company itself (debtor-side advisory) or the creditors seeking to maximize their recovery (creditor-side advisory). This module covers the restructuring advisory process, valuation in distress, and the Pakistan/Gulf legal frameworks.

## Learning Objectives

- Explain debtor-side vs. creditor-side restructuring advisory
- Value a distressed business (liquidation value, going concern value, enterprise value)
- Understand the Restructuring Support Agreement (RSA) and its role
- Navigate Pakistan Companies Act 2017 scheme of arrangement
- Analyze Gulf restructuring case studies (Abraaj, NMC, DAMAC, Arcapita)

---

## 1. The Restructuring Advisory Universe

### Debtor-Side (Company) Advisors

The distressed company retains:
- **Financial advisor (restructuring IB):** Lazard, Houlihan Lokey, Rothschild (global); local firms in Pakistan/Gulf
- **Legal counsel:** Restructuring lawyers (Freshfields, Linklaters; locally Allen & Overy, Haidermota in Pakistan)
- **Management consultants / CRO:** Chief Restructuring Officer brought in for operational improvement

**Debtor advisor's mandate:**
- Stabilize the company (cash management, supplier payments, employee retention)
- Value the business on a going concern and liquidation basis
- Develop a restructuring plan
- Negotiate with creditors
- Manage communication with employees, customers, suppliers, regulators

### Creditor-Side Advisors

Each creditor class may hire its own advisor:
- **Bank syndicate:** May appoint a coordinating committee with a single financial advisor (Rothschild, Houlihan, KPMG Restructuring)
- **Bondholders:** Ad hoc committee hires a financial advisor and legal counsel
- **Shareholders:** May hire their own advisor if they believe there is equity value to protect

## 2. Valuation in Distress

### Three Valuation Scenarios

**Going concern value:**
The business is worth X if it continues operating. This uses standard DCF/comps methodology — but requires adjusting assumptions for the distressed environment:
- Revenue projections: may need to be reduced (customer loss, contract cancellations)
- WACC: distressed WACC is higher (20–30%+ for highly distressed companies)
- Cost of restructuring: one-time costs must be deducted

**Liquidation value:**
What do the assets fetch if the company is wound up and assets sold piecemeal? Usually significantly below going concern value:
- Forced sale discount: 20–50% discount to book value for physical assets
- Inventory: often sold at below cost
- Receivables: discounted for collectability
- Goodwill/intangibles: typically zero in liquidation

**Enterprise value (distributable):**
Once the EV is established (via DCF or comparable distressed transaction analysis), the recoveries for each creditor class are calculated:

```
Enterprise Value
− Senior secured debt (first priority)
= Recovery for senior unsecured
− Senior unsecured debt
= Recovery for junior/subordinated creditors
− Junior creditors
= Recovery for equity (often zero in severe distress)
```

**The "fulcrum security":** The class of debt that is at the money — gets partial but not full recovery. This class has the most negotiating power in a restructuring because they effectively own the company.

## 3. Restructuring Support Agreement (RSA)

An RSA is a binding agreement between the distressed company and key creditors, signed before the formal restructuring plan is implemented. It locks in creditor support for the restructuring.

**Why RSAs matter:**
- Creates certainty before the restructuring goes live
- Prevents creditors from defecting once the process starts
- Signals to suppliers, employees, and customers that the restructuring will succeed

**RSA key provisions:**
- The agreed restructuring terms (new debt terms, equity conversion, haircut)
- Standstill on enforcement: creditors agree not to accelerate or sue during the process
- Support obligations: signatories commit to vote for the plan
- Fiduciary out: allows a signatory to change position only if their board requires (fiduciary duty)

## 4. Pakistan Restructuring Framework

### Companies Act 2017 — Scheme of Arrangement (Sections 279–289)

**Process:**
1. Company or creditors apply to the High Court (typically Sindh or Lahore High Court)
2. Court grants permission to convene scheme meetings (shareholder and/or creditor meetings)
3. Scheme document (equivalent to a restructuring plan) is distributed
4. Voting: 75% by value of creditors in each class must approve
5. Court sanctions the approved scheme
6. Scheme becomes binding on all creditors (including dissenters in classes that voted yes)

**Practical timeline:** 6–12 months for a fully contested scheme; faster if creditors are cooperative.

**Limitations:**
- Pakistan courts are slow; High Court judges handling commercial matters often lack specialized insolvency expertise
- Limited "cramdown" ability vs. US Chapter 11
- No automatic stay on enforcement (creditors can still sue and attach assets while scheme is pending)
- Cultural preference for bilateral negotiation over formal restructuring processes

### State Bank of Pakistan (SBP) Role

SBP has developed frameworks for banking sector restructuring:
- Non-Performing Loan (NPL) resolution guidelines
- Debt restructuring: banks can restructure loans without provisioning hit if "sustainable restructuring" criteria met
- SBP approval required for bank amalgamations and significant capital actions

## 5. Gulf Restructuring Case Studies

### Abraaj Group (2018–2019)

The world's largest EM-focused PE firm, based in Dubai, collapsed after allegations of misappropriation of investor funds:
- $1B+ of LP capital allegedly misused
- DFSA (Dubai Financial Services Authority) investigation
- Abraaj went into provisional liquidation (Cayman Islands)
- Key lessons: insufficient governance of the GP entity; commingling of fund capital

### NMC Health (2020)

UAE-based hospital group (listed on LSE) with $6.6B hidden debt:
- Administration ordered by UK courts (as a UK-listed entity)
- Joint administrators from Alvarez & Marsal managed the process
- Eventually restructured; banks converted significant debt to equity
- Listed on ADX after restructuring

### DAMAC Properties (2023)

Dubai luxury developer conducted a comprehensive liability management exercise:
- Exchange offer and tender offer for its outstanding bonds
- Reduced debt burden ahead of refinancing maturities
- Not a formal insolvency — proactive liability management

(a) Identify the "fulcrum security" in this capital structure. (b) Design a restructuring plan that involves haircut, maturity extension, and partial equity conversion. What are the three biggest obstacles to creditor agreement? (c) Which Pakistan Companies Act 2017 provision would you use to bind the dissenting banks? What vote threshold is required? (d) An operational hedge fund has bought PKR 3B of the bank debt at 65 cents on the dollar. They are demanding full par recovery. How does their presence change the dynamics?

Evaluate their strategy — is it rational?
