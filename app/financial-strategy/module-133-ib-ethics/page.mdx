# Module 133 — Ethics & Conflicts of Interest in IB

Investment banking operates at the center of financial markets — and at the center of enormous potential conflicts of interest. IB professionals have access to material non-public information (MNPI), earn fees contingent on transactions closing, and advise multiple parties who may have conflicting interests. Managing these conflicts — and understanding where the ethical and legal lines are drawn — is not just a compliance exercise: it is a fundamental professional responsibility. This module covers the main conflict and ethics issues in IB.

## Learning Objectives

- Identify and manage conflicts of interest in IB advisory mandates
- Explain Chinese walls and information barriers
- Understand insider trading law and how it applies to IB professionals
- Identify market manipulation and its regulatory consequences
- Apply SECP market abuse regulations to Pakistan capital markets
- Understand DFSA enforcement powers and the Gulf regulatory framework

---

## 1. Conflicts of Interest in IB

### The Fundamental Conflict: Fee Incentives

Investment bankers earn completion fees — fees paid only if a transaction closes. This creates structural conflicts:
- **Sell-side advisor:** Paid to maximize sale price. But is also incentivized to close the deal even at a suboptimal price (rather than advise the client to walk away).
- **Buy-side advisor:** Paid to help the acquirer close the deal. But may fail to identify problems that would justify withdrawing.
- **Fairness opinion provider:** May be advising the seller while simultaneously issuing an opinion that the deal is "fair."

**Managing completion fee conflicts:**
- Retainer fees align the advisor to provide ongoing value regardless of deal outcome
- Clear mandate letters that specify fiduciary duties
- Disclosure of fee structures to boards
- Independent fairness opinion from a separate, non-advising bank

### Dual Role Conflicts

**Advising both buyer and seller:** An IB firm should never advise both parties in the same transaction. This is an absolute conflict.

**Financing the acquirer while advising the target:** If Bank X is providing a leveraged loan to the acquirer and also issuing a fairness opinion for the target's board, there is a severe conflict. Both must be disclosed.

**Equity research and IB:** An IB firm's equity research analysts covering a company while the IB division is advising that company creates conflict — analysts may be pressured to write positive research to support the deal.

## 2. Chinese Walls (Information Barriers)

### What Chinese Walls Are

An information barrier (Chinese wall) is a set of policies and physical/technical controls that prevent confidential information held by one part of a bank from being accessed by another part that would be conflicted.

**Example:**
- IBD (Investment Banking Division) is advising Company A on a potential acquisition
- Equity research division covers Company A
- A Chinese wall prevents the equity research analysts from knowing about the planned acquisition
- Otherwise, they would have MNPI and could not publish unbiased research

**Chinese wall procedures:**
- Separate floors/offices
- Access controls on shared systems
- "Need to know" principle for deal information
- Watch lists: companies under active deal coverage; restricted lists: companies where research is restricted

### Crossing the Wall

When an IB banker "crosses the wall," they share MNPI with someone previously on the other side of the information barrier. Procedures:
- Formal process to cross the wall
- The person crossing is placed on restricted list (cannot trade in that security)
- Documented log of all wall crossings

## 3. Insider Trading

### Definition and Elements

**Insider trading:** Trading in securities while in possession of MNPI (Material Non-Public Information) that would affect the security's price.

**MNPI examples in IB:**
- Knowledge of a pending acquisition (before public announcement)
- Awareness of an IPO in preparation
- Knowledge of a rights issue being planned
- Knowledge of an imminent earnings announcement

**The four elements of insider trading (most jurisdictions):**
1. The person possessed information
2. The information was material (price-sensitive)
3. The information was non-public
4. The person traded on that information (or tipped someone who did)

**Pakistan — SECP Market Abuse Regulations:**
Section 20 of the Securities Act 2015 prohibits insider trading. Penalties: up to 5 years imprisonment and fines up to PKR 10M or 3x the profit made.

**UAE — DFSA Market Misconduct:**
The DFSA enforces market abuse rules under the DIFC Market Rules. Sanctions include: fines, licence revocation, public censure.

### Front-Running

Front-running is a specific form of market abuse where a broker with advance knowledge of a large client order trades ahead of the client (to profit from the price movement the client's order will cause).

## 4. Market Manipulation

### Types of Market Manipulation

**Price manipulation:**
- "Painting the tape": executing fictitious trades to give the appearance of active trading
- Wash trading: selling and repurchasing the same security to create artificial volume
- Pump and dump: driving up the price of a thinly traded stock with false statements, then selling at the inflated price

**Pakistan market manipulation history:**
PSX has had documented cases of organized price manipulation in small-cap stocks. SECP has taken enforcement actions including disqualifications and fines.

**Benchmark manipulation:**
LIBOR scandal (2012): Major banks manipulated interest rate benchmarks — led to $9B+ in fines globally; criminal prosecutions of traders.

## 5. Regulatory Frameworks

### Pakistan — SECP

**Market surveillance:**
SECP has a Market Surveillance function that monitors PSX trading data for suspicious patterns:
- Unusual price movements before announcements
- Concentrated trading in small-cap stocks
- Suspicious timing of trades relative to deal announcements

**Enforcement actions:**
SECP can: issue show-cause notices, impose fines, disqualify individuals from securities markets, refer cases for criminal prosecution to NAB (National Accountability Bureau) or FIA.

### Gulf — DFSA (DIFC)

The DFSA regulates financial services in the DIFC. Key enforcement powers:
- Fines (no upper limit for serious misconduct)
- Public censure
- Prohibition from conducting financial services
- Criminal referral to Dubai Police/Dubai Public Prosecution

**Notable DFSA cases:**
DFSA has taken enforcement action against several individuals for insider trading and market manipulation in DIFC-regulated entities. The DFSA's Whistleblower Program incentivizes reporting.

### Saudi Arabia — CMA

The Saudi Capital Market Authority is one of the most active enforcement regulators in the Arab world:
- Regular enforcement announcements (published on CMA website)
- Fines for insider trading, market manipulation, and disclosure violations
- Saudi market abuse is criminally prosecuted under the Capital Market Law

1. You are an IB associate at a Pakistani firm. Your deal team is advising Lucky Cement on a potential acquisition. Your cousin calls and asks: "I've heard Lucky Cement might be doing something big — should I buy shares?" (a) Identify every ethical and legal issue raised by this scenario. (b) What should you say to your cousin? (c) What should you report to your compliance officer? (d) If your cousin buys shares without you saying anything explicit — are you liable?

2. An IB firm is simultaneously: (a) advising a Pakistani company (Seller) on its sale, (b) providing equity research coverage on the Seller's shares, and (c) arranging the acquisition financing loan for the Buyer. Map all three conflicts of interest and describe the specific Chinese wall or conflict management procedure needed for each.

3. A DFSA-regulated investment bank in Dubai structured a large M&A deal. The lead banker placed personal trades in the target company's shares two weeks before the deal announcement (through his wife's account). The trades generated AED 2.5M in profit. (a) What regulatory violations occurred? (b) What defenses might the banker raise? (c) What penalties does the DFSA have the power to impose? (d) What "red flag" should the firm's compliance monitoring system have detected?
